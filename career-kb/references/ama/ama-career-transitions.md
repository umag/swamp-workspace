## Overview

This is the slide deck and talking-points transcript for an "Ask Me Anything" session on **career transitions (карьерные переходы)** hosted at Vas3k.Club on 22.04.2026. The two presenters:

- **Yulia (Юля) Belozerova** — gestalt practitioner and former project manager; "rolled out of IT" (выкатилась из ИТ) after 15 years in project management; second-year PhD student researching how psychotherapy affects careers; third year running a gestalt-approach therapy practice; 20+ posts in the Club.
- **Svetlana (Света) Moiseeva** — ICF-certified career coach and former HR specialist (came from HR in onboarding, training and employee development). Moved Berlin ⇐ St. Petersburg; left employment "into nowhere" (в никуда) with burnout and a "crisis of the 30-year-olds"; over 6 years became the go-to "how do I relocate? look at my resume" person; now helps people find work, find themselves within it, and grow.

**Purpose:** to give the audience "different optics" (разная оптика) — six distinct ways to look at changing profession — using the parable of the blind men and the elephant. The session is framed by an observation that the volume of articles and anxiety about "rolling out of IT (and not only)" had doubled.

The deck is structured: (1) Symptoms, (2) Theory + applying theory, (3) Context/starting point, (4) Problems (unavoidable vs. voluntary), (5) Outcomes, (6) Therapist vs. Coach, then "what to actually do," contacts, and Q&A.

## Key Constructs & Definitions

The deck presents a "Theory" matrix mapping six lenses, each with a layperson's framing and an example complaint:

- **Career transitions** — inter-role, intra-role transitions; the key process is **sense-making (осмысление)**. Layperson framing: "Wannapreneur problem: decided where to go but not moving"; "I MUST do something or else it's bad." Identity question: "Who am I in the new role?"
- **Career shocks** — unforeseen events that trigger a re-evaluation of one's career. Can be positive/negative, predictable/not. Examples: layoffs, promotions, emigration have predictable impact. Layperson framing: emigration.
- **Career indecision** — "the uncertainty, difficulty, or hesitation experienced when making career choices, such as selecting a company, job, or industry. It is a complex, multifactorial, and dynamic process." Driven by lack of knowledge, anxiety, personal factors. Framing: "AAAAA! So anxious that I can't think about or decide anything"; "Can't decide who I want to become and it makes me feel bad."
- **Career success (subjective & objective)** — Obstacles to success: low emotional stability, weak support, job insecurity. Framing: "Seems like things are fine at work but I'm unhappy"; "Seems like I changed to where I wanted but it's not clear if it's good for me now."
- **Career inaction** — "the failure to take sufficient action over time to realize a desired career change, such as leaving a dissatisfying job, pursuing a promotion, or starting a new business." Framing: "I sit where I sit, even though I feel bad"; "Thought and thought and stayed where I was."
- **Career adaptability** — Framing: "What am I even doing here?!". Operationalised through the **CAAS** dimensions.
- **Impostor syndrome** — self-doubt that lowers career planning, ambition, and motivation toward leadership; linked to self-perception of work and personality traits.
- **Career Adaptability (the 4 C's)** — **Concern** (забота о будущем), **Control** (контроль), **Curiosity** (исследование), **Confidence** (уверенность). These parameters influence subjective well-being.
- **Self-efficacy** — "an individual's belief in their capacity to execute career behaviors necessary to produce specific performance attainments." (Tagged a "sacred cow of academics.")
- **Subjective vs. objective career success** — people judge success via **self-referent** (comparison with oneself) and **other-referent** (comparison with others) criteria.
- **Objective career success** — "tangible, measurable, and observable achievements in a professional career that can be verified by others" (e.g., money, job title, promotions).
- **Career transition outcomes** — success of a transition = subjective satisfaction + objective indicators + adaptation. "The deeper a transition is integrated into a person's life, the better."
- **The Big Five** — Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism.

## Main Findings & Arguments

**The "elephant" framing.** Career change can be viewed through (at least) six lenses; each is "logical, intuitively clear, sometimes helps" but partial. Addressing symptoms one-by-one is tempting but insufficient — e.g., anxiety about AI → "urgently learn to eat it"; visa anxiety → "study bureaucracy! get married! move countries!"; tired of 10–20 years in one profession → "switch everything radically out of spite"; toxic job but staying for money → "mice cried but kept eating the cactus"; migrated as a "trailing" spouse and diploma no longer relevant at 40 → "go work in a shop."

**Section 3 — Context / starting point.** The deck introduces a **2×2 matrix** (see Frameworks) mapping whether a person has a decision and whether they are taking action. The "bright side of context" message: every path is unique; the key early task is to clarify *how we ended up where we are* and *where the soul actually lies (куда на самом деле лежит душа)*.

**Self-exploration ("journey to the centre of ~~the Earth~~ yourself").** Exploring yourself from multiple angles lets you build a personal strategy while clarifying value orientations and difficulties. Tools offered: **Dilts' pyramid of logical levels** (environment → behaviour → capabilities → beliefs/values → identity → mission, each with a question: "what do I have / what do I do / how do I choose / what do I believe in / who am I / what for"); **strengths coaching** via Gallup / MBTI; self-diagnostics.

**Section 4 — Problems: the unavoidable (1/2).** An iceberg image: work is often just the visible tip — the submerged part includes AI, the economy, before/after parental leave, family circumstances, "psychological problems where work is just a symptom," the "age candidate" (возрастной кандидат), politics, integration in emigration.

**Section 4 — Problems: the voluntary (2/2).** Specific transition archetypes and their core tensions:
- **Career abroad** — cross-cultural aspect, "impostor," the gap between "here" and "there."
- **"Leaving IT"** — search for meaning/calling vs. stability.
- **Breaking out of corporate** — earning from a hobby vs. simple manual labour.
- **Employment → entrepreneurship (or vice versa)** — change of responsibility, adaptability, and rest.
- **Portfolio career** — "everyone ran, so I ran" (bandwagon).
- **"I am a multipotentialite"** — feeling cramped in one role and unsure how to talk about one's experience; the "job-hopper" reputation; "a coping strategy of our time."

**Section 5 — Outcomes: solutions and new problems.** Each possible outcome carries a new downside:
- Own business → may burn out.
- Employment + own business (side hustle) → on-trend but is it actually real?
- Switching to a roughly equivalent job → will be ± the same.
- "Rolling out" / retraining → long and hard.
- Move into leadership → "you can't all just lead."
- Growing sideways (breadth) → breadth is not infinite.
- Doing nothing → "what if that's also OK..."
- Part-time employment → "a.k.a. life Dutch-style."

**Section 6 — Therapist vs. Coach.** The session contrasts the two professionals' question sets (see Frameworks). Both share the closing question: *"What does your work / profession mean to you?"*

## Frameworks & Models

**The 2×2 starting-point matrix (Section 3).**
Axes: vertical = есть решение / нет решения (has a decision / no decision); horizontal = нет действий / есть действия (no action / takes action). Four quadrants:
- **Has decision + no action** → "знаю, но не иду" (I know but I'm not going) — career-inaction territory.
- **Has decision + action** → "осознанный переход" (conscious/deliberate transition) — the desired state.
- **No decision + no action** → "стагнация" (stagnation).
- **No decision + action** → "хаотичный поиск" (chaotic search).

**Dilts' Pyramid of Logical Levels** — used as a self-exploration scaffold (environment, behaviour, capabilities, beliefs/values, identity, mission).

**The six theoretical lenses** (the "elephant") — career transitions, career shocks, career indecision, career success, career inaction, career adaptability — plus cross-cutting constructs (impostor syndrome, self-efficacy, Big Five).

**Levels of help ("what to actually do" — Practically):**
- **Lvl 1: Mentorship** — about career *development*, when everything is basically clear.
- **Lvl 2: Career coach** — about *actions* in the career, when the problem is in the work itself.
- **Lvl 3: Psychotherapist** — about *deep difficulties*, when everything is bad.
- **Lvl 4: All of the above.**

## Measurement

The deck links to real instruments for self-assessment:
- **Career Inaction Scale** — the 8-item scale is reproduced verbatim (see Notable Quotes / the CARINAS items): items 1–8 about wanting change but not pursuing it, not daring to give up what one has, feeling paralyzed/stuck, failing to take concrete action, not managing to realize career desires.
- **Career adaptability scale (CAAS)** — a worksheet is shown with the four dimensions and their items rated 1 (Not strong) to 5 (Strongest): *Concern* items (thinking about my future, realizing today's choices shape my future, preparing for the future, becoming aware of educational/vocational choices I must make, planning how to achieve my goals, concerned about my career); *Control* items (keeping upbeat, making decisions by myself, taking responsibility for my actions, sticking up for my beliefs, counting on myself, doing what's right for me); *Curiosity* items (exploring my surroundings, looking for opportunities to grow as a person, investigating options before making a choice, observing different ways of doing things, probing deeply into questions I have, becoming curious about new opportunities); *Confidence* items (performing tasks efficiently, taking care to do things well, learning new skills, working up to my ability, overcoming obstacles, solving problems).

## Practical Implications

**Psychologically:**
- Build structure into the day and life as a whole.
- Organize a support system.
- Find people in the same situation.
- Notice more of the positive.

**Practically — match the helper to the depth of the problem:** mentor when things are clear (Lvl 1), career coach when the problem is in the work (Lvl 2), psychotherapist when there are deep difficulties (Lvl 3), or all of the above (Lvl 4).

**Six hints for navigating a transition:**
1. Don't act rashly / don't cut from the shoulder (Не рубите с плеча).
2. Understand your values.
3. Choose a *path*, not a *result*.
4. Gather info about the available options.
5. Realistically assess the effort required.
6. Don't burn out.

**Coach-style questions to ask yourself** (forward/resource-oriented): "What will become possible when you are fulfilled?"; "Why is what you do so important to you — why is it important to *you* specifically?"; "When you're at the peak of your activity, which qualities/skills were working for you?"; "Which part of you is currently struggling in your career, and which part can support you?"; "What resources and additional topics matter for making this work for you?"

**Therapist-style questions** (broader life/affect-oriented): "What else is happening in your life?"; "How do you sleep, eat, rest?"; "What brings you pleasure (besides work)?"; "What are you afraid of?" (catastrophic scenarios); "If money were not a problem, what would you do with your life?"; "What do you feel when you tell me about X?"
