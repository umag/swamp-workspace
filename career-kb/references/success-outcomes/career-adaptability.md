## Overview

A theoretical article by Mark L. Savickas proposing that **career adaptability** serve as the integrative or "bridging" construct that unifies the fragmented segments of Donald Super's **life-span, life-space theory** of careers. The article traces the 40-year evolution of Super's theory, explains its functionalist epistemological roots, identifies its principal weakness (a fragmented structure lacking parsimony), and argues that adaptation/adaptability offers the best path to integration. Savickas's central recommendation: **drop "career maturity" and replace it with "career adaptability"** as the central construct in the developmental segment, conceptualized using developmental dimensions similar to those used for maturity — planning, exploring, and deciding.

## Key Constructs & Definitions

- **Career adaptability**: "the readiness to cope with the predictable tasks of preparing for and participating in the work role and with the unpredictable adjustments prompted by changes in work and working conditions." Adaptability = "the quality of being able to change, without great difficulty, to fit new or changed circumstances."
- **Adaptation**: to make more suitable (or congruent) by changing; root *adapt* from Latin/French *adaptare* ("to fit"). Emphasizes the *interaction* between individual and environment, and (teleologically) the purpose for which change is made. Preferred over "adjust," "accommodate," "conform" (which carry negative connotations) and over the biological construct of "maturation."
- **Career maturity**: Super's original central construct — readiness to make age-appropriate educational and vocational choices; a structural model with four dimensions (two attitudinal: planning and exploration; two cognitive: information and decision making). Useful for adolescents but proved less useful for adults; carries evaluative connotations.
- **Life-span, life-space theory**: Super's segmental theory; "rubric" denoting a joining (not a fundamental integration) of theory segments. A *functional* theory — "a modest utilization of organized conceptualizations, with more explicit emphasis upon the provisional and tool character of theory."
- **Functionalism**: the system of psychology (Dewey, Angell, Carr) that frames Super's work; addresses how individuals adapt to situations via two questions — "What do people do?" and "Why do they do it?"
- **Planfulness**: future orientation and planning attitudes; "the bedrock of Super's theorizing about career maturity," the prime component in career choice readiness, retained as the core construct in Super's model of adult career adaptability.
- **Progress decision** (from related Image Theory lineage): the decision to retain or reject one's current goal-attainment strategy.

## Main Findings & Arguments

**The four segments of life-span, life-space theory** and their corresponding perspectives:
1. **Career maturity segment** (Super, 1953/1955) — career development theory; developmental perspective; a structural model of adolescent readiness.
2. **Developmental self-concept segment** (Super, 1963/1981) — phenomenological perspective; the formation, translation, and implementation of self-concepts in vocational behavior.
3. **Life-space segment** (Super, 1980/1990) — contextual perspective; life-role salience, the constellation of life roles across the theaters of home, school, work, community.
4. **Differential segment** (the traditional trait-and-factor / matching paradigm) — individual differences perspective; matching abilities and interests to occupational positions.

Super used a **functionalist "compilation" / "empirical integrator"** strategy: synthesizing existing research into conceptual models, but he died before integrating the segments. The theory's strengths (empirical grounding, propositions summarizing data, reluctance to prematurely postulate logico-deductive structures) come with its key weakness: a **fragmented structure and lack of parsimony** (Brown's and Swanson's critiques).

**Candidate bridging constructs** considered: learning, decision making, adaptation. Savickas argues **adaptation is the broadest** — learning and decision making are *components* of adaptation. Adaptation answers the functionalist "What do people do?" ("they adapt to their situations") and touches all four segments: the individual-differences perspective focuses on objective adaptive skills/styles, the phenomenological perspective on subjective goals of adaptation (a self authoring a life story), the developmental perspective on functions/processes of adaptation across the life course, and the contextual perspective on the historical/cultural situation (barriers and affordances) within which one must adapt and flourish.

The **cultural climate** favors the switch: rapid technological/economic change, "dejobbing" and organizational "re-engineering," and the need to highlight life-cycle transitions (the school-to-work transition looks more like an adaptive challenge than a maturational task). Switching also simplifies the theory — a single construct (adaptability) parsimoniously explains development in children, adolescents, *and* adults, and tightens integration across the life-span, life-space, and self-concept segments by focusing each on the individual's adaptation to environmental context and a single source of motivation.

## Frameworks & Models

**Proposed integrative framework — adaptability as bridging construct:**
- Replace **career maturity** with **career adaptability** as the central construct in the developmental segment.
- Conceptualize adaptability through three developmental dimensions parallel to the maturity dimensions:
  1. **Planfulness** (planful attitudes / future orientation) — the *core* dimension; "the sine qua non of mental health in Western culture." Can be learned; career interventions fostering concern for tomorrow increase future orientation and planful attitudes. Operationalized for adults by Super, Thompson & Lindeman (1988) via the **Adult Career Concerns Inventory**.
  2. **Exploration** — appraised in the companion article by Blustein (1997) as a developmental dimension of adaptability for life roles; a context-rich, social-constructionist view.
  3. **Decision making** — appraised in the companion article by Phillips (1997), who contributes the construct of **adaptive decision making** (vs. the over-emphasis on rationality and technique in the career-maturity view of decision making).
- Adaptability applies **across all life roles and all ages** (adolescents and adults), involving planful attitudes, self- and environmental exploration, and informed decision making.

**Functionalist theory-construction logic**: Super's theory currently *organizes and summarizes* knowledge; the goal is to move it toward *explanation* — a form whose logico-deductive principles generate new hypotheses. Adaptation/adaptability is the means proposed to do this.

## Measurement

The article is conceptual and does not present a new scale, but specifies how adaptability *should* be operationalized and assessed:
- **Outcomes of adaptability** assessed as increase/decrease in **person-situation congruence** and **movement toward self-completion**.
- Counselors/researchers assess **readiness to adapt** in terms of: planful foresight, exploration of the situation, relevant knowledge about self and situation, and decisional skill.
- Existing instruments referenced: **Career Development Inventory** (Thompson, Lindeman, Super, Jordaan & Myers, 1984) operationally defines the four career-maturity dimensions; the **Adult Career Concerns Inventory** (Super, Thompson & Lindeman, 1988) operationalizes planfulness as the core construct of adult adaptability; the **Strong Interest Inventory** leisure reports are noted as a model for extending interest assessment to non-work life roles.
- Three dimensions to measure: **planfulness, exploration, decision making** (the proposed components of career adaptability).

## Practical Implications

- **Career counseling** is reframed: from helping clients choose and adjust to occupations toward developing **readiness to cope** across all life roles and all ages — helping clients "look ahead and to look around, to develop the self, and, in due course, to choose suitable and viable opportunities to become the person she or he wants to be."
- Counselors should help individuals **continually anticipate** future choices/transitions, **explore possibilities**, and **choose directions** that improve fit and develop the self.
- Move beyond an exclusive focus on occupations: help clients see that values and interests can be fulfilled through many social roles; help with **balancing and sequencing commitments** to school, work, family, leisure, worship, and community — designing a life structure.
- **Planful attitudes can be learned** and taught — interventions that foster concern for tomorrow increase future orientation; "if they use the present as the dominant time zone in making important decisions, they may lose their future."
- "Adaptability" is preferred terminology because it lacks the evaluative connotations of "maturity," making it more usable with clients of all ages.

## Notable Quotes

- "Career adaptability [is] the readiness to cope with the predictable tasks of preparing for and participating in the work role and with the unpredictable adjustments prompted by changes in work and working conditions."
- "Now it seems worthwhile to drop maturity from the adolescent model and replace it with adaptability."
- "Adaptation … offers a better and more fundamental answer to the functionalist question 'What do people do?'—they adapt to their situations."
- "Because a future orientation and planful attitudes are the sine qua non of mental health in Western culture, planfulness must be an important dimension of adaptability."
- "If they use the present as the dominant time zone in making important decisions, they may lose their future."
- "Career counseling includes helping a client to look ahead and to look around, to develop the self, and, in due course, to choose suitable and viable opportunities to become the person she or he wants to be."
- On the theory's weakness: "its major weakness is its fragmented structure, with the concomitant complexity and lack of parsimony."

## Connections

- **Career success & outcomes (cluster core)**: adaptability is a key proximal predictor of career success; person-situation congruence and self-completion are outcome criteria. This is the foundational paper for the modern career-adaptability literature (later operationalized in Career Construction Theory and the Career Adapt-Abilities Scale).
- **Career shocks**: adaptability is precisely the "readiness to cope with unpredictable adjustments" — career shocks are the unpredictable events that test and require adaptability; both cite Image Theory and the Super lineage.
- **Career inaction**: planfulness, exploration, and decision making are the very capacities whose absence allows the inertia-enhancing mechanisms of career inaction to dominate; adaptability is a counterweight to being "stuck."
- **Transitions**: the school-to-work transition is recast as an adaptive challenge rather than a maturational task; adaptability governs how well people navigate predictable transition tasks.
- **Indecision**: adaptive decision making (Phillips) directly addresses career indecision; the cognitive maturity dimensions concern decision-making knowledge and skill.
