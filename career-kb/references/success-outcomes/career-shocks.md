## Overview

A theoretical/conceptual article (SA Journal of Industrial Psychology, 2018) arguing that the contemporary careers literature has **overemphasized individual agency** (protean career, boundaryless career, career self-management, proactive behaviors, employability) and neglected the role of **context** — specifically, unexpected external events the authors call **career shocks**. Invoking Lewin's classic view that behavior is a function of person *and* environment, the authors aim to (1) provide an integrative definition and conceptualization of career shocks, (2) delineate key attributes/dimensions that shape their impact, (3) review theoretical perspectives useful for studying them, and (4) set a research agenda. The goal is to *supplement, not replace* agentic career models, yielding a richer account of how careers actually evolve.

## Key Constructs & Definitions

- **Career shock** (the paper's proposed definition): "a disruptive and extraordinary event that is, at least to some degree, caused by factors outside the focal individual's control and that triggers a deliberate thought process concerning one's career. The occurrence of a career shock can vary in terms of predictability, and can be either positively or negatively valenced."
- **Chance events / serendipity / happenstance**: prior labels for major unexpected career-relevant events (Bright, Pryor & Harpham; Betsworth & Hansen; Miller); the careers-literature stream that grounds career shocks.
- **Shock** (turnover literature, Lee & Mitchell, 1994): "a very distinguishable event that jars employees towards deliberate judgements about their jobs"; shocks "initiate psychological analyses" and "trigger deliberation."
- **Two founding literature streams**: (1) **career chance events** (rooted in the careers literature) and (2) **shocks and turnover** (rooted in organizational behavior).
- **Career** (Arthur, Hall & Lawrence, 1989): the authors note the *correct* quote is "the **evolving** sequence of a person's work experiences over time" — often misquoted as "unfolding"; "evolving" allows for environmental interaction and chance events.

### Four core characteristics implied by the definition
1. Shocks **activate deliberate thought** about one's career (and can lead to behavior change altering the career path).
2. Shocks are **contextual antecedents at least partly outside the individual's control** in occurrence and consequences.
3. Shocks **vary in how unexpected vs. expected** they are (in occurrence and/or in effect).
4. Shocks can be **positively or negatively valenced**.

## Main Findings & Arguments

Career shocks had a relatively dominant position in career theorizing decades ago (Roe & Baruch, 1967; Miller, 1983; Hart et al., 1971) but interest waned with the rise of the boundaryless/protean paradigms. The authors argue shocks are **more relevant than ever** because contemporary careers are more complex, dynamic, and unpredictable (rise of temporary/flexible/"gig" work; Frey & Osborne's estimate that 47% of US occupations are at risk of automation within two decades; the post-2012 economic crisis). Empirical precedents: Hirschi (2010) found career shocks affect the school-to-work transition and relate to subjective career success; Seibert et al. (2013) found positive and negative shocks differentially affect the decision to pursue graduate education (a departing mentor *increases* the odds; a negative organizational change *reduces* them); Hirschi & Valero (2017) linked shocks to career decidedness.

A key conceptual point on **valence**: it is the **experienced** valence of the shock to the individual — not the ultimate positive/negative career consequences — that determines categorization (e.g., having twins is a *positive* shock because experienced with positive emotions, even if it damages the career). Shocks should be studied in **series**, not isolation (positively and negatively valenced shocks may combine to a neutral composite; Morrell et al. found expected shocks tend to be positive and personal, negative shocks tend to be work-related).

The overarching argument: contemporary career processes can only be fully understood by studying the **interplay between agency and context** — both are crucial.

## Frameworks & Models

**Six attributes of career shocks** (a proposed basis for an evidence-based typology and future measure development):
1. **Frequency** — shocks are by definition relatively infrequent/extraordinary (habituation to frequent events inhibits the deliberate thought process); low base rate. Repeated shocks may show sensitization (e.g., serial unemployment) or adaptation (e.g., serial divorce) and may interact to cross a **threshold** that "kicks in" responses.
2. **Controllability** — degree of (perceived/actual) control over the event and its effects.
3. **Predictability** — degree to which the event can be foreseen (ranging expected-to-unexpected). Predictability and controllability are **related but distinct** (a layoff may be predictable yet uncontrollable; a broken leg unpredictable yet controllable) and likely **interact** (when both are low, e.g., an unforeseen uncontrollable layoff, career outcomes are most negative).
4. **Valence** — positive vs. negative, based on *experienced* emotion; can vary in intensity; more intensely valenced shocks have stronger relationships with career decisions/outcomes.
5. **Duration** — of the event itself and of its proximal/distal consequences; longer-duration shocks tend to have more severe consequences (with interactions, e.g., short-but-intense vs. long-but-mild).
6. **Locus / source** — interpersonal (harassment, discrimination), family-related (pregnancy, divorce, death, illness), organizational (mass layoffs), environmental (natural disaster), or geopolitical (war); also classifiable as **generic** vs. **context-/population-specific**. Morrell et al.'s k-means cluster analysis found two clusters: one more expected, positive, personal, specific, and unavoidable than the other.

**Five candidate theoretical frameworks for studying career shocks:**
1. **Image Theory** (Beach, 1990) — individuals hold a **value image** (values/ideals), **trajectory image** (goals/ideal future state), and **strategic image** (strategies/tactics); they screen alternatives against these images and tend to hold to the status quo (the "progress decision" governs whether to retain or reject the current strategy). A shock disrupts and can alter these images.
2. **Unfolding model of turnover** (Lee & Mitchell, 1994) — borrows from Image Theory; describes four/five decision paths to turnover; introduces "shock" as the jarring event; shocks precede turnover in three of five paths.
3. **Event System Theory** (Morgeson, Mitchell & Liu, 2015) — focuses on non-routine, transient events bounded in space/time; strong/impactful events are high in **novelty, disruption (discontinuity), and criticality**. Career shocks are such strong events.
4. **Conservation of Resources Theory** (Hobfoll, 2001) — not yet applied to shocks but promising; positive shocks may boost resources (self-efficacy, perceived employability), negative shocks may deplete resources and trigger a loss spiral.
5. **Affective Events Theory** (Weiss & Cropanzano, 1996) — addresses the **proximal emotional reactions** in the immediate aftermath of a shock (other theories focus on judgmental processes/distal outcomes); incorporates dispositions as a moderator of the work-event => affective-reaction link, explaining why the same shock yields different outcomes for different people.
- Also relevant: **Chaos Theory of Careers** (Bright & Pryor, 2005) and **Happenstance Learning Theory** (Krumboltz, 2009) — counseling-oriented; emphasize *that* unplanned events matter but do not explicate *how* the process unfolds.

**Research agenda**: study effects of shock *occurrence* and *quantity/frequency* (threshold effects) on career outcomes; study the *interplay* between shocks and agency-related factors (career self-management, career crafting); investigate whether individuals can be "inoculated" / become more resilient against negative shocks; elaborate the **nomological network** by studying attributes separately (intrapersonal vs. interpersonal, social vs. organizational); develop further theory and a dedicated theoretical model integrating person and environment.

## Measurement

No empirical scale is presented — this is conceptual. Recommendations: develop and validate measurement instruments for career shocks. The authors argue **against one overall reflective measure** of "career shocks as a construct"; instead, they advocate developing a **typology of career shocks and its attributes** with **relatively independent operationalizations** of each shock/attribute, so unique variance is not lost. Suggested methods: survey-based research building on Seibert et al. (2013); **qualitative/exploratory interview studies** across target groups (e.g., young vs. older workers, who face different shocks); analysis via **text mining** or **qualitative comparative analysis (QCA)** for large, varied datasets.

## Practical Implications

- **Career scholars / I-O psychology / HRM**: include career shocks in research models to obtain actionable insight about career development and management; study agency and context together.
- Implicit guidance for **individuals and counsellors**: because shocks are partly uncontrollable, purely agentic self-management models are insufficient; the predictability and controllability attributes have a clear bearing on individual agency in *mitigating* consequences — more predictable and controllable shocks (e.g., advance notice plus a good outplacement programme) have diminished negative effects via emotion-regulation and stress mechanisms.
- For **organizations/governments**: the locus of a shock determines which stakeholders should marshal resources to address its consequences (a shock can affect single individuals or whole populations).
- People may be able to **prepare for and become more resilient** to career shocks (cf. Chaos Theory of Careers) — an argument for building personal resources in advance.

## Connections

- **Career success & outcomes (cluster core)**: shocks are "a major antecedent of career development episodes"; how one reacts to them is "an important determinant of the level of one's career success" — directly tied to subjective career success (Hirschi, 2010) and career decidedness.
- **Career adaptability**: career shocks are the "unpredictable adjustments prompted by changes in work and working conditions" that Savickas's career adaptability is defined as the readiness to cope with; both papers draw on Image Theory and the Super/Lee-Mitchell lineage. Adaptability and resilience are proposed buffers against negative shocks.
- **Career inaction**: a conceptual mirror image — career inaction is the failure to act *despite agency*; career shocks are external triggers that *force* deliberation. Both critique the over-rational, agency-centric career paradigm and both build on the unfolding model of turnover.
- **Transitions**: shocks frequently precede turnover and major career transitions (school-to-work, graduate education decision); EST's novelty/disruption/criticality describes what makes a transition-triggering event impactful.
- **Indecision/decidedness**: Hirschi & Valero link chance events to career decidedness; shocks can both disrupt and crystallize career direction.
