## Overview

A study (Journal of Career Assessment, 2011) investigating the Emotional and Personality-related Career decision-making Difficulties (EPCD) model and questionnaire developed by Saka, Gati and colleagues. Whereas most career-indecision research targets temporary/developmental difficulties (the "undecided"), the EPCD model targets the more chronic, pervasive difficulties characteristic of "indecisive" individuals. The paper tests EPCD's associations with established personality measures across three samples (1,119 young adults, ages 17-35): pre-academic students, train passengers, and visitors to a career-planning website.

## Key Constructs & Definitions

- **Career indecision:** Difficulties preventing individuals from making a career decision; problems/challenges to be dealt with prior to or during the decision-making process.
- **Career indecisiveness:** More chronic and pervasive difficulties stemming from emotional problems or personality-related characteristics (vs. the normative, temporary difficulties of the "undecided").
- **EPCD model:** 11 difficulty categories organized into 3 major clusters — Pessimistic Views, Anxiety, and Self-concept and Identity.
- **Pessimistic Views cluster** — negative cognitive biases/perceptions; 3 categories: pessimistic views about the process (low career decision self-efficacy), about the world of work (overly negative views of occupations/jobs), and about one's control (external locus of control over process/choice/outcomes).
- **Anxiety cluster** — 4 categories: anxiety about the process; anxiety connected with the uncertainty in choosing (uncertainty about the future, about being in an undecided state, low tolerance for ambiguity); anxiety about choosing (perfectionism, fear of losing potential options, fear of unsuitable occupation, anxiety about responsibility); anxiety about the outcome (fear of failure / not fulfilling expectations).
- **Self-concept and Identity cluster** — 4 categories: trait anxiety, low self-esteem, uncrystallized identity (unstable vocational self-concept), conflictual attachment and separation (excessive dependence on / need to please significant others).
- **Need for cognitive closure (NFC):** A tendency to avoid ambiguity and find a definite answer on a topic.
- **Locus of control (LoC):** Degree to which individuals expect outcomes to be contingent on their behavior (internal) vs. chance/fate/powerful others (external).
- **Career decision self-efficacy (CDSE):** Degree to which individuals feel confident they can successfully perform career decision-making tasks.

## Main Findings & Arguments

**EPCD and the Big Five (NEO-PI, n = 254), Hypothesis 1 — supported:**
- Neuroticism strongly positively correlated with total EPCD (r = .60); correlations with the three clusters: Pessimistic Views .36, Anxiety .46, Self-concept and Identity .66.
- Conscientiousness negative (r = -.29 total), Extraversion negative (r = -.26 total).
- Openness to experience NOT significantly correlated with total EPCD (r = .06); unexpectedly positively correlated with Pessimistic Views (r = .13).
- Agreeableness positively correlated with total EPCD (r = .20) — contrary to some prior research; higher agreeableness associated with *more* difficulties (interpreted: desire to please others is maladaptive in career decision making).

**EPCD with NFC and Perfectionism (Hypothesis 2 — partially supported):**
- NFC (n = 351) significantly positively correlated with EPCD; correlations with clusters generally higher than perfectionism's.
- Multidimensional Perfectionism Scale (MPS, n = 190): positive and significant with all clusters except a nonsignificant correlation with Pessimistic Views, though correlations were small.
- NFC more strongly associated with the Anxiety cluster than with Pessimistic Views; no support for the hypothesis that Anxiety's correlation with perfectionism would differ from the other clusters.

**EPCD with LoC and CDSE (Hypothesis 3 — partially supported):**
- LoC (n = 344): more external LoC associated with higher EPCD (r = -.35 total; coding 1 = external, 2 = internal).
- CDSE (n = 511): lower self-efficacy associated with higher EPCD (r = -.54 total). Contrary to hypothesis, CDSE was most strongly correlated with the Self-concept and Identity cluster (r = -.50), not Pessimistic Views (r = -.36).
- LoC more strongly correlated with Pessimistic Views than with Anxiety.

**EPCD and decision status (Hypothesis 4 — supported):**
- One-way ANOVA on decision status (decided / partially decided / undecided) was significant (F = 80.83, η² = .13).
- Decided participants had lower EPCD (M = 4.20) than the two other groups; partially decided (M = 5.04) lower than undecided (M = 5.48). Less-decided individuals had higher emotional/personality difficulties — supports the EPCD's diagnostic validity.

**Cluster intercorrelations:** Anxiety-Self-concept/Identity (r = .58) > Pessimistic Views-Anxiety (r = .43) > Pessimistic Views-Self-concept/Identity (r = .45). The Anxiety and Self-concept/Identity clusters are the closest pair.

**Group differences:** The Internet group showed slightly higher EPCD, NFC, neuroticism, and lower CDSE and extraversion than the train/preparatory groups; differences were small (η² mostly ~.01-.10) so results were reported across the combined sample.

## Frameworks & Models

**EPCD three-cluster, 11-category model** (Saka & Gati 2007; Saka et al. 2008):
- Pessimistic Views → process / world of work / control
- Anxiety → process / uncertainty in choosing / choosing / outcome
- Self-concept and Identity → trait anxiety / self-esteem / uncrystallized identity / conflictual attachment and separation

Saka and Gati suggest Self-concept and Identity difficulties are more central and may developmentally precede Anxiety and Pessimistic Views difficulties; the study's pattern (no differentiation of this cluster's associations from the other two) lends partial support.

## Measurement

- **EPCD questionnaire:** Short 25-item version (original 53 items); each of 11 categories represented by 2 statements plus a warm-up item; 2 embedded validity items. 9-point scale (1 = does not describe me at all, 9 = describes me well). Cronbach's α in study: total .90; Pessimistic Views .72, Anxiety .89, Self-concept and Identity .84.
- **NEO-PI-R:** 60-item short Big Five (12 items/factor), 5-point Likert.
- **NFC (Webster & Kruglanski 1994):** 42 items, 6-point Likert; 5 factors (order/structure, predictability, decisiveness, ambiguity, closed-mindedness).
- **MPS (Hewitt & Flett 1991):** Used self-oriented and socially prescribed perfectionism subscales (7-point); other-oriented subscale excluded as irrelevant to chronic indecisiveness.
- **LoC (Rotter 1966):** 15 forced-choice items; score 1 (external) to 2 (internal).
- **CDSE (Taylor & Betz 1983):** 30-item Hebrew short form, 5 subscales, 0-9 confidence scale; total α = .94.
- **Range of Considered Alternatives (RCA):** 6-statement single-item measure of decision status, grouped into undecided / partially decided / decided.

## Practical Implications

- The EPCD can help career counselors distinguish clients with developmental indecision from those with more pervasive, chronic indecisiveness rooted in personality/emotional factors.
- Future development of a clinically significant cutoff score could turn the EPCD into a screening instrument.
- Diagnosing the *source* of difficulties (which cluster/category is salient) gives counselors targeted leverage; in this study participants received personalized feedback on the salience of each cluster.
- A tendency to please others / dependence on others (linked to agreeableness and conflictual attachment) is maladaptive for career decision making and should be addressed in counseling.
- Because Self-concept and Identity difficulties may be developmentally primary, interventions may need to address identity and self-esteem before, or alongside, anxiety and pessimistic views.

- "These individuals are often considered as indecisive" — distinguishing chronic indecisiveness from the normative "undecided."
- "Higher levels of Agreeableness were associated with higher levels of emotional and personality-related career decision-making difficulties... a tendency to please others is maladaptive."

## Connections

- **Indecision:** This is a core indecision/indecisiveness document — it operationalizes the chronic, personality-rooted end of the career indecision spectrum (vs. developmental indecision).
- **Inaction:** Anxiety about choosing, fear of losing options, and decisional procrastination connect to career inaction and avoidance.
- **Impostorism:** Low self-esteem, low career decision self-efficacy, and pessimistic views about one's own process overlap strongly with impostorism and self-doubt.
- **Adaptability:** Low tolerance for ambiguity and high need for cognitive closure are the inverse of adaptability/curiosity resources (Savickas).
- **Success:** External locus of control and low CDSE here mirror the trait hurdles that Ng & Feldman (2014) found depress subjective career success.
- **Transitions:** Career indecision is most acute at transition points (school-to-work, choosing a major); uncrystallized identity links to transition-theory's identity reconstruction.
