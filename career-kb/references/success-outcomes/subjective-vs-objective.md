## Overview

This is a conceptual/theoretical article (Journal of Organizational Behavior, 26, 113-136, 2005) by Peter A. Heslin (Cox School of Business, Southern Methodist University). It argues that within the vast literature on antecedents of career success, the success *criterion* itself has generally been operationalized in a "rather deficient manner." The paper reviews the most common approaches to assessing objective and subjective career success, identifies four implicit and problematic assumptions, develops a typology of four types of subjective success criteria, and offers five propositions about the contextual and individual factors that influence when objective versus subjective and self-referent versus other-referent criteria are most salient. It draws heavily on Everett Hughes's (1937, 1958) objective/subjective career distinction and on social comparison theory (Festinger, 1954).

## Key Constructs & Definitions

- **Objective career** (Hughes) — "directly observable, measurable, and verifiable by an impartial third party." Objective career success is defined by verifiable attainments such as pay, promotions, and occupational status.
- **Subjective career** (Hughes) — "only experienced directly by the person engaged in her or his career." Subjective career success is defined by an individual's reactions to his or her unfolding career experiences; it spans reactions to actual and anticipated career-related attainments across a broader time frame than immediate job satisfaction and a wider range of outcomes (sense of identity, purpose, work-life balance).
- **Criterion contamination** — objective criteria are affected by factors beyond an individual's control (power structures, taxation systems, occupational pay norms, labor market conditions, competition).
- **Criterion deficiency** — objective criteria do not capture relevant facets of the focal construct; pay and promotions are not the only objective outcomes people seek (e.g., teachers, mentors, doctors, bus drivers value other objective markers).
- **Self-referent criteria** — career outcomes evaluated relative to personal standards, aspirations, and preferences.
- **Other-referent criteria** — career outcomes evaluated relative to the attainments and expectations of others (e.g., whether one is paid more/less than the industry average or a colleague).
- **Winner-take-all markets** (Frank & Cook, 1995) — markets characterized by huge rewards for high performance *relative to* others, where prizes go to a few top performers with small differences in talent producing massive differences in rewards.
- **Work orientation** (Wrzesniewski et al., 1997) — three distinct orientations: *job* (focus on financial rewards, work as means to income), *career* (deeper investment, advancement, status/prestige/power within the occupation), *calling* (fulfillment as an end in itself, work makes the world better).
- **Goal orientation** (Dweck & Elliott, 1983) — *performance goals* (demonstrate/validate competence, avoid negative judgments; based on entity implicit theory that ability is fixed) vs *learning goals* (acquire new knowledge/skills; based on incremental implicit theory that ability is malleable).
- **Ideological currency** (Thompson & Bunderson, 2003) — "credible commitments to pursue a valued cause or principle (not limited to self-interest) that are implicitly exchanged at the nexus of the individual-organizational relationship."

## Main Findings & Arguments

**Four implicit, problematic assumptions in the career-success literature:**
1. Objective outcomes (pay, promotions) are adequate proxies for success, even beyond managerial/professional contexts.
2. Job and career satisfaction adequately capture the breadth of dimensions on which people react to their careers.
3. People are similar in their concern for objective vs subjective success.
4. People conceptualize and evaluate their career success only relative to self-referent criteria.

**Evidence the assumptions fail:**
- Arthur & Rousseau (1996) found more than 75% of career-related articles published in major interdisciplinary journals between 1980 and 1994 focused on objective perspectives.
- Reitman & Schneer (2003) — a survey of 116 MBA managerial careers over 13 years found two-thirds had NOT followed the prototypical managerial career path; protean managerial careers paid no price in income, career satisfaction, or job security.
- Friedman & Greenhaus (2000) — 800+ business professionals; factor analysis revealed five dimensions of the meaning of career success: status, time for self, challenge, security, and social. With the exception of status, considerable emphasis on subjective criteria beyond prestige/power/money/advancement.
- Finegold & Mohrman (2001) — among 4,500 knowledge workers and managers from eight countries, work-life balance was rated the MOST important career facet, yet most studies do not include even a single item assessing it.
- Cangemi & Guttschalk (1986) — survey of 35,000 employees: what employees most wanted (full appreciation for work done, feeling in on things, sympathetic understanding of personal problems) differed substantially from what supervisors thought they wanted (money).
- Hilton & Dill (1962, p. 163): "the shortcomings of salary as a measure of man's progress are well known" — yet salary and promotions continued to be applied as the sole criteria of career success 40 years later.
- Heslin (2003a) — among 71 part-time MBA students, 68% used other-referent criteria; 39% of all criteria generated were other-referent. Ratings of other-referent success accounted for an additional 12% of variance in overall subjective career success when self-referent career success was held constant.
- Brooks et al. (2003) — survey of 1,481 I/O psychologists: practitioners valued affiliation, structure, and money significantly more than academics, who valued autonomy and science more — demonstrating within-profession variation in success criteria.
- Konrad et al. (2000) meta-analysis — men most value objective outcomes (money, advancement); women tend to evaluate career success in broader, more subjective ways (accomplishment, growth, challenge, interpersonal relationships, opportunities to help others).
- Juntunen et al. (2001) — in-depth interviews with 18 Northern Plains American Indians revealed a communitarian construal of success, measured largely in terms of contribution to others' well-being.

**Cautions about subjective measures:** A person who thinks they have a successful career does not necessarily consider themselves less successful if they hold a dissatisfying job; high job satisfaction does not equal subjective career success if it exacts a toll on health, family, or personal values. Standardized scales (e.g., Greenhaus et al., 1990) measuring satisfaction with hierarchical success or advancement may be of limited relevance to contractors, small-business owners, or career-plateaued individuals — assessing irrelevant constructs inflates measurement error.

## Frameworks & Models

**Typology — Four types of subjective success criteria (Table 1)** crosses the *objective domain / subjective domain* against the *self-referent domain / other-referent domain*:
1. **Objective/self-referent** — e.g., my financial and promotion aspirations. Potential correlate: a market culture.
2. **Subjective/self-referent** — e.g., my goals for work-life balance and fulfillment. Potential correlates: a calling orientation, a non-linear career.
3. **Objective/other-referent** — e.g., my colleagues' pay and my social standing. Potential correlates: a winner-take-all market, a job or career orientation.
4. **Subjective/other-referent** — e.g., my stimulation and fun, relative to my peers. Potential correlate: a clan culture.

**Five propositions:**
- **P1:** People working in winner-take-all markets use more objective and other-referent criteria of career success than those in fields where rewards are more evenly distributed (quadrant 3).
- **P2:** In clan cultures, subjective and other-referent criteria are relatively salient, compared to objective and self-referent criteria that are more salient in market cultures (quadrants 1 and 4).
- **P3:** For those with a calling, subjective and self-referent criteria are more salient than objective and other-referent criteria, compared to people with a job or career work orientation (quadrants 2 and 3).
- **P4:** Performance goal orientation is positively associated with use of other-referent criteria of career success, especially by those who perceive their career as successful.
- **P5:** Subjective and self-referent criteria are particularly salient when individuals have a non-linear, rather than a linear, career.

**Supporting mechanisms:** Social comparison theory (Festinger, 1954) — people evaluate outcomes by comparing to similar others when objective information is unavailable; clan cultures' vague performance criteria and close peer relationships foster other-referent comparison. The attraction-selection-attrition (ASA) cycle (Schneider, 1987) and socialization explain how winner-take-all/market cultures become populated by people who use objective/other-referent criteria. Market culture = contractual individual-organization relationship; clan culture = fraternal, committed, loyalty-based relationship. Linear career = "climber," progressive hierarchical steps, zero-sum/competitive → other-referent. Non-linear careers (boundaryless, protean, expert, self-realizer) involve commitment to personal values → self-referent; Hall (2002) — non-linear protean career contract "is with the self and one's work."

## Measurement

- **Objective criteria:** salary (Thorndike, 1934), salary growth (Hilton & Dill, 1962), promotions (Thorndike, 1963) — the most widely used and accessible indicators; readily available, standardized within firms, free of self-serving and common-method variance. But contaminated and deficient; have "limited meaning" where pay and promotions are institutionalized (civil service, military).
- **Subjective criteria:** most commonly operationalized as job or career satisfaction (Judge et al., 1999 argue dissatisfied individuals are unlikely to consider their careers successful). Greenhaus et al. (1990) career satisfaction scale is widely adopted. The paper argues SCS should span a broader time frame than immediate job satisfaction and a wider range of outcomes (identity, purpose, work-life balance) — and that future research should avoid adopting job satisfaction as a sole proxy for SCS.
- **Self-referent measurement:** self-report career-success measures have been almost exclusively self-referential. Notable exceptions: Lawrence's (1984) measure of feeling on/ahead/behind schedule relative to peers; two items in Turban & Dougherty's (1994) 4-item measure assessing success relative to "your co-workers" and "significant others."
- **Three avenues for improving SCS measurement:** (a) drawing on research into what employees actually want; (b) studying how people in different career contexts conceptualize success; (c) adopting more qualitative methods.
- **Improved measurement heuristics:** ask the focal population how they know/anticipate the extent to which their career is successful; assess participants on the 7-10 criteria they identify as most salient; ensure each criterion assessed is valued and relevant to those surveyed; consider weighting success criteria by rated importance.

## Practical Implications

- Career scholars should transcend the four implicit assumptions and be more sensitive to the criteria that study participants actually use to construe and judge their career success — improving criterion-related and ecological validity of career theories and intervention programs.
- For one-on-one career coaching, assess and weight the specific 7-10 criteria a client identifies as most salient rather than imposing standardized criteria.
- Organizations can use *ideological rewards* (Blau, 1964) — opportunities to contribute to a worthwhile cause — to nurture relevant subjective success criteria ("made a difference"); but ideological currency can backfire (moral outrage) if the organization is perceived to have violated its ideological commitments.
- Recognize gender, cultural, and sub-population differences: men vs women, individualist vs collectivist cultures, profit vs not-for-profit contexts, entrepreneurs, older workers, the self-employed, expatriates, stigmatized workers — career-success research has overwhelmingly focused on white, male, well-educated managers/professionals in large hierarchical organizations.
- Be aware that receiving high pay and promotions does not necessarily make people feel proud or successful, and can cause work and personal alienation or depressive reactions.

## Notable Quotes

> "Within the vast literature on the antecedents of career success, the success criterion has generally been operationalized in a rather deficient manner."

> "Objective career success is defined by verifiable attainments, such as pay, promotions, and occupational status, which have long been considered the hallmarks of career success across a wide range of societies."

> "Schein (1990) provides two anecdotes of how social comparisons operate in evaluating career success. In the first, an entrepreneur who made two million dollars felt like a failure because his friends all owned 300-million-dollar companies. In the second example, another person who leveled off in middle management felt very successful because he went so far beyond the level his father attained."

> "Ratings of other-referent success accounted for an additional 12 per cent of variance in ratings of overall subjective career success, when career success relative to self-referent criteria was held constant. Thus, other-referent success appears to be a potentially important, albeit largely neglected facet of subjective career success."

> "If you compare yourself with others you may become vain or bitter, for always there will be greater and lesser persons than yourself. (Desiderata)"

> "Even though most people who have careers are not white, male, well-educated managers or professionals working in large, hierarchical organizations, the vast majority of careers research has been focused on this very narrow subset of all the people who are engaged in a career."

> "Career success potentially means much more to people engaged in a career than has typically been measured by empirical research on this topic. The resulting criterion deficiency has probably diminished the validity with which many career theories, models, and intervention programs have been assessed."

## Connections

- **Subjective & objective outcomes (Spurk, Hirschi & Dries, 2019)** — that systematic review directly builds on this paper's OCS/SCS distinction and criterion-deficiency argument; Heslin is cited there as foundational.
- **Success** — the foundational conceptual treatment of what "career success" means and how it should be measured; the criterion-deficiency thesis underpins most later success research.
- **Adaptability** — the protean/boundaryless/non-linear career types and self-referent evaluation connect to career adaptability literature; non-linear careers require self-set yardsticks.
- **Transitions** — non-linear career types involve "regular changes between seemingly unrelated careers"; September 11 example (Wrzesniewski, 2002) of people transitioning to teaching/nursing/firefighting with altered success criteria links to career-transition research.
- **Impostorism** — social comparison theory and other-referent criteria (feeling like a failure despite objective success — Schein's entrepreneur anecdote) connect strongly to impostor phenomenon literature.
- **Indecision / inaction** — work orientation and goal orientation as individual differences shaping how people evaluate careers relate to career decision-making and goal-engagement literatures.
- **Shocks** — September 11 as a societal shock that caused people to re-evaluate linear careers against broader altruistic, subjective success criteria.
