## Overview

A systematic review (Medline, Embase, PsycINFO; Jan 1966–May 2018) plus a novel lay-literature search, evaluating the published evidence on the prevalence of impostor syndrome in employed populations, its relationship to workplace performance and burnout, its common comorbidities, and the most effective treatments. 284 titles screened, 149 excluded, yielding 66 articles describing 62 studies with 14,161 participants. Published in the Journal of General Internal Medicine. The review's stated motivation: impostor syndrome is heavily discussed in lay/media literature as a behavioral-health condition impairing performance and contributing to burnout, yet no prior systematic review existed and clinicians lack evidence to diagnose or treat it.

## Key Constructs & Definitions

- **Impostor syndrome (also impostor phenomenon, fraud syndrome, perceived fraudulence, impostor experience):** describes high-achieving individuals who, despite their objective successes, fail to internalize their accomplishments and have persistent self-doubt and fear of being exposed as a fraud. They struggle to accurately attribute performance to actual competence — attributing successes to external factors (luck, help from others) and setbacks to evidence of professional inadequacy.
- **Not a recognized psychiatric disorder:** not in the DSM-5 nor ICD-10. Originally identified by psychologists Clance and Imes in 1978 among high-achieving professional women; broadened to men, women, many professional settings, and multiple ethnic/racial groups by more recent research.
- **Minority status stress:** discrimination-related stress experienced by ethnic minority students; one study found impostor syndrome was a *stronger* predictor of mental health problems than minority status stress itself.
- **Survivor guilt:** guilt over one's own success/survival; found to significantly predict depression and to relate to impostorism among African American college students.
- **Burnout:** characterized by emotional exhaustion, cynicism/depersonalization, and reduced personal efficacy; impostor syndrome found to be a significant contributor among employee populations including clinicians.

## Main Findings & Arguments

- **Volume and recency:** 62 studies, 14,161 participants; all included studies published 1990–2018, half within the last 6 years — congruent with an explosion of interest. 34 of the studies were of student populations (weighted mean age ~20 years); 19 described employed populations.
- **Prevalence:** varied widely from 9% to 82%, largely depending on the screening tool and cutoff used. Example: among 654 Korean Catholics, prevalence was 24% using a CIPS cutoff of 62 vs. 39% using a cutoff of 58. Likely subject to publication bias (all included studies reported some participants endorsing impostor feelings).
- **Demographics:** common among both men and women and across a wide age range (adolescents to late-stage professionals). 60% of participants were women.
- **Gender effects:** of 33 articles comparing rates by gender, 16 found women reported significantly higher impostor feelings than men; 17 found no difference. Brauer & Proyer found a gender effect among psychology students but not professionals.
- **Age effects:** of 6 studies, 2 found increased age associated with decreased impostor feelings, 3 found no age effect. Brauer & Proyer found age significantly negatively correlated with impostor feelings among working professionals but not undergraduates.
- **Ethnic minorities:** 11 articles evaluated minority groups. Impostor syndrome common among African American, Asian American, and Latino/a American college students; significantly negatively correlated with psychological well-being and positively correlated with depression and anxiety. Predisposing factors: lack of adequate financial aid, need to work, racial discrimination, negative stereotypes, being first in family to pursue advanced education. Bernard et al.: African American freshmen reporting frequent discrimination but low distress from it had higher impostor syndrome than those with high distress from discrimination. Few non-White individuals were included in samples used to standardize impostor measures — raising validity concerns for minority populations.
- **Comorbidities:** depression, anxiety, low self-esteem, somatic symptoms, and social dysfunction. Among high school students, impostor feelings correlated significantly with a history of prior suicidal ideation and attempts, and depression.
- **Workplace consequences:** impostor syndrome associated with impaired job performance, lower job satisfaction, and burnout across employee populations including healthcare professionals. Bechtoldt: supervisors scoring high on impostorism were more inclined to delegate both routine and challenging tasks to subordinates who doubted their own abilities. Crawford et al.: significant relationship between impostor syndrome and self-reported work/life conflict among community college employees, minimized when employees perceived greater organizational support. Neureiter & Traut-Mattausch: employees with more impostor feelings reported less career planning and motivation to lead.
- **Treatment:** none of the included articles evaluated a specific treatment (e.g., CBT). The only related work was Matthews & Clance's 1985 qualitative description of treating 41 people in private practice — recommended validating patients' doubts/fears, directly addressing fears of failure, and group therapy to counter isolation; no treatment-intensity or outcome data.
- **Quality limitations:** nearly all studies were cross-sectional convenience-sample surveys; only one experimental design and one longitudinal study; no randomized trials; only 20 studies reported IRB approval; response rates ranged 2.8%–97.2% (often unreported).
- **Lay literature:** during March 2018–March 2019, 2,317 internet articles published on impostor syndrome (150–200/month), with 133,545 social-media engagements. Academic literature is almost entirely indexed under "Impostor Phenomenon"; lay literature is entirely indexed under "Imposter Syndrome." Lay articles are mostly "What is..." then "How-To" treatment-tip pieces.

## Frameworks & Models

The review does not propose its own theoretical model; it synthesizes others. Recurrent explanatory ideas surfaced across included studies:

- **Attribution pattern:** impostors attribute success externally (luck, charm, help, error) and failure internally (inadequacy) — the inverse of a self-serving bias.
- **Impostor cycle (Clance):** because successes are not internalized, each new task triggers renewed fear of failing, perpetuating the cycle.
- **Profile-based approach:** Leonhardt et al. / Rohrmann et al. found impostorism is *not* homogeneous — distinguished "true impostors" (high impostor self-concept, work-related stress, negative self-evaluation, procrastination) from "strategic impostors" (use deliberate self-presentation, do not show trait-level self-doubt).
- **Comorbidity-screening implication:** in the absence of impostor-specific treatments, the review frames impostor syndrome practically as a flag for screening and treating comorbid depression and anxiety with evidence-based therapies.

## Measurement

Diagnostic tools used across included studies:
- **Clance Impostor Phenomenon Scale (CIPS):** the predominant tool; 20 items, 5-point Likert, covering self-assessed competency, praise, and success. Common cutoffs: <40 = no impostorism, 40–59 = mild, 60–79 = moderate, >80 = severe; others use a cutoff of 62, or the population median, to classify impostors vs. non-impostors. Mean CIPS scores in included studies frequently ran in the 53–67 range.
- **Harvey Impostor Phenomenon Scale (HIPS):** used by six studies; 14 items, 7-point Likert, on personality traits; higher score = greater identification.
- **Leary Impostor Scale:** used by two studies.
- **Self-developed questionnaires:** used by two studies.
- **Perceived Fraudulence Scale:** one study.
- **Young Impostor Scale:** one study.
Considerable variation exists in how researchers interpret specific scores and cutoffs.

## Practical Implications

- Clinicians and employers should be aware that impostor syndrome is prevalent across the *entire* population, not just women — and take steps to assess for it.
- Caregivers, professors, and employers should be mindful of its prevalence among ethnic minorities; current impostor measures may not be valid for minority populations and need re-standardization.
- In the absence of impostor-specific treatments, patients with impostor feelings should be rigorously screened for depression and anxiety and treated with evidence-based therapies; referral to group therapy (where peers discuss doubt and failure) may be particularly therapeutic to counter the "only one" isolation.
- Authors recommend prospective evaluation of individual and group CBT focused on impostor feelings, and recommend impostor syndrome be considered for DSM inclusion.
- Employers/professors can target impostor syndrome by creating healthier expectations, a culture where mistakes are not interpreted as failures, and by publicly acknowledging and celebrating accomplishments. Structured (training, orientation, onboarding) and unstructured (mentoring, coaching) development activities and resilience training are recommended; de-identified assessments can track change over time.

## Connections

- **Impostorism:** the anchoring construct of this cluster; this review is the umbrella synthesis that the other two papers (Hutchins et al. on work outcomes; Neureiter & Traut-Mattausch on career development) sit beneath as primary studies.
- **Success / outcomes:** directly links impostorism to impaired job performance, lower job satisfaction, and burnout — the cluster's core theme of how internal states shape career outcomes.
- **Transitions:** student-to-professional transition is salient — most evidence is from students; impostor feelings carry into early-career and late-stage professional life (e.g., clinical nurse specialists post-role-transition).
- **Inaction / indecision:** connects to findings that impostors show less career planning and lower motivation to lead — a bridge to Neureiter & Traut-Mattausch's career-development consequences.
- **Adaptability:** comorbidity with depression/anxiety and links to burnout speak to reduced capacity to adapt to career demands and shocks.
- **Shocks:** survivor guilt and discrimination experiences function as antecedent stressors/shocks that predispose to impostorism, especially in minority populations.
