## Overview

A meta-analytic review (Journal of Vocational Behavior, 2014) that inverts the usual research question: instead of asking what *promotes* career success, it asks what *undermines* people's subjective perceptions and feelings about their careers. The authors conceptualize career hurdles as obstacles in attaining career goals and use conservation of resources (COR) theory to argue that hurdles represent resource losses that both make people feel worse about their careers and make goal attainment harder. They cumulate 216 independent samples from 191 empirical articles plus 4 dissertations (1980s-2013), with cumulative N = 94,090.

## Key Constructs & Definitions

- **Subjective career success (SCS):** Individuals' perceptual evaluations of, and affective reactions to, their careers. Distinguished from objective career success (salary, promotions).
- **Affect-based vs. cognition-based SCS:** Affect-based = feelings/satisfaction with career success; cognition-based = beliefs about whether one is advancing as far/fast as desired. 86% of studies in the meta-analysis used affect-based measures.
- **Self-referent vs. other-referent SCS:** Self-referent = comparison to one's own aspirations/past achievements/expectations; other-referent = comparison to co-workers, supervisors, mentors, family. Most studies (and the meta-analysis results) use the self-referent form.
- **Career hurdles:** "Obstacles individuals face in the attainments of their career goals." Sources vary — some reside in the person (dispositional traits), some in the environment (low support) — but all reduce resources for successfully attaining career goals.
- **Conservation of resources (COR) theory:** Individuals are motivated to acquire and protect resources; they react negatively to resource losses and try to avoid them. Career hurdles = resource losses that can trigger worsening loss spirals.
- **Hindrance stress:** Stress (per LePine et al.) generated by career hurdles that shifts attention away from obtaining resources and dissipates time, energy, and positive emotions.

## Main Findings & Arguments

Six categories of hurdles were tested (corrected effect sizes r_c in Table 2):

- **Background-related hurdles (H1 — NOT supported):** Low socioeconomic origins, being female, being non-Caucasian, being married, employed spouse, having children, high number of children — all effect sizes negligible or near zero (e.g., being female -.00, non-Caucasian -.03).
- **Trait-related hurdles (H2 — fully supported):** Low emotional stability (-.34), low extraversion (-.24), low conscientiousness (-.16), low openness (-.17), low agreeableness (-.15), low core self-evaluation (-.53), external locus of control (-.40), low proactivity (-.29) — all negatively related to SCS.
- **Motivational hurdles (H3 — fully supported):** Job dissatisfaction (-.61), low organizational commitment (-.52), low occupational commitment (-.56), low work centrality (-.12), low job motivation (-.18), low job involvement (-.28), low work engagement (-.57).
- **Skill-related hurdles (H4 — NOT supported, one exception):** Low education level (-.06), weak university reputation (-.02), low GPA (-.02), lack of international experience (.02), few job position changes (.02), few employer changes (.03) all nonsignificant. Only low participation in training and development activities (-.23) was significant.
- **Social network hurdles (H5 — supported):** Poor LMX quality (-.33), low general supervisor support (-.58), low career-related supervisor support (-.55), low social integration at work (-.33), low social support at work (-.17), low social status/reputation (-.30), not having mentors (-.17), low number of mentors (-.10), low career/psychosocial/overall mentoring (-.30/-.27/-.35), low networking behavior (-.24), low political knowledge/behavior (-.37). Small network size (-.07) was the lone exception.
- **Organizational and job hurdles (H6 — fully supported):** Low general organizational support (-.46), low career-related organizational support (-.45), lack of promotion opportunities (-.52), low job importance (-.39), low job control (-.37), low job challenge (-.45), low skill utilization (-.42), role ambiguity (-.45), procedural unfairness (-.45), **unmet expectation (-.77 — strongest of all variables)**, job insecurity (-.29).

The four most consistently and strongly related hurdle sets are dispositional traits, motivation, social networks, and organizational/job context. **Unmet expectations had the strongest relationship with SCS of any variable studied**, corroborating that people judge success by what they have *not* achieved, not just what they have. 64% of cumulated studies post-dated the prior Ng et al. (2005) meta-analysis.

## Frameworks & Models

**Conservation of Resources theory applied to SCS:** Career hurdles → hindrance stress → (a) negative affective evaluations of career = lower SCS, and (b) depletion of resources (time, energy, positive emotions) → fewer career attainments. Burned-out individuals possess fewer resources, conserve energy by choosing less ambitious goals, and risk loss spirals.

**Six-category hurdle taxonomy:** Background-related, trait-related, motivational, skill-related, social network, organizational/job hurdles — distinguished by whether the obstacle originates in the person or the environment.

## Measurement

- SCS measured via self-reports as a continuous variable. Affect-based measures (86% of studies; average α = .83) ask satisfaction with career status/aspects. Cognition-based measures (14%; average α = .83) ask perceptions of how successful one has been at attaining career outcomes.
- Table 1 lists operationalizations of all ~50 correlates (e.g., role ambiguity = "perceptions of lack of clarity about sent roles"; unmet expectation = "perceptions that organizations have failed to meet their expectations"; job insecurity = "perceptions of potential loss of current jobs").
- Variables typically framed as positive drivers (education level) were reverse-scored so all indicate hurdles.
- Analysis used Raju, Burke, Normand & Langlois (1991) meta-analytic technique; results suppressed where k < 3.

## Practical Implications

- Organizations focus career development on "top talent"/"rising stars"; they should instead devote attention to employees with *low* SCS, because low SCS creates self-perpetuating, self-defeating cycles (Abele & Spurk: high SCS fuels confidence, motivation, goal-striving and later success).
- Managers should pay particular attention to employees' perceptions of their *current* jobs and organizations (job dissatisfaction, low engagement, low organizational commitment, lack of promotion opportunities).
- Supervisors have an inordinate influence on SCS; more research is needed on supervisors withholding resources.
- Because unmet expectations are the strongest negative correlate, helping employees with realistic expectations and identifying where psychological contracts were breached is critical. Resources firms offer (e.g., tuition reimbursement) often fail because they do not address what employees actually want.
- Tailored career development plans and targeted interventions are needed, or low-SCS individuals face worsening loss spirals.

## Notable Quotes

- "We conceptualize those hurdles here as obstacles individuals face in the attainments of their career goals."
- "Conservation of resources theory suggests that career hurdles create stress which simultaneously makes individuals feel worse about their career success while also making it more difficult for individuals to achieve their career goals."
- "Career hurdles lower SCS because they generate 'hindrance stress'... that is, career hurdles shift individuals' attention away from obtaining additional resources, dissipate their resources, and lead to fewer job/career attainments."
- "Counter to expectations, background-related hurdles (e.g., being female) and skill-related hurdles (e.g., lack of job changes and international experience) were not significantly related to SCS."
- "It is also important to note that, of all the variables studied here, unmet expectations had the strongest relationship with SCS."
- "Employees consider not only what they have achieved but also what they have not achieved when they formulate SCS."

## Connections

- **Success:** This is a foundational mapping of the negative-side predictors of subjective career success; complements predictor-focused reviews (Ng & Feldman 2005; Seibert, Akkermans & Liu 2024).
- **Shocks:** COR-framed resource loss spirals connect directly to the career shocks literature; unmet expectations and psychological contract breach are shock-adjacent.
- **Adaptability:** Low career adaptability (Zacher 2014) cited as predisposing individuals to lower SCS.
- **Transitions:** Job/employer changes and international experience (skill hurdles) are transition events — surprisingly unrelated to SCS here, possibly because accumulating skills is itself stressful.
- **Inaction/indecision:** Low proactivity and low job motivation as hurdles link to the inaction literature; passive individuals fail to capitalize on opportunities.
- **Impostorism:** Low core self-evaluation (-.53) — the strongest trait hurdle — overlaps conceptually with self-doubt and impostorism themes.
