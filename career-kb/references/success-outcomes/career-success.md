## Overview

A critical review by Seibert, Akkermans & Liu that synthesizes career success research across two parallel "invisible colleges" — organizational psychology (OP; rooted in vocational/work psychology, focused on internal processes and subjective outcomes) and organizational behavior (OB; rooted in management, economics, sociology, focused on workplace behaviors and objective outcomes). It reviews conceptualizations of objective and subjective career success, surveys seven categories of predictors, and proposes an integrative future research agenda. Written in the aftermath of COVID-19, "Great Resignation," and "Quiet Quitting."

## Key Constructs & Definitions

- **Career:** "The evolving sequence of a person's work experiences over time" (Arthur et al. 1989).
- **Career success:** "The positive material and psychological outcomes or achievements resulting from those work activities and experiences" (Judge et al. 1995).
- **Objective career success (OCS):** Outcomes that are quantitative and independently verifiable — occupational status, income, number/speed of promotions, professional title or organizational rank. Reflects an intersubjective standard; implicitly reflects social comparisons.
- **Subjective career success (SCS):** The individual's cognitive evaluation of and affective reaction to all aspects of one's career, including objective outcomes plus idiosyncratic personal values and meanings. Self-referential frame of reference; less influenced by peer comparison.
- **Human capital theory (HCT):** Investment in education/training/experience increases productivity, which the labor market rewards with higher earnings and status.
- **Internal labor market (ILM):** A bounded organization in which wages and promotions are governed by organizational rules and procedures (Doeringer & Piore 1971).
- **Contest vs. sponsored mobility:** Contest mobility = advancement based on merit/performance; sponsored mobility = individuals selected early for elite status and groomed (Turner 1960).
- **Proactive personality / proactive behavior:** Stable disposition (and associated behaviors) shown to predict career success beyond broad individual and occupational variables.
- **Career adaptability:** A psychosocial resource for dealing with career-related challenges; four resources — concern, control, curiosity, confidence (Savickas & Porfeli 2012).
- **Employability:** One's ability to realize job opportunities within and between employers; includes a proactive (preparing for change) and reactive (adapting to change) component.
- **Political skill:** "The ability to effectively understand others at work, and to use such knowledge to influence others to act in ways that enhance one's personal and/or organizational objectives" (Ferris et al. 2005) — four dimensions: apparent sincerity, social astuteness, interpersonal influence, networking ability.
- **Career sustainability:** A dynamic balance of happiness, health, and productivity over the life course (De Vos et al. 2020).
- **Career shocks:** Disruptive events, positive or negative, that trigger deliberation about one's career behavior or career success (Seibert et al. 2013; Akkermans et al. 2018).

## Main Findings & Arguments

**Conceptualization debate.** Since the 1990s, "new careers" scholars argued OCS would become less relevant and SCS more central. The authors push back: OCS remains important and should *not* be abandoned — its objectivity avoids common-method-variance inflation, financial security still matters (especially for WEIRD-sample critiques and marginalized groups), and de-emphasizing OCS may be rhetorical rather than empirical. They recommend including *both* OCS and SCS unless there is a strong theoretical reason not to. Arthur et al. (2005): 65% of studies included both; Spurk et al. (2019): less than half did — inclusion of both may be declining.

**SCS construct expansion.** Greenhaus et al.'s (1990) career satisfaction scale remains the most popular SCS measure. Newer multidimensional views (Shockley et al. 2016; the 5C/Briscoe et al. 2021 scale) identify eight emerging SCS dimensions but have seen little empirical uptake. Concern: the new fine-grained SCS dimensions overlap with existing OB constructs (job satisfaction, work-family balance, intrinsic job characteristics) — risk of construct contamination and weak incremental validity.

**Seven predictor categories** (Section 4):
1. **Human capital** — most-used theory pre-2000; correlates with salary, weak/nonsignificant effects on promotions and career satisfaction; criticized as a "loose theoretical catch-all."
2. **Internal & external labor markets** — tournament theory (Rosenbaum 1979): early-career success raises probability of later promotions (path dependence); ILM vs. external moves involve trade-offs (internal moves → more managerial responsibility, higher satisfaction; external moves → higher pay).
3. **Sponsorship, developmental support, developmental networks** — organizational support and high-quality LMX positively related to OCS and SCS; mentoring has small effects on OCS, small-to-moderate on SCS; developmental networks spanning diverse social worlds and structural holes matter more than sheer amount of support.
4. **Gender and women's career equality** — three perspectives: career preference (opting-out), work-family conflict, gender bias (pushed-out). Gender gaps in salary/promotions larger than in performance evaluations; the authors favor "career equality" over "career success" framing here.
5. **Stable individual differences** — Big Five: extraversion/conscientiousness/(low) neuroticism → higher salary, promotions, satisfaction; agreeableness positive for satisfaction but negative for salary/promotions; Big Five effects larger on SCS than OCS. Proactive personality, core self-evaluations, general mental ability (GMA), and emotional intelligence (EQ) all predict career success.
6. **Career self-management (CSM) behaviors** — planning, goal setting, skill development, feedback seeking, networking, self-nomination, job mobility; empirical evidence inconsistent — no conclusive CSM-to-career-success relationship.
7. **Malleable individual differences** — protean/boundaryless career attitudes (self-directed dimension reliably related to SCS, weak ties to OCS); career competencies and resources frameworks (career adaptability, employability, political skill, Career Competencies Questionnaire, Career Resources Framework) — compelling but plagued by "jingle-jangle" construct proliferation and overlap.

## Measurement

- OCS: company archival data / objective ratings (occupational status), panel datasets (job title), or self-reports (salary).
- SCS: most studies use Greenhaus et al. (1990) unidimensional career satisfaction scale; multidimensional alternatives (Shockley et al. 2016; Briscoe et al. 2021 5C scale; Seibert et al. 2013) exist but are underused empirically.
- Recommendation: fine-grained multidimensional SCS measures for specific predictors/interventions; short unidimensional/global SCS measures (Greenhaus et al. 1990) when SCS is part of a broader model or predicting broad outcomes like turnover. Consensus is needed on how to use the different SCS measurement approaches.

## Practical Implications

Future research agenda (Table 3):
- **Career sustainability** — adopt a temporal perspective; balance happiness, health, productivity over the life course; reconciles OP (happiness-focused) and OB (productivity-focused) research.
- **Career shocks** — integrate event-based disruptions into career success models; clarify event characteristics, theoretical mechanisms, and which shocks relate to which OCS/SCS facets.
- **Socially marginalized and underrepresented groups** — study career success of women, racial/ethnic minorities, people with disabilities, displaced immigrants through a sustainable-career lens; apply identity theory and stigma theory; examine intersectionality.
- **Alternative employment/work arrangements** — self-employment, gig work, contract/temporary/platform work understudied; examine how flexibility dimensions (employment relationship, scheduling, location) affect career success.
- **Theoretical clean-up** — Spurk et al. (2019) found 44 theories used; only 8.6% of studies compared theories. Prune redundant/overlapping constructs ("jingle-jangle"), test untested mechanisms, conduct comparative theory testing.

## Notable Quotes

- "Career success is a social construction rather than an objective reality, embedded in understanding a particular historical time and place, and therefore there are multiple and dynamically changing perspectives on the construct."
- "OCS remains relevant and important. The solution to the limitations of OCS is not to abandon this outcome but, rather, to broaden our assessment of the career success domain such that it includes both OCS and SCS."
- "The emphasis on personal meaning in one's career may be a luxury of the WEIRD (Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, and Democratic) samples often the focus of career research."
- "There is no conclusive evidence relating CSM to career success."
- "A career is sustainable to the extent that it allows the individual to be happy, healthy, and productive over the life course."
- "Let's move beyond disciplinary silos and learn from each other's expertise and knowledge to advance career success research even further."

## Connections

- **Success:** The definitive contemporary mapping of OCS/SCS conceptualization and predictors; directly extends and frames Ng & Feldman (2014) and Ng et al. (2005) meta-analyses.
- **Shocks:** Dedicates an entire future-research section to career shocks (Seibert et al. 2013; Akkermans et al. 2018) as disruptive triggers of career deliberation.
- **Adaptability:** Career adaptability (Savickas & Porfeli 2012) and employability treated as key malleable predictors of both OCS and SCS.
- **Transitions:** ILM/external labor market moves, internal promotions, and the unfolding model of turnover connect to the transitions literature.
- **Impostorism:** Core self-evaluations, self-efficacy, and self-concept threaded through the personality predictor discussion.
- **Inaction/indecision:** Career self-management behaviors and proactive personality bear on action vs. passivity in shaping one's career.
