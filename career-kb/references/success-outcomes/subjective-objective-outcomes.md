## Overview

This is a systematic review (Journal of Management, Vol. 45 No. 1, 2019, pp. 35-69) of quantitative empirical research on the antecedents AND outcomes of career success, distinguishing objective career success (OCS) from subjective career success (SCS). It addresses two "competing perspectives" in the literature: (a) the range and prevalence of theoretical approaches used to explain career success, and (b) whether OCS and SCS require theoretically differentiated explanations. The review's distinctive contribution is challenging the assumption that OCS and SCS are only *ultimate* outcomes of careers — proposing instead that career success also acts as an *antecedent* to other career and life outcomes. The authors organize everything within an integrative resource management framework derived from conservation of resources (COR) theory. The review covers 244 publications (266 individual studies) located via Web of Science search on "career success" (1900-2016).

## Key Constructs & Definitions

- **Objective career success (OCS)** — career outcomes that are directly observable by others and measurable in a standardized way; a person's career weighed against societal norms concerning salary, job level, promotion history, or occupational prestige.
- **Subjective career success (SCS)** — the focal career actor's evaluation and experience of achieving personally meaningful career outcomes; typically measured as career satisfaction, perceived career success, or (more recently) a multidimensional evaluation of career facets (growth and development, personal life, authenticity — Shockley et al., 2016).
- **Resources (COR)** — any entity that helps people obtain personally valued objects or states. Resources exist at personal and contextual levels.
- **Personal resources** — more volatile individual resources: knowledge, experience, awareness.
- **Proximal environmental resources** — supervisor support, organizational policies, employment type.
- **Personal key resources** — more structural individual resources: self-esteem, optimism, stable personality traits.
- **Contextual macroresources** — culture, labor market, social welfare systems.
- **Resource caravans / resource gain spirals / resource loss cycles** — COR principle that resources generate other resources and accumulate into outcomes over time.
- **Resource management behaviors and attitudes** — actions/attitudes by which people develop career success: political behavior, self-directed career attitude, career planning.

## Main Findings & Arguments

**OCS-SCS correlation:** "The correlation between OCS and SCS reported in meta-analytical reviews has typically been small to moderate, ranging from .22 to .30 (Ng et al., 2005) — with even smaller or nonsignificant correlations reported between indicators of OCS and specific SCS facets (Shockley et al., 2016)." OCS and SCS are nomologically different facets of career success.

**Review corpus:** 592 Web of Science hits → final 244 publications (266 studies). Growth over time: 37 studies (13.9%) 1973-1999; 94 (35.3%) 2000-2009; 135 (50.8%) 2010-2016. 88% include explicit hypotheses. Studies measuring career success: OCS only 71 (26.7%), SCS only 75 (28.2%), both 120 (45.1%). 85.7% study only antecedents; just 2.6% study only outcomes; 11.7% study both. 65.8% cross-sectional, 34.2% longitudinal.

**Theoretical heterogeneity:** Across 259 antecedent studies, 559 theoretical approaches were coded into 14 categories. Past research drew on an even wider and more heterogeneous range of theories than prior reviews assumed. Dominant approaches: social environment (34.0%; e.g., social capital theory), career agency (27.4%; e.g., social cognitive career theory), human capital (21.2%), roles and identity (16.6%). Personal key resources, contextual macroresources, hybrid and single-occurrence theories were each ≤12.0%.

**Time trends:** In the current decade, frequently used approaches are career agency (31.8%), social environment (36.4%), person-environment interactions and human capital (both 16.7%). Before 2000, the most cited were human capital (40.0%), roles and identity (34.3%), social and work environment (both 28.6%). Implies declining interest in human capital and growing interest in career agency.

**Differentiation of OCS vs SCS antecedents:** Among studies citing personal-resources approaches, 40.4% looked at OCS only vs 16.3% SCS only — strongest for human capital (38.1% OCS only vs 7.3% SCS only) and competitive performance approaches (41.4% OCS only vs 13.8% SCS only). Roles/identity approaches showed less difference (37.2% OCS only vs 27.9% SCS only). Resource management behaviors/attitudes more often examined SCS only (12.2% OCS only vs 36.6% SCS only); personal key resources likewise (12.9% OCS only vs 25.8% SCS only). Proximal environmental resource approaches did NOT show a clear OCS/SCS preference (33.0% vs 24.8%), consistent with being suited to both.

**Differential hypotheses within studies:** Of the 120 studies measuring both OCS and SCS, only 22 (18.3%) explicitly stated differential hypotheses. Examples: Hennekam (2016) found integrity predicted SCS but not OCS; Wayne et al. (1999) found desire for upward mobility positively related to OCS but negatively to SCS; Van den Born & Witteloostuijn (2013) found flexibility and work-life balance motivations negatively related to OCS but positively to SCS.

**Comparative variance findings:** Johnson & Eby (2011, African American males) — human capital accounted for 39% of explained variance in OCS, sociodemographic attributes 34%, individual differences 14%, social capital 13%. Van den Born & Witteloostuijn (2013, freelancers) — personal capital (36%), business strategies (26%), social capital (21%) explained the largest proportions of SCS variance.

**Outcomes of career success:** Only 38 studies (14.3%) examined outcomes; the review focused on 25 where career-success outcomes were not themselves measures of career success. An increasing trend since 2008 (2 studies before 2008, 23 from 2008 onward). Withdrawal (40.0%) and career attitudes (36.0%) most studied, then well-being/health (24.0%) and work-environment reactions (16.0%); self-concept outcomes (8.0%) added inductively. Only 5 studies (1.9%) tested reciprocal relations between career success and another outcome.

- **Withdrawal:** Stumpf (2014) — both OCS and SCS predicted lower career mobility 12 months later; SCS effects survived but salary effects became nonsignificant when SCS entered. Pachulicz et al. (2008, emergency physicians) — both OCS (salary change) and SCS predicted actual retirement and intentions to leave. SCS is a more proximal predictor.
- **Career attitudes:** SCS studied more than OCS. Gao-Urhahn et al. (2016) — salary change positively affected organizational commitment change (reflection theory of compensation, social identity theory). Spurk & Abele (2014) — OCS positively affected occupational self-efficacy expectations over time, mediated by more proximal SCS evaluations. Praskova et al. (2014) — weak effects of SCS on career calling.
- **Well-being and health:** Judge & Kammeyer-Mueller (2012) — individuals in prestigious occupations (1940-1960) had higher life satisfaction up to 32 years later and lower mortality up to 42 years later; income showed no mortality effect. Judge, Ilias, & Dimotakis (2010) — prestigious occupations linked to better objective and subjective health and higher economic and subjective well-being 3-9 years later. No study has examined SCS effects on well-being/health changes longitudinally.
- **Reactions from the (work) environment:** Modeled as outcomes of OCS only. Stumpf & Tymon (2012) — past promotions/salary changes positively affected managerial assessments of a career actor's human capital value. Negative effects also found: Westman & Etzion (1990, vignette) — career success associated with personal failure attributions and long working hours; Heilman et al. (2004) — objectively successful women judged less likable and more interpersonally hostile, especially in male-dominated occupations.
- **Self-concept:** Studied only as outcomes of OCS. Kammeyer-Mueller, Judge, & Piccolo (2008) — assumed but did NOT support that high income predicts self-regard. Stumpf & Tymon (2012, cross-sectional) — number of promotions affected core self-evaluations.

**Dark-side / negative outcomes:** COR theory predicts that the pursuit and protection of career success drains other resources, producing work-family conflict, exhaustion, and failure in other life domains; career success can be "a double-edged sword." OCS, being visible to third parties, is expected to more strongly affect external variables (mentoring, interpersonal competition, peer envy, work-nonwork demands); SCS expected to more strongly affect internal processes (work motivation, identity change, well-being, lower turnover intentions). Hypothesis 4 expectation: OCS, compared to SCS, is more likely to lead to negative, undesirable outcomes.

## Frameworks & Models

**Resource management framework (COR-based)** — organizes both antecedents and outcomes. Three COR assumptions applied: (1) resources are critical to attaining valued aims and exist at personal/contextual levels; (2) resources protect against resource loss; (3) resources generate other resources (caravans, gain spirals, loss cycles, accumulation/dynamics over time).

**14-category taxonomy of theoretical approaches** (sorted into superordinate resource-management categories):
- *Personal resources:* 1. Human capital (k=104, 40.2%); 2. Roles and identity (k=43, 16.6%); 3. (Competitive) performance (k=29, 11.2%).
- *Proximal environmental resources:* 4. Social environment (k=109, 42.1%); 5. Work environment (k=29, 11.2%).
- *Resource management behaviors and attitudes:* 6. Career agency (k=82, 31.7%); 7. Stress and coping (k=13, 5.0%).
- *Personal key resources:* 8. Stable traits (k=31, 12.0%).
- *Contextual macroresources:* 9. National culture (k=8, 3.1%).
- *Resource accumulation and dynamics:* 10. Person-environment interactions (k=35, 13.5%); 11. Life span (k=14, 5.4%); 12. Career transitions (k=9, 3.5%).
- *Other:* 13. Hybrid (k=23, 8.9%); 14. Single occurrences (k=9, 3.5%).

**Five-category taxonomy of career-success outcomes:** (a) Withdrawal — cognitions/behaviors directed toward leaving the job/organization/occupation; (b) Career attitudes — individual attitudes related to the work and career domain (e.g., career self-efficacy, career calling); (c) Well-being and health — objective or subjective indicators of physical/mental well-being; (d) Reactions from the (work) environment — reactions of others in the work/nonwork domain; (e) Self-concept — generalized self-perceptions about personal characteristics/attributes (core self-esteem, core self-evaluations).

**Theoretical alignment expectations:** Human capital theory (Becker, 1962), tournament theory, contest mobility better aligned with OCS (external awarding of success). Calling model of psychological success (Hall & Chandler, 2005), kaleidoscope career model, protean career model, stable traits, stress experiences, cognitive mechanisms better suited to SCS (internal evaluation). Sponsored mobility, mentoring/social capital, role and identity theories, mobility theories, COR, broaden-and-build appropriate for both.

## Measurement

- **OCS** operationalized via objective, verifiable, standardized indicators: salary, job level, promotion history/number of promotions, occupational prestige/status. Visible to third parties by definition.
- **SCS** operationalized as career satisfaction (Greenhaus, Parasuraman, & Wormley, 1990 scale), perceived career success (Heslin, 2003; Turban & Dougherty, 1994), or multidimensional facet evaluations (Shockley et al., 2016 — growth and development, personal life, authenticity).
- **Methodological note:** the review prioritizes studies using designs that allow stronger causal inference (longitudinal designs, change analysis, cross-lagged panel designs, experimental/vignette designs) to distinguish antecedents from outcomes. Studies that violated the career-success construct (e.g., using educational level or organizational commitment as success indicators) were excluded.

## Practical Implications

- Researchers should explicitly dissect the theoretical assumptions underlying the approaches they use, and develop clear, concise operationalization standards for antecedent and outcome variables so future career-success research aligns more deliberately with prior work.
- Future research should design studies that explicitly state differential hypotheses for OCS vs SCS, separating theoretical rationale and including different antecedent (and outcome) variables for each.
- Career counseling and HR management could eventually translate differential mediation findings into "custom-fit" recommendations for achieving and dealing with OCS and SCS (e.g., Converse et al., 2012 — self-control and proactive personality affected salary/prestige via educational attainment, but affected career satisfaction via achievement opportunities).
- Practitioners should be aware of the "dark side": pursuing/protecting career success drains resources and may produce work-family conflict, exhaustion, interpersonal hostility from others, peer envy — and OCS, being visible, is more exposed to these negative environmental reactions.
- Boundary conditions matter: macrocontext (public vs private sector, entrepreneurship) and competitive climate define when OCS and SCS coincide or diverge.

## Notable Quotes

> "The existing literature has typically conceptualized OCS and/or SCS as the ultimate outcome of a career. However, some variables that are typically treated as antecedents of career success might just as well be outcomes (Ng & Feldman, 2014a)."

> "As such, attaining and sustaining career success becomes a double-edged sword where career success is accompanied by the depletion of valued other resources, leading to, for example, work–family conflict ... exhaustion, or experienced failure in other life domains."

## Connections

- **Subjective vs objective career success** — directly extends and complements Heslin's (2005) conceptualization paper; both treat the OCS/SCS distinction as central and under-theorized.
- **Shocks** — career shocks (Seibert, Kraimer, Holtom, & Pierotti, 2013) cited as a future-research stressor affecting career success; COR loss cycles connect to shock literature.
- **Adaptability** — career adaptability (Zacher, 2015a) explains daily within-person variance in career satisfaction; the career adaptation model (Zhou et al., 2016) links success criteria and locus of control to adaptive readiness.
- **Transitions** — career transitions modeled as both antecedents and outcomes; career-transition frameworks (Andresen & Biemann, 2013) flagged as underused.
- **Success** — the core construct; provides the most rigorous taxonomy of how success has been theorized and measured.
- **Impostorism / self-concept** — self-concept outcomes (core self-evaluations, self-esteem) link to impostorism literature; OCS predicts changes in neuroticism/agreeableness (Sutin et al., 2009).
- **Inaction / indecision** — withdrawal outcomes (turnover intentions, retirement, career mobility) and the COR view of resource investment connect to literatures on career inaction and decision-making.
