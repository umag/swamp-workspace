## Overview

Meryl Reis Louis's foundational theoretical paper (Academy of Management Review, 1980) developing an understanding of career transitions as a *general* phenomenon rather than studying particular transition types (retirement, first job) in isolation. It has three parts: (1) working definitions, five basic propositions, and features common to all transitions; (2) a typology of nine transition varieties; (3) a framework — the sense-making process — for understanding how individuals cope with transition experiences. This article is the primary source for the modern definition and typology of career transitions.

## Key Constructs & Definitions

- **Career:** "An accumulation of role-related experiences over time" (following Hall 1976). May refer to a work or nonwork role; does not necessarily imply hierarchical/professional progression. Includes the steady-state career and the spiral career, not just the linear/hierarchical career.
- **Career transition (CT):** "The period during which an individual is either changing roles (taking on a different objective role) or changing orientation to a role already held (altering a subjective state)."
- **Transition period:** Analogous to the *encounter period* in organizational socialization — extends from entry into the new situation/role until substantial adjustment and acceptance is reached. Duration depends on the extent of difference between old and new roles/orientations.
- **Changes:** Objective differences between old and new role/setting that are publicly noticeable and knowable, often knowable in advance (e.g., title, organizational affiliation, office address, co-workers, supervisor).
- **Contrasts:** Subjective differences — perceptual products of the individual's experience in the new setting; person-specific figures against the background of a total field; NOT generally knowable in advance. Two people undergoing the same objective change experience different contrasts.
- **Surprises:** Differences between the individual's anticipations (tacit and explicit) of the new situation and subsequent real-time experiences. May be positive or negative; result from undermet or overmet anticipations about the job, organization, or oneself. Trigger both affective and cognitive reactions.
- **Anticipation:** Conscious and unconscious expectations developed to replace voids in first-hand knowledge; serve as belief-like guides for selecting behaviors and interpreting events.
- **Role orientation:** The individual's subjective state / map of a role; intrarole transitions involve changing this without the formal role changing.
- **Sense-making:** The essential coping process in CTs — revising the cognitive maps used to interpret and describe experiences in the new role, in light of newly gained and previously unavailable experiences.

## Main Findings & Arguments

**Five basic propositions:**
- P1: During all CTs, individuals face differences between old and new roles, role orientations, and role settings.
- P2: The more elements that differ in the new role/situation, and the more they differ from previous roles, the more the transitioner potentially has to cope with.
- P3: The type of transition undertaken is an indicator of the general nature and magnitude of differences to be coped with.
- P4: A typical coping process exists by which individuals interpret and respond to differences experienced during transitions of all types.
- P5: Understanding the coping process combined with analyzing the type of transition can be used to foresee needs and facilitate adaptation.

An **inverse relationship between anticipatability and objective difference** is assumed: the greater the objective newness, the greater the *subjective* newness, because of the difficulty of accurately anticipating one's own reactions to a novel situation.

**Why transitioners have special sense-making needs:** Insiders (organizational members not in transition) normally know what to expect, so little surprises them; when surprises do arise, insiders have sufficient history to interpret them and other insiders to test interpretations with. Transitioners — particularly interrole transitioners — lack accurate context-specific interpretation schemes, lack a history in the setting, and often lack relationships with insiders who could serve as sounding boards. They therefore attach meanings using interpretation schemes from *prior* roles/settings, producing inappropriate or dysfunctional interpretations (e.g., attributing a denied raise to "the boss is always like that" — a stable cause — rather than "the boss is going through a rough period" — a temporary cause).

## Frameworks & Models

**The typology — nine varieties of career transitions** (Table 1):

*Interrole transitions* (a new and different role is actually taken; discussed in chronological sequence):
1. **Entry/Re-entry** — entering or re-entering a labor pool (college graduate's first job; housewife returning to work; marrying into a spouse role).
2. **Intracompany** — change of role within the same company (department/division move; technical-to-managerial shift).
3. **Intercompany** — moving from one organization to another (traditionally "labor turnover").
4. **Interprofession** — change in profession (dentist takes up law; corporate employee becomes entrepreneur; military officer enters business). Entails a move to a "foreign culture" — differences in language, interaction norms, code of ethics, reference group, professional self-identity.
5. **Exit** — leaving a labor pool. Varieties: leave of absence (brief, planned, finite); withdrawal (long-term/semipermanent, transitioner-initiated, indeterminate); involuntary unemployment (organization-initiated, unanticipated, unplanned); retirement (planned and permanent).

*Intrarole transitions* (a new orientation to a role already held; may occur without conscious awareness):
1. **Intrarole adjustment** — adjustment in orientation to a role in response to experiences over time (e.g., MBA graduates' complacency replacing enthusiasm as they meet corporate realities).
2. **Extrarole adjustment** — a change in one life role leads to adjustment in orientation toward another (e.g., accepting a journal editorship reduces time/energy for teaching, research, family; first baby of a dual-career couple).
3. **Role/career-stage transition** — major passage through a stage in the total career cycle; the formal role need not change; changes are diffuse, pervasive, gradual, less conscious.
4. **Life-stage transition** — passage through normal stages of human development (Erikson's psychosocial stages — e.g., generativity vs. stagnation; the "mid-life crisis").

Note: any particular transition may contain elements of several types (e.g., a technical-to-managerial shift could be an intracompany transition, an interprofession transition, and a career-stage transition simultaneously).

**The sense-making process model** (Figure 1): Experiences in old role + career transition → experiences in new role and anticipations of new role → **differences (changes, contrasts, surprises)** → fed into **SENSE-MAKING** (along with the personal cognitive map and other inputs: past experiences, personality and purpose, others' interpretations, local interpretation schemes) → **attribute meaning to differences** → branches to (a) select behavioral response and (b) revise map and anticipations (feedback loop). Discrepancy between anticipation and experience creates a tension state — a "quasi-need" (Lewin) for return to equilibrium — that triggers sense-making.

## Measurement

This is a purely theoretical/conceptual paper — no empirical measurement, scales, or data. It offers a conceptual scheme and typology intended to be used to "predict, analyze, and facilitate the experiences of individual transitioners" and to "detect theoretical and empirical gaps."

## Practical Implications

To understand, classify, and facilitate specific career transitions, examine five areas:
1. Differences between pre- and post-transition roles and settings.
2. The current and historical organizational contexts in which the transitioner's roles are embedded.
3. The transitioner's larger lifespace, including relationships among extraorganizational roles and work roles.
4. The transitioner's present life stage and relevant events from earlier psychosocial development.
5. The transitioner's current career stage, career development history, and the relationship between current career and life stages.

Transitioners need three things: (1) accurate historical and broad contextual information about the setting; (2) help acquiring setting- and role-specific interpretation schemes; (3) relationships with insiders who will serve as sounding boards for reality testing. Insiders can be a rich source of assistance — providing background information, sharing interpretations, and facilitating acquisition of local "meaning dictionaries."

## Connections

- **Transitions:** This is THE foundational career transition theory document — the source of the standard definition and the nine-type typology that the "Career transition outcomes" review (Mussagulova et al. 2023) in this same cluster is built on and calls to update.
- **Shocks:** Surprises — gaps between anticipation and experience — are a direct theoretical ancestor of the career shocks construct (Seibert et al. 2013; Akkermans et al. 2018). Involuntary unemployment as an unanticipated, unplanned exit is a paradigmatic negative shock.
- **Adaptability:** The sense-making coping process and the encounter/adjustment period prefigure career adaptability and adjustment-perspective research; revising cognitive maps = adaptive response.
- **Success:** The subjective/objective distinction in roles (changes vs. contrasts) maps onto the objective/subjective career success dichotomy.
- **Indecision/inaction:** Anticipation as belief-like guidance, and the disconfirmation that creates tension, connect to decision-making and the "non-event" transitions discussed in later literature.
- **Impostorism:** Transitioners' lack of context-specific interpretation schemes and reliance on prior-role maps — feeling out of place in a new setting — overlaps with impostor experiences during role entry.
