## Overview

An original-research article in Frontiers in Psychology (2016) investigating how impostor feelings function within the career-development domain — an area the authors note is empirically sparse. They propose and empirically evaluate a model of both the *preconditions* of IP (fear of failure, fear of success, low self-esteem) and its *career-relevant consequences* (career planning, career striving, motivation to lead). Two independent samples at different career stages: Study 1, university students (N=212, 70% female, mean age 23.23) in a European university laboratory study; Study 2, working professionals (N=110, 50% female, mean age 33.35) at an international airport in southern Germany (sales and security), via online study. The framing question: do impostors have very clear career plans to handle their insecurity, or do impostor fears hold them back from striving for higher positions and power?

## Key Constructs & Definitions

- **Impostor phenomenon (IP):** "an internal experience of intellectual and professional incapability despite objective evidence to the contrary." Sufferers believe their success is due to luck or error and live in constant fear of being unmasked as unintelligent or less capable. An integrative concept comprising different cognitive features. May function as a "career theme" in career construction theory — "a controlling implicit idea or perspective that imposes personal meaning on past memories, present experiences and future aspirations."
- **Fear of failure:** "a tendency to appraise threat and feel anxious in situations that involve the possibility of failing" (Conroy et al.). Found to be one of the best predictors of IP (alongside self-handicapping); fits the impostor profile of high neuroticism and low conscientiousness.
- **Fear of success:** fear of losing connection to others (e.g., work colleagues) when one's success is noticed; rooted in the belief that being successful results in dislike, resentment, and loss of affection/approval. To handle it, impostors try to deny their success or self-handicap. Fear of success relates to negative consequences anticipated following social rejection — and may strengthen a motivation to *fail* to protect the social support network.
- **Low self-esteem:** in impostors, a missing internal attribution of successful achievements impairs the development of high self-esteem; consistently negatively correlated with IP yet conceptually and empirically distinct from it.
- **Career planning:** future-oriented thinking and imagining possible pathways to achieve career goals; includes proactive designing of career plans; one of the most influential aspects of successful career development.
- **Career striving:** proactive career behavior to climb the career ladder. Split into **nonobservable (NO) career striving** (striving when no one is watching — e.g., privately taking a job-information sheet) and **observable (O) career striving** (striving visible to others — e.g., taking a flyer in full view of an experimenter). Impostors are predicted to show *lower* NO striving but *higher* O striving (to maintain a high-performer image / impression management).
- **Motivation to lead:** "an individual differences construct that affects a leader's or leader-to-be's decision to assume leadership training, roles, and responsibilities and that affect[s] his or her intensity of effort at leading and persistence as a leader" (Chan & Drasgow). People high in it see themselves as having leadership ability and value competition/achievement.

## Main Findings & Arguments

- **Hypotheses:** H1a — fear of failure positively related to IP; H1b — fear of success positively related to IP; H1c — self-esteem negatively related to IP; H2a — IP negatively related to career planning; H2b — IP negatively related to motivation to lead; H2c — IP negatively related to (nonobservable) career striving; H2d — IP positively related to observable career striving.
- **Study 1 (students):** all three preconditions correlated highly with impostor feelings. Fear of failure and fear of success were highly positively correlated with IP; self-esteem negatively (ps < .001). Significant relations of IP with career planning (p=.001) and motivation to lead (p=.005). The three preconditions together explained **63%** of the variance in impostor feelings. IP significantly predicted three of four career-development aspects: less career planning (p<.001, H2a), less motivation to lead (p=.004, H2b). H2c (NO career striving) only marginal (p=.083). H2d supported — more impostor feelings → higher observable career striving (p=.046). **Low self-esteem was the strongest precondition** for students, followed by fear of failure and fear of success. The model fitting students best included preconditions + career planning.
- **Study 2 (working professionals):** impostor feelings correlated highly with all three preconditions. Fear of success was the strongest predictor this time, followed by fear of failure; self-esteem negatively correlated (ps < .001). The three preconditions explained **71%** of the variance in IP. More impostor feelings → less career planning, less career striving, and less motivation to lead (all ps < .001). The model fitting workers best included preconditions + motivation to lead, followed by + career striving.
- **Stage-dependent pattern:** IP is relevant to career development in *different ways at different career stages* — low self-esteem dominates as a precondition for students; fear of success dominates for working professionals (who are more aware of and affected by colleagues' judgments, and face real competition for scarce promotions).
- **Career planning most impaired in students:** the more impostor feelings, the less clear plans/strategies — alarming because planning may be the most important issue students face in current career development.
- **Full model never fit well:** path models including all preconditions *and* all consequences did not show acceptable fit; separated models (preconditions + one consequence each) fit better.
- **Mechanism:** as people with impostor feelings are not aware of their own competences, they cannot form a clear vision of their future career; they act on the slogan "never change a running system," avoiding promotion out of fear of failing publicly and of social rejection — effectively self-handicapping their career path.

## Frameworks & Models

**Hypothesized model (Figure 1):**

`Preconditions → Impostor feelings → Career-relevant consequences`

- **Preconditions:** Fear of failure, Fear of success, Low self-esteem.
- **Impostor feelings:** the central mediating construct.
- **Career-relevant consequences:** Career planning, Career striving (nonobservable + observable), Motivation to lead.

**Career construction theory (Savickas) framing:**
- A **developmental maxicycle** of career stages: growth, exploration, establishment, management, disengagement — each with its own developmental tasks.
- A **minicycle** supplements the maxicycle: stages such as exploration recur whenever a person transitions between career stages or is destabilized by socioeconomic/personal events.
- Students are in the **exploration** stage (mainly 14–24-year-olds): the task is acquiring occupational information to make matching choices — so career *planning* is the salient task.
- Working professionals are in the **establishment** stage (typically 25–44-year-olds): they think about untapped leadership potential and new responsibilities — so *motivation to lead* and career striving are the salient consequences.
- IP is theorized as a **career theme** — an integrative implicit perspective imposing meaning on past, present, and future career experiences.

Directional logic (why IP causes, not just correlates with, outcomes): career planning is more directly tied to concrete actions and predicts objective career success, whereas IP is more closely linked to enduring constructs like depression and general anxiety.

## Measurement

- **Impostor feelings:** German translation of the Clance Impostor Phenomenon Scale (CIPS; Clance, 1986; Klinkhammer & Saul-Sopron, 2009); 20 items (e.g., "I can give the impression that I'm more competent than I really am"); 5-point scale; α = .88 (Study 1), .92 (Study 2).
- **Fear of failure and fear of success:** 4 of 24 vignettes from the GEHFEM (Bauer et al.; Peters-Häderle), describing true-to-life work-context scenarios (e.g., being proposed for a promotion despite insecurity about requirements). Two items each for fear of failure ("I hope I do not fail to have the necessary requirements") and fear of success ("Will my colleagues reject me if I climb the career ladder?").
- **Self-esteem:** German version of the Rosenberg Self-Esteem Scale (10 items; Study 1) and the Single-Item Self-Esteem Scale (SISE; "I have high self-esteem," 7-point; Study 2).
- **Career planning:** German version of Gould's Career Management Scale; 6 of 9 items (e.g., "I have a strategy to achieve my career goals").
- **Nonobservable (NO) career striving (Study 1):** participants viewed a pyramid of internship career levels (1=Staff to 5=Management) with task/responsibility descriptions; rated which level they would like; a private (unobserved) choice.
- **Observable (O) career striving (Study 1):** fictitious flyers showing the pyramid, five stacks each highlighting one career level (1=Staff to 5=Management), placed on a table by the lab entrance; the flyer level a participant took (in full view of the investigator) was recorded; 0 if none taken, highest number if multiple taken.
- **Career striving (Study 2):** a pyramid matching the company's actual organizational levels (1=Staff to 5=Top Management); participants chose the level they desired to reach in 2 and in 5 years; the 5-year score was used.
- **Motivation to lead:** Study 1 — Hamburg Motivation to Lead Inventory (HMLI; Felfe et al.) plus a direct-request item ("I feel confident taking a leadership position"); Study 2 — the direct-request item only (validated against full HMLI, r=.62, p<.001).
- **Analysis:** path modeling in AMOS; fit indices CFI, GFI, RMSEA, SRMR (good fit: GFI/CFI ≥ .90, RMSEA/SRMR ≤ .08).

## Practical Implications

- IP is an integrative motivational construct that forms an inner barrier to a successful career-development process — useful for career counselors and HR/organizational development.
- The three preconditions form a dispositional risk profile for IP; interventions should target them — coaching programs focused on **reducing fear of failure and fear of success** and **enhancing self-esteem**.
- Help clients learn to deal with fears of failure and to **attribute successes to their own abilities** (to boost self-esteem).
- Because fear of success is associated with fear of rejection by colleagues, and social support buffers IP's negative effects, provide **networking programs or supervision/support groups** where employees can share experiences.
- Incorporate the IP topic into employee support measures to reduce impostor feelings and their negative career-development effects.
- IP can be approached in career counseling by including it in a Career Construction Interview / Career Story Interview as a "career theme."
- For students, career planning is most affected — counselors should help impostor students build clear career strategies. For professionals, motivation to lead and striving are most affected — relevant to succession planning, as promising candidates may be lost from the leadership pipeline.

## Connections

- **Impostorism:** a primary study cataloged in the Bravata et al. systematic review (impostor-one); shares the CIPS instrument and the construct definition.
- **Inaction / indecision:** the core link — IP reduces career planning and (nonobservable) career striving; impostors avoid acting on career advancement ("never change a running system"), a direct manifestation of career inaction driven by an internal state.
- **Success / outcomes:** positions IP as an *inner barrier* between ability and objective career success — high-potential people are "lost" in the development process because impostor feelings block planning, striving, and leadership pursuit.
- **Transitions:** explicitly built on career construction theory's maxicycle/minicycle — IP operates differently at the exploration (student) vs. establishment (professional) stage, and the minicycle re-triggers exploration tasks at each transition or destabilizing event.
- **Adaptability:** the authors flag career adaptability as a future research variable; IP plausibly relates positively to adaptability for image-protection (impostors adapt to changing conditions to preserve a capable image) even while it suppresses planning.
- **Shocks:** the minicycle is destabilized "each time an individual is destabilized by socioeconomic and personal events" — career shocks reactivate the exploration stage where impostor feelings impair planning.
- **Self-handicapping / fear of success** connect to Hutchins et al.'s (impostor-two) avoidant coping — both describe imposters protecting themselves by withdrawing effort/engagement rather than confronting the challenge.
