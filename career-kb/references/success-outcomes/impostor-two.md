## Overview

A quantitative study published in Human Resource Development Quarterly (2018), part of a larger mixed-methods project on academic faculty imposter experiences. It addresses a gap: most IP research links IP to psychological well-being indicators, with few studies examining work outcomes and none empirically testing the *mediating mechanism*. Using conservation of resources (COR) theory, the authors examine how IP — treated as a personal demand — contributes to emotional exhaustion and low job satisfaction, and the role of avoidant coping as the mediating mechanism. Two samples of nontenured (clinical, instructional, research), tenure-track, and tenured faculty: Sample 1 from a public research-intensive university (N=89) and Sample 2 from a public medical teaching school (N=221), both in a large urban area of the south-central US.

## Key Constructs & Definitions

- **Imposter phenomenon (IP):** "the internal experience of intellectual phoniness and the inability to internalize professional success despite objective indicators noting otherwise." An internal experience of intellectual/professional incapability marked by reoccurring self-doubt, inaccurate performance attributions, and persistent anxiety that others will view one as an intellectual phony. Imposters underestimate their ability, make inaccurate attributions about success and failure, and engage in maladaptive behaviors (overworking) to compensate. Vergauwe et al. described IP as a constellation of personality traits: low self-efficacy (self-doubt), maladaptive perfectionism (unrealistic goal setting), and neuroticism (fear and worry).
- **Conservation of resources (COR) theory (Hobfoll, 1989):** resources are "objects, personal characteristics, conditions, or energies that are valued by the individual." Resources can be invested to meet job demands and obtain additional resources. People seek to develop and stockpile resources to offset future loss; under stress they act to conserve and protect remaining resources. Stressful conditions cause net resource loss; strain (e.g., burnout) results.
- **Emotional exhaustion:** feelings of being overextended and worn down; the key component of burnout; reflects emotional resource depletion — exhausted employees "feel as though they lack adaptive resources and cannot give any more to their job."
- **Avoidant coping:** attempts to reduce negative emotions triggered by a stressor by conserving resources — through mental or behavioral disengagement, denial, distraction. Requires investment of *fewer* resources than active coping, so it conserves resources short-term, but because resources are not directed at eliminating the stressor, the stressor persists and exhaustion increases (only temporary relief).
- **Active coping:** taking intentional steps to remove or lessen a stressor (emotional support, active planning); requires investment of resources toward the stressor.
- **Job satisfaction:** cognitive and affective evaluations of one's job; the study focuses on *global* job satisfaction.
- **Resource loss spiral:** poor investment of resources via avoidant coping leaves high-IP individuals with few remaining resources to address imposter thoughts that continue to plague them, creating a spiral of increasing emotional exhaustion.

## Main Findings & Arguments

- **Hypotheses (all from COR logic):** H1 — IP positively related to emotional exhaustion; H2 — IP negatively related to job satisfaction; H3 — IP→emotional exhaustion mediated by avoidant coping; H4 — IP→job satisfaction serially mediated by avoidant coping and emotional exhaustion.
- **Descriptives:** Sample 1 reported higher IP (M=2.81) than Sample 2 (M=2.55, t=2.69, p=.008), and marginally higher avoidant coping (M=2.05 vs 1.83, p=.057).
- **H1 supported:** IP positively related to emotional exhaustion (r = .41 and .47 for Samples 1 and 2, p<.01).
- **H2 supported:** IP negatively related to job satisfaction (r = −.30 and −.28 for Samples 1 and 2, p<.01).
- **H3 partially supported:** in Sample 2, both direct (b=.42) and indirect (b=.15) effects of IP on emotional exhaustion through avoidant coping were significant — the IP–emotional exhaustion relationship is *partially*, not fully, mediated by avoidant coping. In Sample 1, effects were in the expected direction but not significant (likely low power, small N).
- **H4 partially supported:** the direct effect of IP on job satisfaction was not significant in either sample, but the total indirect effect was significant (b = −.25 and −.34) — IP–job satisfaction is *fully mediated* through avoidant coping and emotional exhaustion. The breakdown differed by sample: Sample 1 showed a significant indirect effect through emotional exhaustion alone (b=−.10); Sample 2 showed the full serial indirect effect (b=−.06) plus the indirect effect through emotional exhaustion alone (b=−.18).
- **Avoidant coping only partially mediates:** contrary to expectations, IP relates directly to emotional exhaustion controlling for avoidant coping — IP functions as a personal demand sufficient to tax resources on its own.
- **IP indirectly relates to job satisfaction through emotional exhaustion independently** — another route to low job satisfaction is the exhaustion produced by ruminating on imposter thoughts.
- **Gender moderation (exploratory post hoc):** gender moderates the IP–avoidant coping relationship (β=.41, p=.027) — the relationship between IP and avoidant coping is *stronger for men than for women*. Even though women on average reported more avoidant coping, among high-IP individuals men appear to engage in more avoidant coping. Women faculty were more likely to experience more IP, use more avoidant coping, and report higher emotional exhaustion.
- **Why it matters for HRD:** imposters are generally regarded as competent and successful by others yet are more likely to experience emotional exhaustion and low job satisfaction — limiting their ability to fully develop their work potential.

## Frameworks & Models

**Proposed fully serially mediated model (Figure 1):**

`Imposter Phenomenon → Avoidant Coping → Emotional Exhaustion → (−) Job Satisfaction`

Components and causal logic:
1. **IP as personal demand / resource deficit:** high-IP employees overperform to hide self-perceived shortcomings, function with an inherent resource deficit, and rarely attribute success to their own ability (external locus of control), so success does not replenish their resources.
2. **Avoidant coping as resource-conserving response:** operating with few resources, high-IP individuals conserve remaining resources by disengaging/distracting rather than confronting the stressor.
3. **Emotional exhaustion as resource depletion:** because avoidant coping does not eliminate the stressor, imposter thoughts persist; performing under resource depletion increases the threat of being revealed, deepening exhaustion (loss spiral).
4. **Low job satisfaction as outcome:** an emotionally exhausted imposter's job becomes a source of chronic stress and dissatisfaction; the job is perceived as threatening to expose inadequacy.

The model bridges Whitman & Shanine (2012) and Vergauwe et al. (2015) by applying COR theory to empirically test the mediating roles.

## Measurement

- **Imposter phenomenon:** Clance's Imposter Phenomenon Survey (CIPS; Clance, 1985), abbreviated 15-item version, 5-point Likert (1 = strongly disagree, 5 = strongly agree), α = .92 combined. Sample items: "I'm afraid people important to me may find out that I'm not as capable as they think I am"; "Sometimes I feel or believe that my success in my life or in my job has been the result of some kind of error." Distinct from self-esteem, social anxiety, and self-monitoring.
- **Avoidant coping:** four items from the Brief COPE (Carver, 1997), α = .82 combined — two from behavioral disengagement ("I've given up trying to cope with having these thoughts") and two from mental disengagement/self-distraction ("I've been doing something to think about it less, such as going to movies, watching TV..."). 4-point Likert (1 = I haven't been doing this at all, 4 = I've been doing this a lot).
- **Emotional exhaustion:** five items from the emotional exhaustion subscale of the Maslach Burnout Inventory (MBI; Maslach, 1982), α = .91. Sample items: "I feel emotionally drained from my work"; "I feel I'm working too hard on my job." 5-point Likert.
- **Job satisfaction:** three-item global job satisfaction subscale from the Michigan Organizational Assessment Questionnaire (Cammann et al., 1979), α = .90. 5-point Likert.
- **Analysis:** PROCESS macro (Hayes, 2012), model 6 for serial mediation; bootstrapped 1,000-resample confidence intervals; CFA in Mplus 8 indicated satisfactory discriminant validity (RMSEA=.075, SRMR=.061). Harman's single-factor test and an unmeasured-latent-method-construct test ruled out substantial common method bias.

## Practical Implications

- **For HRD practice:** because IP–job dissatisfaction operates through avoidant coping, develop learning and development interventions that promote *active* coping for emotional exhaustion — normalizing imposter experiences, peer support groups, cognitive reframing, mentoring, performance coaching.
- **Cognitive reframing / attributional error training:** help imposters become aware of and correct their attributional errors (attributing success to external causes, failure to internal); Lyden et al. found coaching on attribution raised self-efficacy. "Cognitive reframing" adjusts an imposter's internal identity asymmetry to align self-perception with how colleagues perceive them.
- **Train managers, mentors, support agents** to recognize signs of imposter tendencies (perfectionistic work tendencies, emotional exhaustion, attribution errors) and engage in developmental conversations.
- **Build cultures where "smart mistakes" are rewarded** and success is attributed to skill rather than luck — normalizing imposter concerns helps debunk the notion that IP is an individual deficiency.
- **For academic faculty specifically:** include imposter discussions in new faculty orientation; provide regular performance feedback via department chairs and mentors; explore how imposter concerns slow career advancement (fewer promotions/leadership opportunities) for otherwise high-potential faculty — especially women and underrepresented minority faculty.
- **Gender consideration:** men may actually be at greater risk for decreased work outcomes under an unsafe work climate given their reliance on avoidant coping; tailor interventions to gender differences in coping.
- **Consider psychological safety / organizational climate** as a mediating factor; the "publish or perish" climate can accentuate imposter concerns.

## Connections

- **Impostorism:** a focused primary study of the construct that the Bravata et al. systematic review (impostor-one) catalogs; uses the same CIPS instrument family.
- **Success / outcomes:** directly operationalizes the cluster theme — shows the causal pathway by which an internal state (IP) degrades concrete career outcomes (job satisfaction) and well-being (emotional exhaustion/burnout).
- **Inaction / indecision:** avoidant coping is a form of behavioral inaction — disengaging from rather than confronting a stressor — and the paper shows this inaction is what perpetuates the damage.
- **Adaptability:** COR theory frames adaptability as a resource-management problem; high-IP individuals operate at a resource deficit, reducing their capacity to adapt to job demands.
- **Shocks:** Hutchins & Rainbolt's companion critical-incident work identified specific *triggering events* (imposter episodes) across career stages — career shocks that activate imposter coping.
- **Transitions:** imposter episodes recur across faculty career stages (assistant → associate → full), suggesting impostorism is a perennial career-development issue re-triggered at each transition.
