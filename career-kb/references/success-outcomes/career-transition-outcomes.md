## Overview

A systematic literature review (Frontiers in Psychology, 2023) following PRISMA guidelines, reviewing 244 peer-reviewed articles (1980-2022, drawn from 545 full-text screened, 976 identified) in organizational behavior and HRM journals. It asks three questions: (1) What is a successful career transition and what makes it so? (2) What is the state of the art? (3) What are the implications for future research? The review builds on Louis (1980) and prior reviews by Sullivan & Al Ariss (2021) and De Vos et al. (2021), aiming to clean up the nomological net around career transition success.

## Key Constructs & Definitions

- **Career transition (Louis 1980):** "The period during which an individual is either changing roles (taking on a different objective role) or changing orientation to a role already held (altering a subjective state)." Both a process of change and the period during which change takes place.
- **Career transition (alternative, O'Neil et al. 1987):** "Events or non-events in the career development process causing changes in the meaning of the career, one's self-assumptions, and view of the world."
- **Successful career transition:** No academic consensus or definition exists; studies define metrics narrowly within their own scope.
- **Objective career transition outcomes:** Easily measurable — employment status, salary, performance, promotion, international mobility.
- **Subjective career transition outcomes:** Adjustment & coping, affective outcomes (satisfaction, commitment, positive emotional response), career-related resources (career adaptability, career development, job fit, CDSE, etc.), personal resources (health/well-being, growth/learning).
- **Self-referent success criteria:** Personal standards/references in accordance with a personally defined metric or value.
- **Other-referent success criteria:** Social comparison — comparing one's outcomes to those of other people (Festinger 1954; Heslin 2005). **No study in the sample used other-referent criteria.**
- **Adjustment:** "Readiness to cope with changing work and work conditions" — a stress-coping perspective acknowledging transitions are volatile and stress-inducing.
- **Career adaptability:** "Readiness to cope with changing work and work conditions" (Savickas 1994).
- **Employability:** A broad range of factors contributing to a person's ability to secure paid work.

## Main Findings & Arguments

**Profiles of studies:**
- Publications negligible 1981-1984, marked increase after 2007 (parallel to Career Construction Theory, protean/boundaryless orientations, expansion of social cognitive career theory). Peak: 23 articles in 2019.
- Geography heavily skewed: USA 28.3%, UK 11.9%, Australia 7%; >40% Europe, 34% North America. Africa, South-East Asia, South America each <1%.
- Methods: 63.9% quantitative, 29% qualitative, 6.8% mixed; surveys dominant (122 studies); 66.4% cross-sectional.
- Heavy reliance on student samples (37.3%) and school-to-work as the dominant transition type studied.

**Outcomes of transitions** (Table 5 matrix — objective/subjective × self-referent/other-referent):
- ALL studies used self-referent criteria; NONE used other-referent criteria.
- **Entry into workforce** — most prominent transition type (141 studies, mostly school-to-work). Most-used outcome: employment status (N = 44-48), followed by satisfaction (N = 16), adjustment & coping and career adaptability and employability (N = 12 each), job fit/job-qualification fit (N = 9), salary (N = 7).
- **Promotion** — markedly less studied (15 studies); subtypes intra-organizational, inter-organizational, intersectoral.
- **Lateral moves** — 45 studies; subtypes inter-role, inter-organizational, intersectoral, overseas; most common outcome adjustment & coping and employment status.
- **Resignation** — least studied (2 studies); outcomes engagement, performance, positive emotional response.
- **Retirement** — 13 studies; majority operationalize the outcome as employment status (bridge employment / career renaissance).
- **Other transition types** (Table 6, outside Louis 1980 typology): involuntary (7), school→higher education (4), full-time→part-time (4), to self-employment (3), to portfolio career (2), work→school (2), part-time→full-time (1), precarious→stable (1), stalled (7).
- **Employment status is the single most common outcome across all transition types** — but the authors argue it is a "simplistic conceptualization" and merely finding employment may no longer be adequate.

**Antecedents of transitions** (Table 7): far more varied than outcomes. Most prominent antecedent — demographic characteristics (mentioned in 7 transition types) — followed by social support (6 types). Crucially, **most antecedents are individual rather than contextual** — i.e., researchers use factors within an individual's control rather than external environmental factors.

**Two underlying causes of under-theorization:** (1) no single definition of "career transition"; (2) no universal typology of career transitions nor of successful-transition criteria. Louis's (1980) typology needs revisiting for emerging transition types (freelancing, self-employment, portfolio/gig work, part-time, return-to-education).

## Frameworks & Models

- **Louis (1980) five-type transition typology** used by the review to categorize transitions: (1) entry, (2) promotion, (3) lateral movements, (4) resignation, (5) retirement — plus an "other" category for transitions not fitting the typology.
- **Objective-subjective × self-referent-other-referent matrix** (Table 5) — combining Ruschoff et al. (2021) objective/subjective dichotomy with Heslin's (2005) self-referent/other-referent distinction. Result: only the self-referent column is populated; the other-referent column is entirely empty.
- **Table 7** organizes antecedents by career stage (early/mid/late) and transition type across columns: job-search related resources/actions, employment status, personal resources, adjustment & coping, career-related resources, affective outcomes.

## Measurement

- This is a review of how others operationalized constructs, not a primary measurement study.
- Career transition success operationalizations are fragmented and study-specific; employment status is the most common because it is the easiest to measure.
- The Career Transitions Inventory (Heppner 1998) is highlighted as a useful instrument that empirically tested several subdimensions of a positive career transition experience.
- The review used Scopus CiteScore (top 50%) to select 120 journals; Journal of Vocational Behavior was the most frequent source (56 articles).

## Practical Implications

- **For researchers:** Develop an authoritative, updated typology of career transitions that incorporates emerging forms (gig, freelance, portfolio, return-to-education). Move away from a "pathological" view toward a view of transitions as processes with positive/successful potential. Reverse-engineer subdimensions of successful transitions (via the Career Transitions Inventory). Expand beyond employment status to capture perceived correspondence between employment and individual career wishes.
- **For HRM practitioners:** Acknowledge the different transition types and their distinct outcomes/antecedents. Be aware of individual differences in work priorities (e.g., by age group). Facilitate successful transitions through enhancement strategies — identifying transferable skills from prior careers (leadership, communication, performing under pressure). Foster a stimulating work environment, internal locus of control, generalized self-efficacy, and career curiosity to help workers maintain adaptive responses.
- Expand research on under-studied groups facing barriers (persons with disabilities, ex-offenders, refugees — <10% of sampled papers).

## Notable Quotes

- "We found that career transition success outcomes studied fall under the self-referent category, with the outcomes in the other-referent category absent."
- "There is no single definition of 'career transitions' in the academic literature... [and] there is neither a universal typology of career transitions nor a consensus."
- "Most authors use a simplistic conceptualization of a successful transition as defined by having attained employment. However... merely finding employment may no longer be an adequate indicator of a successful transition."
- "Most antecedents are individual rather than contextual. In other words, most researchers use antecedents that are in an individual's control rather than external to the individual undergoing a career transition."
- "Research in this area should move towards adopting a less pathological view of career transitions."
- "Authors who conceptualize transitions as 'successful' in their research narrowly define the metrics of success within the limits of their studies."

## Connections

- **Transitions:** This is a central transitions document — a systematic map of how career transition *success* has (and has not) been studied; directly builds on Louis (1980) "Career transition theory" in this same cluster.
- **Success:** Operationalizes the objective/subjective career success dichotomy specifically for transition outcomes; connects to Seibert, Akkermans & Liu (2024) and Ng & Feldman (2014).
- **Shocks:** Involuntary transitions, job loss, and the "Great Resignation" framing connect to the career shocks literature; calls for less-pathological framing of disruptive events.
- **Adaptability:** Career adaptability and employability are among the most-studied subjective transition outcomes; internal LoC and career curiosity recommended as practitioner levers.
- **Indecision/inaction:** Career decision-making self-efficacy and career decidedness appear as transition outcomes; "non-events" (O'Neil et al.) connect to career inaction.
- **Impostorism:** Self-efficacy, self-esteem, self-knowledge, and career doubt appear repeatedly as entry-transition antecedents in Table 7.
