## Overview

This article (Journal of Career Assessment, 2006, Vol. 14[2], 235-251) tests the validity of the developmental vs. chronic career indecision typology over time and explains the indecision types as a function of self-efficacy, autonomy, and social support from parents and friends. It uses a 3-year longitudinal design with college students (N = 325 with complete data; initial cohort 834) from Quebec, applying self-determination theory (SDT; Deci & Ryan, 1985) as the conceptual lens. Prior studies (cluster analyses, longitudinal CDS designs) had methodological shortcomings: clustering and CDS subscales did not actually evaluate the *continuity* of indecision over time, and using CDS subscales as predictors of CDS-defined indecision status was circularly biased. This study uses semiparametric group-based trajectory modeling and SDT-derived predictors that are independent of the indecision measure.

## Key Constructs & Definitions

- **Career indecision** — being unable to make a decision about the career one wishes to pursue.
- **Developmental indecision** — a developmentally normal problem resulting from a lack of information on the self and on the world of work. Students with developmental indecision should experience a *decrease* in career indecision over time as they gather information.
- **Chronic indecision** — a pervasive inability to make a decision about one's career. Chronically undecided students should remain *stably* undecided over time. (Both terms originate with Dysinger, 1950.)
- **Self-determination theory (SDT)** — an approach to human motivation highlighting three fundamental psychological needs: autonomy, competence, and relatedness, whose satisfaction produces well-being and optimal functioning.
- **Perceived competence** — individuals feel effective when they perform a given activity (operationalized here as self-efficacy).
- **Perceived autonomy** — individuals experience choice in the initiation, maintenance, and regulation of their behaviors; behaving for intrinsic/identified reasons rather than introjected/external reasons.
- **Perceived relatedness** — the degree to which individuals feel connected to significant others (found only weakly related to career indecision; not a focus here).
- **Autonomy-supportive behaviors** — significant others considering individuals' perspectives, acknowledging feelings, providing information and choice, minimizing pressure and control.
- **Controlling behaviors** — imposing deadlines, rewarding contingently on performance level; thwart perceived competence, relatedness, and autonomy.

## Main Findings & Arguments

- **Three trajectory groups identified** via semiparametric group-based trajectory modeling (SAS TRAJ; BIC-selected three-group model, BIC = -1,026.37):
  - **Decided** — 48% of the sample; steady and low levels of career indecision over time.
  - **Developmentally undecided** — 27%; started at moderate levels, experienced a significant decrease over time (linear parameter significant, p < .001).
  - **Chronically undecided** — 25%; steady, moderate levels of career indecision over time.
- **At T1, chronically and developmentally undecided students reported equivalent levels of career indecision** — meaning a single first-year assessment cannot distinguish them; multiple assessments over time are needed.
- **Self-efficacy** showed a significant Time × Group interaction. Pattern: decided > developmentally undecided > chronically undecided. At T3, decided (M = 4.10) and developmentally undecided (M = 3.9) had equivalent self-efficacy, both higher than chronically undecided (M = 3.4). For developmentally undecided students, all T1-T2-T3 comparisons were significant (T1 M = 3.4, T2 M = 3.6, T3 M = 3.9) — decreases in career indecision were paralleled by increases in self-efficacy. Chronically undecided showed no significant change.
- **Perceived autonomy** (main effect of group): decided and developmentally undecided students (M = 6.1) reported higher autonomy than chronically undecided students (M = 4.4); no difference between decided and developmentally undecided. This is the key variable distinguishing those at risk of chronic indecision: at T1, chronically undecided students had lower autonomy than developmentally undecided students.
- **Social support (autonomy support and control from parents and peers) did NOT distinguish chronic from developmental indecision** — an "intriguing" absence. Decided students did perceive less control and more peer autonomy support than chronically undecided students, but developmentally and chronically undecided groups did not differ on social correlates. This raises the unanswered question: if developmentally undecided students feel more autonomous and self-efficacious but do not perceive more autonomy-supportive parents/peers, what is the source of their autonomy and self-efficacy?
- Correlational findings: self-efficacy, followed by autonomy, generated the strongest correlations with career indecision across all three waves (self-efficacy with CDS indecision: -.51, -.57, -.59).
- Tentative explanation: developmentally undecided students experienced decreased indecision because their self-efficacy toward career decision-making activities increased; their relatively high, stable autonomy may have driven information-seeking behaviors that promoted self-efficacy. Chronically undecided students' low autonomy may have impeded self-efficacy development through avoidance of career decision activities.

## Frameworks & Models

**The career indecision typology (Dysinger, 1950):** two categories — developmental indecision (normal, information-based, transient) and chronic indecision (pervasive, persistent inability to decide). This study adds a third empirically-derived group: decided.

**SDT applied to career indecision:** SDT contributes three things — (1) it explains the interplay among contextual and personal determinants (effects of context on outcomes occur via both competence/self-efficacy beliefs and perceptions of autonomy); (2) it identifies students at risk of chronic indecision (those with low autonomy support and low perceived autonomy/self-efficacy, even when their T1 indecision levels look equivalent to developmentally undecided peers); (3) because SDT focuses on contextual variables promoting successful adaptation, it points to interventions — an autonomy-supportive school counselor would help chronically undecided clients become more self-responsible and find values/interests/goals not contingent on external standards.

**Hypothesized pattern of means:** decided > developmentally undecided > chronically undecided for self-efficacy, perceived autonomy, and autonomy support; reverse for perceived control (decided < developmentally undecided < chronically undecided).

## Measurement

- **Career indecision** — Career Decision Scale (CDS; Osipow, 1987), 18 items: Certainty (items 1-2) and Indecision (items 3-18); 4-point continuum (1 = like me to 4 = not like me). Only the Indecision subscale used. Cronbach's α = .90 (T1), .91 (T2), .92 (T3).
- **Self-efficacy** — shortened Career Decision-Making Self-Efficacy Scale (Betz et al., 1996), 25 items measuring belief one can successfully complete career-decision tasks (accurate self-appraisal, gathering occupational information); 5-level confidence continuum. α = .92/.94/.93.
- **Perceived autonomy** — Career Decision-Making Autonomy Scale (CDMAS; Guay, 2005), surveys 8 career-decision activities; for each, participants rate the extent participation was based on intrinsic motivation, identified regulation, introjected regulation, and external regulation; 7-point Likert. A Perceived Autonomy Index (PAI) was computed per activity: (intrinsic + identified) − (introjected + external), then averaged. Subscale αs .91-.95.
- **Parental control and autonomy support** — adapted Perceived Interpersonal Style Scale (Otis & Pelletier, 2004), 29 items, 5 subscales: incompetence feedback (4), autonomy-supportive behaviors (6), controlling behaviors (7), involvement (6), informational feedback (6); 7-point Likert. Parental autonomy support = autonomy-supportive behaviors + informational feedback + involvement; parental control = incompetence feedback + controlling behaviors. αs .74-.94.
- **Peer control and autonomy support** — adapted version, 22 items, 4 subscales: incompetence feedback (4), controlling behaviors (4), involvement (9), informational feedback (5); 7-point Likert. Peer autonomy support = informational feedback + involvement; peer control = incompetence feedback + controlling. αs .64-.90 (one subscale .48).
- **Analytic method** — semiparametric group-based (mixture) trajectory modeling using SAS TRAJ (Jones, Nagin, & Roeder, 2001), with optimal group number chosen by BIC; repeated-measures analyses (Time × Group) for the six personal/social measures.

## Practical Implications

- A single first-year assessment of career indecision cannot distinguish chronically from developmentally undecided students because they report equivalent indecision at T1; counselors relying solely on the CDS will intervene identically with both groups, despite their needing different interventions. Multiple assessments over time are needed.
- The Career Decision-Making Autonomy Scale (CDMAS) plus the CDS can be used jointly to distinguish chronic from developmental indecision at T1 — chronically undecided students show lower autonomy.
- School counselors should use interventions with chronically undecided students that promote both self-efficacy beliefs *and* feelings of autonomy toward career decision-making activities. Adopting an autonomy-supportive counseling approach should help chronically undecided students feel more autonomous and self-efficacious.
- For developmentally undecided students, the natural course (gathering information → increased self-efficacy → decreased indecision) tends to resolve over time.

## Connections

- **Indecision (chronic vs. developmental)**: This is the canonical empirical validation of the Dysinger typology; directly referenced in the Xu (2019) review's discussion of differentiating developmental indecision from chronic indecisiveness.
- **Inaction**: chronic indecision — a stable, persistent inability to decide accompanied by low autonomy and avoidance of career decision activities — is a defining form of long-term career inaction.
- **Adaptability**: self-efficacy and autonomy operate as adaptive resources; developmentally undecided students "adapt out" of indecision while chronically undecided students do not.
- **Self-efficacy / impostorism**: low career decision-making self-efficacy as a marker of chronic indecision links to broader self-doubt and competence-belief themes.
- **Transitions**: studies the college-to-career transition longitudinally; trajectory modeling captures who resolves the transition and who stalls.
- **Coping**: the avoidance of career decision activities by chronically undecided students parallels Nonproductive coping (escape, helplessness) in the SCCI model.
