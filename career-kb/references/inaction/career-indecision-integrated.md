## Overview

A literature review (European Journal of Training and Development, 47, 1/2, 2023, 166–182) by Xinyi Bian (Texas A&M University). It is positioned as the first integrative review and theoretical synthesis of career indecision, aimed at stimulating interest in the Human Resource Development (HRD) field. Using Torraco's (2005, 2016) integrative review methodology, the author screened 201 publications down to 60 articles and synthesized them into a nomological network (Cronbach & Meehl, 1955) of antecedents, outcomes, and interventions. The review identifies "omissions" in the literature and proposes a research agenda. Career indecision is defined broadly as "the difficulty of deciding in terms of choosing educational and career goals."

## Key Constructs & Definitions

- **Career indecision** — the difficulty of deciding on educational and career goals; per Osipow (1999), "a developmental phase through which individuals may pass on their way to reaching a decision"; a state that "comes and goes over time as a decision is made, is implemented, grows obsolete and eventually leads to the need to make a new decision."
- **Career indecisiveness** — per Osipow (1999), "a personal trait which generalizes across situations demanding decisions"; a chronic inability to make decisions, contrasted with the developmental, transient nature of indecision. The review argues theorists draw a clear line between the two but empirical studies often confuse them.
- **Nomological network** — Cronbach & Meehl's (1955) framework depicting the structural relationships among constructs ("graphical depiction of what is known and not known about a theory"); used to organize the review.
- **Ambiguity intolerance** — "a psychological construct in which individuals view ambiguous stimuli as a threat"; directly predicts general indecisiveness; often used interchangeably with intolerance of uncertainty.
- **Career self-efficacy** — "the degree to which individuals feel confident that they can successfully perform tasks in the career decision-making process" (Gati et al., 2011).
- **Decisional procrastination** — Bańka & Hauziński's (2015) framing of career indecision as "school-to-work transition procrastination."
- **Career inaction** — Verbruggen & De Vos's (2020) construct: "the failure to act sufficiently on a desired change"; the review names career indecision a subcategory of career inaction warranting deeper analysis.

## Main Findings & Arguments

- **Definitional evolution (Table 1).** Definitions traced chronologically from Holland (1964: "deferment of vocational choice") through Slaney (1988: distinguishing normal/developmental indecision from longer-lasting indecisiveness), Osipow (1999), Gati et al. (2011), to Lipshits-Braziler et al. (2019). The construct has broadened from students/young adults to wider populations.
- **Measurement growth (Table 2).** Brown & Rector (2008) identified over 50 variables correlating with career indecision. Major instruments: Career Factors Inventory (CFI; Chartrand et al., 1990 — career choice anxiety, generalized indecisiveness, need for career information, need for self-knowledge); Career Decision-Making Difficulties Questionnaire (CDDQ; Gati & Saka, 2001 — lack of readiness, lack of information, inconsistent information); Career Indecision Scale (CIS; Germeijs & Boeck, 2003 — information problems, valuation problems, outcome uncertainty); Career Indecision Profile (CIP; Brown & Rector, 2008; CIP-167, Brown et al., 2012 — neuroticism/negative affectivity, choice/commitment anxiety, lack of readiness, interpersonal conflicts); Emotional and Personality Career Difficulties scale (EPCD; Saka et al., 2008 — pessimistic views, anxiety, self-concept and identity); CIP-65 / CIP-short (Hacker et al., 2013; Xu & Tracey).
- **Antecedents — individual factors** (35 of 60 studies):
  - *Mental health disorders*: high anxiety is "responsible for career indecision"; chronic anxiety → more career decision-making difficulties (lack of readiness, lack of information). Some studies find anxiety may *facilitate* decision-making by increasing exploration over time (Park et al., 2017). Depression is linked to indecision and dysfunctional decision-making for both genders.
  - *Personality*: studies extend beyond Big Five into ambiguity tolerance/intolerance, procrastination, career self-efficacy, perfectionism. Ambiguity intolerance directly predicts general indecisiveness; excessive fear/worry fosters dysfunctional beliefs about future outcomes. Perfectionism's link to indecision may arise via its inseparable relationship with anxiety. Big Five: high conscientiousness → active problem-focused coping; high neuroticism → denial and ineffective coping; "high values of indecision are accompanied by high values in neuroticism."
  - *Procrastination vs. proactiveness*: career indecision framed as decisional procrastination at the school-to-work transition; self-efficacy and occupational commitment predict procrastination; proactive personality negatively predicts decision-making difficulties (though proactive individuals may struggle early then overcome over time).
  - *Capabilities*: career adaptability is a significant antecedent that alleviates indecision and improves adjustment; emotional intelligence significantly affects three dimensions of indecision (lack of readiness, lack of information, inconsistent information) — but "the relation of emotional intelligence to career indecision difficulties is not reciprocal."
- **Antecedents — contextual factors** (three components):
  - *Family environment*: parental support and emotional warmth reduce indecision; family conflict (a type of interpersonal conflict) is a major source, especially in collectivist cultures (e.g., Asian Americans); chaotic childhood family life increases interpersonal conflicts in decision-making.
  - *Perceived social support*: higher social support facilitates decision-making; career education and career counseling are the most-studied aspects.
  - *Political and social references*: Ukil (2016, Bangladesh) found political influences in recruitment/selection and national job shortages increase decision-making difficulties — only one reviewed article took this macro view.
- **Outcomes.** Little research exists. Exceptions: Arbona et al. (2021) and Miller & Rottinghaus (2014) found indecisive individuals are more likely to have anxiety issues and a lack of sense of meaning.
- **Interventions.** Lipshits-Braziler et al. (2016): coping strategies classified as productive coping, support-seeking, and nonproductive coping; support-seeking is most effective, but young adults ironically use nonproductive coping more. Vertsberger & Gati (2016): young adults prefer easy-access support (family, friends, internet) over professional counselors. Solution-focused brief career counseling (SFBCC; Akyol & Bacanlı, 2019) reduces decision-making difficulty. Maree (2020): career construction counseling moves help-seekers from a motivated agency to an autobiographical author of their career-life story. Assessment feedback (item-level interpretation; Rochat, 2019) improves career decision-making self-efficacy.

## Frameworks & Models

**Nomological network of career indecision** — the organizing structure of the review:
- *Antecedents* → Individual factors (mental health disorders; personality — ambiguity, perfectionism, career self-efficacy, procrastination vs. proactiveness, Big Five; capabilities — career adaptability, emotional intelligence) and Contextual factors (family environment, perceived social support, political/social references).
- *Outcomes* → anxiety, lack of meaning in life (underexplored).
- *Interventions* → coping strategies (productive/support-seeking/nonproductive), solution-focused approaches (SFBT, SFBCC), career construction counseling, assessment feedback.

**Method:** integrative review (Whittemore & Knafl, 2005; Torraco, 2005/2016) embracing empirical and non-empirical studies; search keywords "career indecision" and "career decision-making difficulties" across Academic Search Ultimate, Business Source Ultimate, Vocational Studies Complete; 2011–2021; 267 → 204 → 201 → three rounds of screening (157 → 72 → 60). Critical analysis used to deconstruct the topic into antecedents, outcomes, interventions.

**Gordon's (1998) career decidedness typology** cited as worth re-testing: seven subtypes — very decided, somewhat decided, unstable decided, tentatively undecided, developmentally undecided, seriously undecided, chronically indecisive.

## Measurement

The review surveys instruments rather than developing one (see Table 2 in Main Findings). Key dimensional structures:
- CFI: 21 items / 4 factors.
- CDDQ: 34-item version / 3 domains.
- CIS (Germeijs & Boeck 2003): information problems, valuation problems, outcome uncertainty.
- CIP-167: high anxiety/depressive affect/trait neuroticism; low self-esteem/hardiness/problem-solving confidence; fear of commitment; external locus of control — plus lack-of-information, lack-of-preparedness, and external barrier/interpersonal conflict latent dimensions.
- EPCD (Saka et al. 2008): pessimistic views, anxiety, self-concept and identity.
- Scholars caution against cross-cultural use without measurement invariance testing (Abrams et al., 2013 — US vs. Iceland).

## Practical Implications

- **Personal growth**: a lack of personal ability significantly hinders searching for a desired career; individuals can improve career planning, adaptability, and emotional intelligence. Professionals should propose learning/development interventions (career advising, skill training) to raise confidence and self-efficacy.
- **Organizational context**: career indecision can happen at any life stage; employers should support career advancement via career development programs, identifying milestones, recommending role models, outlining potential career paths.
- **Social support**: career education and counseling are valuable resources. Because anxiety and career indecision form a "vicious circle" intensifying each other, it is better to tackle one thing at a time — depressive symptoms are expected to ease once overall life decidedness improves (Bańka & Hauziński, 2015).
- HRD professionals should avoid "muddling" indecision and indecisiveness by studying them separately.

## Notable Quotes

- "the issue now encompasses broad life spectrum because of the increased frequency of events that require people to revise their career decisions over their life span" (Osipow, 1999, p. 147).
- Career indecisiveness is "a personal trait which generalizes across situations demanding decisions" (Osipow, 1999, p. 148).
- "excessive fear and worry would foster dysfunctional beliefs about future outcomes, thus hindering decision-making" (Arbona et al., 2021).
- "high values of indecision are accompanied by high values in neuroticism" (Bańka & Hauziński, 2015, p. 41).
- "the relation of emotional intelligence to career indecision difficulties is not reciprocal" (Phang et al., 2020, p. 665).
- "the failure to act sufficiently on a desired change is a specific career phenomenon that is largely ignored" (on Verbruggen & De Vos, 2020, p. 376).
- "Having difficulties in choosing occupational goals is a normal developmental stage and prevalent in individuals' career lives. However, career indecision could affect individuals' sense of meaning and mental health without sufficient understanding and proper interventions."

## Connections

- **Indecision vs. indecisiveness**: central theme — directly continues the Germeijs & De Boeck (2003) distinction; the review explicitly flags empirical confusion of the two.
- **Career inaction**: explicitly names career indecision a subcategory of Verbruggen & De Vos's (2020) career inaction; the Rogiers et al. psychodynamic study is the empirical follow-up to that theory.
- **Anxiety**: anxiety appears as both antecedent and outcome; the anxiety↔indecision "vicious circle" connects directly to Miller & Rottinghaus's mediation findings.
- **Meaning in life**: cites Miller & Rottinghaus (2014) under outcomes (indecisive individuals lack a sense of meaning).
- **Adaptability**: career adaptability as a protective antecedent links to the broader adaptability cluster.
- **Shocks / transitions**: research agenda calls for studying indecision among working adults seeking a career shift or returning from a career break.
- **Measurement lineage**: catalogs the CIS, CDDQ, CIP, EPCD scales referenced across the other inaction-cluster docs.
