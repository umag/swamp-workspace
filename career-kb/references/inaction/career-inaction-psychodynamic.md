## Overview

A research article (Journal of Vocational Behavior, 136, 2022, 103745) by Rogiers, Verbruggen, D'Huyvetter & Abraham (KU Leuven / Vlerick Business School). It is the first empirical exploration of the *lived experience* of career inaction, a phenomenon recently theorized by Verbruggen & De Vos (2020). Whereas most career-transition research assumes a linear path (desire change → search → decide → act), this study examines career desires that are *not* acted upon. Using an inductive grounded-theory approach (Strauss & Corbin, 1997; Charmaz, 2006) and the broader literature on psychodynamics, the team analyzed semi-structured interviews with 43 employees across five Belgian public/private organizations who self-identified as "feeling stuck." The research question is twofold: how do people experience career inaction, and how do they respond/cope?

## Key Constructs & Definitions

- **Career inaction** — "the failure to act sufficiently over some period of time on a desire to make a change in one's career" (Verbruggen & De Vos, 2020, p. 376). Inclusion criteria operationalized it as: presence of a change desire, lack of sufficient action, and a continued change desire.
- **Psychodynamic "me"-identifications** — identifications with different feelings and wishes (Brown, 2021; Petriglieri & Petriglieri, 2020). Psychodynamic theory holds that "[inner] conflict is unavoidable and constitutive of the subject." Three were identified:
  - **Striving me** — "a psychodynamic identification that captures individuals' mental striving for change and growth." Rooted in a lack of career progress and a need for continuous learning. A "general" condition (present in all/all-but-one informants). Example: Joanne (25): "I realized: 'Oops, I am sitting here, and I am actually not learning anything new.'"
  - **Comfortable me** — "a psychodynamic identification that captures individuals' seeking of comfort, despite their desire for change." Tied to job benefits (salary, seniority, pension), familiarity, and mastery; often described as a "golden cage." A "typical" condition.
  - **Uncertain me** — "a psychodynamic identification that captures individuals' sense of uncertainty and fear, despite their desire for change." A "typical" condition. Covers fear of the unknown, self-doubt, impostor syndrome, and uncertainty about what change is even wanted.
- **Emotional tensions** — feelings of psychological strain and uneasiness (APA, 2022) situated *between* the "me"-identifications; described by informants as "a wrenching feeling," "ropes" or "lassos," "a personal struggle." Two key tensions: striving me ↔ comfortable me; striving me ↔ uncertain me.
- **Tension-easing strategies** — coping responses, applied consciously and unconsciously, that reduce the tensions between "me"-identifications and ease the psychological strain of inaction.
- **Golden cage** — the dominant in-vivo metaphor for the comfortable me: being embedded and comforted by job benefits/familiarity while still being in a "cage" that suppresses the change desire.

## Main Findings & Arguments

- **Career inaction is emotionally tense**, not a passive or low-conflict state. People's experience is situated among the interaction of the striving, comfortable, and uncertain "me"-identifications. What looks from the outside like career stability is, internally, "a complex mix of emotional tensions."
- **Striving me** (43 informants): informants cited a lack of career progress and a need for continuous learning; the mismatch between lived career experience and developmental needs. Often emerged over extended periods of inaction ("the longer you have been sitting here... you start to be too much in your comfort zone").
- **Comfortable me** (33 informants): informants felt both comforted and stuck by benefits, familiarity, and mastery — the "golden cage." Includes personal "resistance to change."
- **Uncertain me** (38 informants): fear of stepping into the unknown; uncertainty rooted in past failed actions ("I already got the door slammed in my face a couple of times"). Multiple layers of uncertainty: (1) uncertainty/self-doubt about securing a different job — often described as "impostor syndrome," sometimes tied to perceived bias/discrimination based on age (11 informants), gender, pregnancy, mental health, job-related burnout, turbulent career history, or criminal activities; (2) uncertainty about what career change is really wanted (often unspecified/unclear); (3) uncertainty about the new job itself ("the fear of not knowing where you will end up").
- **Tension between striving me and comfortable me** (32 informants): people weigh the desire for change against comfort with the status quo. The longer people remained inactive, the more the comfortable me grew and suppressed the change desire. Perceptions of organizational bias (favoritism, seniority valued over ability) added to this tension.
- **Tension between striving me and uncertain me** (38 informants): people weigh the desire for change against fear and uncertainty.
- **Tension-easing strategies ease strain but perpetuate inaction.** Strategies that focus on postponing or suppressing the striving me reduce psychological strain but do not alter the career situation — so inaction often continues. (Notable exceptions: seeking creative outlets and taking courses.) This contrasts with studies showing people succeed at "synchronizing and harmonizing" multiple work identities — most participants here struggled to feel whole/fulfilled.

## Frameworks & Models

**Data structure (grounded theory, three aggregate dimensions):**

1. **Psychodynamic "me"-identifications**
   - Striving me (43 informants) — vague desire for big change; desire to learn/grow; desire to be the "authentic me"; wanting better job benefits; no/lacking actionable plans.
   - Comfortable me (33 informants) — holding on to the familiar; "golden cage"; stress about losing acquired routine/benefits.
   - Uncertain me (38 informants) — feeling paralyzed/fear of the unknown; uncertainty/low self-esteem; fear of judgment; uncertainty about "direction to take"; avoiding risk.

2. **Emotional tensions**
   - Tension between striving me and comfortable me (32 informants).
   - Tension between striving me and uncertain me (38 informants).

3. **Tension-easing strategies** (one typical, three variant, three rare):
   - *Typical* — **Emphasizing external barriers** (32 informants): foregrounding financial/family situation, age, lack of skills, the job market; helps people explain inaction to themselves and others; involves a "double bind" — recognizing change is still desirable but setting long timeframes (e.g., 5–10 years).
   - *Variant* — **Making high demands** (11 informants): demanding a "clearly better" offer before considering change; appeases the comfortable me. Example (Thomas, 48): "I'm not going to actively search... It would be foolish to resign after 25 years."
   - *Variant* — **Weighing all options** (13 informants): endlessly listing pros and cons; leads to "choice paralysis" and temporarily ceasing the search.
   - *Variant* — **Fantasizing** (16 informants): fantasizing about untrodden career paths; gives positive feeling/energy but recognized as "a mental escape" / "dreaming"; inaction continues.
   - *Rare* — **Seeking creative outlets** (3 informants): hobbies, volunteering, "a second life" / "parallel life."
   - *Rare* — **Taking courses** (3 informants): evening courses, workshops, teacher training.
   - *Rare* — **Meditation** (2 informants): putting thoughts/experience into perspective to ease tensions.

**Theoretical positioning:** extends Verbruggen & De Vos's (2020) theory of career inaction by probing the inaction phase itself (not just consequences after the window closes); draws on psychodynamic theory (Epstein, 1994; Gabriel & Carr, 2002; Petriglieri & Petriglieri, 2020) and Freud's (1936) defense mechanisms; calls for renewed attention to bounded rationality and emotionality in contemporary careers, and suggests the contextual action theory of careers (Young et al., 1996) as a future angle.

## Measurement

Qualitative study — no psychometric scale. Methodological apparatus:
- **Sample**: 43 employees (from 44 retained of 48 interviews) across five Belgian organizations (a university, an international bank/insurance branch, an international pharmaceutical branch, a federal and a regional government entity). 72.9% female; mean age 44.8 (SD 9.6); 72.1% with children; 90.7% higher education (60.5% master's); average 4 jobs in career; experiencing inaction on average over 2 years.
- **Inclusion criteria**: (1) presence of a desire for change, (2) lack of sufficient action, (3) continued change desire — operationalized via career counseling participation in past 6–18 months with no job change in that window.
- **Semi-structured interviews**: conducted Dec 2019 – Apr 2021, in person then via video conferencing (COVID-19), audio-recorded, transcribed verbatim, average 37 minutes; 42 in Dutch, 1 in English; member checks performed; quotations translated and back-translated.
- **Analysis**: MAXQDA (v2020); grounded-theory team coding in rotating pairs; first-order in-vivo/open codes → second-order tentative categories → aggregate dimensions; Hill et al.'s (2005) framework used to label findings as "general," "typical," "variant," or "rare."
- **Researcher positionality**: four KU Leuven careers researchers; two with personal connection to inaction; reflexivity managed via memo-writing and peer debriefing.

## Practical Implications

- **For individuals**: career inaction is hard to live through; people unconsciously seek to ease emotional tensions, but in doing so often postpone or suppress their desire to change.
- **For career counselors**: an opportunity to draw on principles of psychodynamic therapy — help employees find patterns in their contradictory feelings, thoughts, and beliefs, rather than focusing on inaction behaviors alone. The fact that participants had had counseling but still experienced emotional tensions underscores this need.
- **For organizations**: with career management as a shared responsibility, organizations must help people identify suitable internal and external paths for growth and advancement. Novel career development programs could help employees build career resources and accustom them to changing positions (e.g., bidding on organization-wide projects parallel to regular jobs).
- Career counselors, organizations, and individuals should all be aware that career inaction is emotionally challenging to experience.

## Connections

- **Career inaction (core)**: the empirical companion to Verbruggen & De Vos's (2020) theory; the integrative review (Bian, 2023) explicitly names career indecision a subcategory of career inaction.
- **Indecision / decision theory**: the "uncertain me" (uncertainty about what change is wanted; about outcomes; about the new job) maps onto Germeijs & De Boeck's (2003) valuation problems (value unclarity) and outcome uncertainty; "weighing all options"/"choice paralysis" parallels evaluative evenness.
- **Impostorism**: the "uncertain me" explicitly includes "impostor syndrome" and self-doubt about being capable of changing jobs.
- **Anxiety**: emotional tensions, "fear of the unknown," and the strain of inaction connect to anxiety as both antecedent and outcome in Miller & Rottinghaus and Bian.
- **Job embeddedness / "golden cage"**: links the comfortable me to organizational-behavior constructs of job embeddedness and "stuck in a job."
- **Transitions / shocks**: positions inaction against the linear transition model; Wordsworth & Nilakant's earthquake/career-shock study is cited for inaction lasting up to 12 years.
- **Bounded rationality & emotionality**: explicitly calls to reattend to these in contemporary careers, against the field's emphasis on agency and self-management.
