## Overview

This article (Applied Psychology, 2025, 74(1), 1–35, article e12590; DOI 10.1111/apps.12590) develops and validates the **Career Inaction Scale (CARINAS)** — the first reliable, valid, direct measurement instrument for career inaction. It operationalizes the **inaction phase** (the "beating heart") of Verbruggen & De Vos's (2020) theory of career inaction (see `career-inaction.md`), focusing on people's *own appraisal* that they are not taking sufficient action to realize a desired career change.

The paper opens with two headlines framing the problem: "Job unhappiness is at a staggering all-time high, according to Gallup" (Collins, 2022) and "Stuck In A Job You Don't Want? UK Workers Have Entered The 'Great Career Depression'" (Baskerville, 2023). Prior quantitative work could only assess career inaction *indirectly* (by studying people who failed to realize a desired change), which cannot capture the core element — lack of *sufficient action*.

**Four studies:** Study 1 (N = 258, Belgian workers) — item generation and pilot test of the Dutch CARINAS; Study 2 (N = 799, Flemish career-voucher clients) — reliability, measurement invariance, convergent/discriminant validity, first nomological exploration; Study 3 (N = 170/176, US workers via Prolific) — reliability/validity of the English CARINAS, rerun correlations; Study 4 (N = 289 → 198, two-wave Belgian panel) — re-test factor structure, test-retest reliability, expanded nomological network.

## Key Constructs & Definitions

- **Career inaction** — "the failure to act sufficiently over some period of time on a desire to make a change in one's career" (Verbruggen & De Vos, 2020, p. 8). Has three phases: awareness, inaction, recall. The CARINAS targets the **inaction phase**: the desire for change AND the lack of sufficient action — the two core elements reflected in the scale. A high score implies the person has, and is aware of, a career desire and feels they could act more on it than they currently are.
- **CARINAS** — the Career Inaction Scale; a short, efficient, **unidimensional (one-factor)** Likert measure; final version has **eight items** (a ninth item was dropped after CFA in Study 2).
- **Convergent validity** — a scale relates significantly to measures of theoretically related constructs. Tested against career self-directedness, helplessness, being locked-in.
- **Discriminant validity** — a scale is unrelated or at most weakly related to conceptually unrelated constructs. Tested against job autonomy and work pressure.
- **Nomological network** — the web of theoretically/empirically relevant antecedents and outcomes around the construct.
- **Career self-directedness** — an attitude of steering one's own career and taking responsibility for it; reflects personal agency.
- **Helplessness** — the expectation that there is no link between one's actions and the outcomes one desires.
- **Being locked-in** — staying in a non-preferred workplace due to a *perceived lack of alternative job opportunities* (external cause; contrasts with inaction's internal cause — not taking sufficient action).
- **Subjective norms to stay** — perception that important others want one to stay with the current employer.
- **Transition magnitude** — degree of difference (skills, location, knowledge) between a pre-transition and post-transition situation; larger for occupational changes.
- **Proactive personality** — tendency to show initiative, take action, and persevere until meaningful change occurs.
- **On-the-job embeddedness** — job-related elements (promotional opportunities, pension, good colleagues) that keep people embedded/stuck in their organization.
- **Risk aversion** — tendency to avoid options with uncertain outcomes.
- **Outcomes tested** — career satisfaction, affective well-being, job performance, regret about not changing, perceived health.

Career inaction is distinguished from **being locked-in** (external cause), **unanswered callings** (occupational only), and **career indecision** (the state of not having made an optimal choice; indecision typically *precedes* the decision, inaction *hinders enactment* — inaction can include indecision but also internal hindrances after a decision is made).

## Main Findings & Arguments

**Final scale.** A single-factor solution; in Study 1 the first factor had an eigenvalue of 6.25 explaining 69.5% of variance; nine items retained initially with Cronbach's α = .94. In Study 2 a CFA flagged item 5 ("I fail to effectively change the aspects in my career that I want to change") via modification indices; the **eight-item model** fit well (χ²[20] = 104.49; CFI = .960; TLI = .944; RMSEA = .073; SRMR = .040) and significantly better than the nine-item model. Final eight-item α = .87, composite reliability = .88.

**Measurement invariance (Study 2).** Configural, metric, scalar, and strict invariance all confirmed across **gender, educational level, partner status, and having children** — the scale measures career inaction equivalently across these groups. Study 4 confirmed metric, scalar, and strict invariance **over time** (T1 vs. T2).

**Reliability across studies.** Cronbach's α: Study 1 = .94 (9-item); Study 3 (English) = .93; Study 4 = .93 (T1) and .94 (T2). **Test-retest reliability** (Study 4, one month apart): Pearson r = .62, ICC = .62 — judged "good," in line with related career instruments (e.g., CIP-65); the authors note this also suggests career inaction is **not fully stable** (consistent with Rogiers et al.'s finding that its duration/intensity varies between people — for some it takes 2 years to realize the career change, for others 5 to close down the desire).

**Construct validity (Study 2).** *Convergent:* career inaction correlated negatively with career self-directedness (r = −.41) and positively with helplessness (r = .49) and being locked-in (r = .16) — all as hypothesized (H1a–c). *Discriminant:* no significant correlation with job autonomy (r = −.05) or work pressure (r = .04) — as hypothesized (H2a–b). CFAs confirmed CARINAS is a distinct factor.

**Nomological network — antecedents.** Hypotheses and results:
- *Subjective norms to stay* (H3a, expected +): **mixed** — positive in Study 2 (r = .13) but **negative** in Study 3 (r = −.17) and Study 4 (r = −.34 / −.22). Resolved by splitting Study 3's sample: positive (non-significant, r = .12) for people *with* a desire for change, negative (r = −.37) for people *without* one. Interpretation: predictor relationships may only hold for people who already have a desire for change.
- *Transition magnitude* (H3b, expected +): non-significant in Study 2 (r = −.06) but **significant positive** in Studies 3 and 4 (r = .44, .33, .29) — partial support.
- *Proactive personality* (H3c, expected −): **consistently negative** across Studies 2, 3, 4 (r = −.34, −.40, −.34) — supported.
- *On-the-job embeddedness* (H3d, expected +): tested only in Study 4 — **negative** (r = −.47 / −.42), contrary to hypothesis. Cautious interpretation: in a general sample, embedded people may simply be comfortable and have no desire for change.
- *Risk aversion* (H3e, expected +): positive but only **marginally significant** over time — partial support.

**Nomological network — outcomes (consistently supported).** Career inaction was significantly and negatively related to **career satisfaction** (r = −.12, −.34, etc.), **affective well-being** (r = −.23, −.41), and **job performance** (r = −.11, −.41 — though the Study 4 regression effect on task performance was non-significant, B = −.13, p = .12). It was positively related to **regret about not changing** (Study 4 regression B = .29, p < .001). It was negatively related to **perceived health** (Study 4 regression B = −.22, p = .01). Hierarchical regressions (Study 4) showed career inaction explained incremental variance beyond demographics and antecedents: ΔR² = .09 (career satisfaction), .06 (affective well-being), .05 (regret), .03 (perceived health).

**Prevalence.** Career inaction is a prevalent phenomenon: means of **3.21** (SD .70, Study 2 — career-counseling clients), **2.78** (SD .99, Study 3 — US workers), and **2.70** (SD .79, Study 4 — general Belgian/US workers) on a 1–5 scale. A non-negligible share of workers score above the midpoint of 3. The higher Study 2 mean suggests people may seek out career counselors when struggling with inaction.

**Key theoretical takeaways.** (1) Outcome relationships behaved as the theory predicts (inaction → poorer career outcomes), but **antecedent relationships were more ambiguous** — social norms and embeddedness flipped sign depending on whether the sample had a desire for change. The authors conclude predictors of career inaction may only apply to (or change sign for) people who *have at least some desire for change*, and that **benchmarks/thresholds** may be needed. (2) The *experience* of career inaction itself, by contrast, behaves consistently. (3) **Sample characteristics matter enormously** for interpreting CARINAS scores — researchers must reflect carefully on sampling decisions.

## Frameworks & Models

The paper is built on the **three-phase theory of career inaction** (Verbruggen & De Vos, 2020): awareness phase → inaction phase → recall phase. The CARINAS deliberately measures only the **inaction phase**, because that is the "core phase" and the one that brings important negative outcomes (tensions, stress).

**Scale-development procedure** followed Boateng et al. (2018): (1) item generation via the *deductive method* (items based on the 2020 theory plus Rogiers et al.'s 2022 qualitative study of 43 Belgian employees), expert check by 7 career scholars + 10 laymen; (2) dimensionality and reliability testing (EFA, then CFA); (3) construct validity (convergent + discriminant); (4) nomological network exploration.

**Nomological model tested:** five **antecedents** (subjective norms to stay, transition magnitude, proactive personality, on-the-job embeddedness, risk aversion) → **career inaction** → five **outcomes** (career satisfaction, affective well-being, job performance, regret about not changing, perceived health).

## Measurement

**The CARINAS — final 8 items** (5-point Likert, 1 = completely disagree, 5 = completely agree). English translation (with original Dutch); Study 1 standardized loadings shown:
1. "I would like to change something in my career, but I don't actively pursue it" (*Ik wil graag iets veranderen in mijn loopbaan, maar ik maak er geen werk van*) — λ = .81
2. "I would like to change something in my career, but I don't know how to start" (*...maar ik weet niet goed hoe eraan te beginnen*) — λ = .81
3. "I want to change something in my career, but I don't dare to give up what I currently have" (*...maar ik durf niet op te geven wat ik nu heb*) — λ = .73
4. "I fail to take concrete actions to fulfill my career desires" (*Ik slaag er niet in om concrete acties te ondernemen om mijn loopbaanwensen waar te maken*) — λ = .88
5. "I feel paralyzed when thinking about realizing my career desires" (*Ik voel me verlamd wanneer ik nadenk over het realiseren van mijn loopbaanwensen*) — λ = .81
6. "I find it difficult to take action to change something in my career" (*Ik vind het moeilijk om actie te ondernemen om iets aan mijn loopbaan te veranderen*) — λ = .79
7. "I feel stuck in my career" (*Ik heb het gevoel dat ik vastzit in mijn loopbaan*) — λ = .84
8. "I don't manage to realize my career desires" (*Ik kom er niet toe om mijn loopbaanwensen te realiseren*) — λ = .77

**Dropped item** (originally item 5 in the 9-item version): "I fail to effectively change the aspects in my career that I want to change."

**Measures used for the nomological network:** career self-directedness (Briscoe et al., 2006, 8 items, α = .78); helplessness (Odéen et al., 2013, 3 items adapted, α = .75); being locked-in (Stengård et al., 2016 approach — combination of low employability and workplace non-preference); job autonomy (Kossek et al., 2006, 4 items, α = .75); work pressure (Peeters et al., 2005, 3 items, α = .86); subjective norms to stay (adapted Zikic & Saks 2009, 2 items, α = .77); transition magnitude (West et al., 1987, 3 items, α = .87); proactive personality (Claes et al., 2005, 6 items, α = .83); on-the-job embeddedness (Clinton et al., 2012, 6 items, α = .82); risk aversion (Mandrik & Bao, 2005, 6 items, α = .77); career satisfaction (single item, "how satisfied are you currently with your career?"); affective well-being (GHQ, Goldberg & Hillier, 5 items, α ≈ .87–.89); job performance (Abramis, 1994, 6 items, α = .85–.89); regret about not changing (Verbruggen & van Emmerik, 2020, 3 items, α = .91); perceived health (single-item general health measure).

## Practical Implications

**For career counselors and HR professionals** — the CARINAS is a **diagnostic tool** to assess the degree to which a client is in career inaction (i.e., not undertaking sufficient action to realize a desired career change):
- Although external barriers (social norms, economic conditions) may be hard or impossible to alter, working on the **internal forces** of career inaction — fear of the unknown, paralysis caused by the difficulty of the career decision — is more achievable.
- Administering the CARINAS in counseling can itself prompt clients to **reflect on the reasons for their insufficient action**, helping them **map their internal barriers** to the desired change; this awareness enables more efficient, tailored support.
- The **separate item scores** (not just the total) can help counselors design effective, person-specific interventions.
- Counselors can build on **implementation intention theory** (Gollwitzer): forming "if-then" links bound to situational cues — e.g., "Whenever I receive a rejection from an application, I will summarize two points of improvement for the next one" or "If I get scared, I will go talk to people who already succeeded in making this change" — to keep clients enacting their desire and overcome paralysis. (Caveat: this requires a strong, positive, activated goal, which may not always be the case for people struggling with career inaction.)
- **Researchers** must reflect carefully on sampling decisions and may need population-specific **benchmarks** to correctly judge CARINAS scores.

## Connections

- **Career inaction theory** — this paper is the direct empirical operationalization of `career-inaction.md` (Verbruggen & De Vos, 2020); two of those authors overlap (Verbruggen). It tests the 2020 theory's propositions: outcome propositions (inaction → lower satisfaction/well-being/performance, → regret) were supported; antecedent propositions (norms, embeddedness, magnitude) were only partially or conditionally supported.
- **AMA: Career Transitions** — the AMA deck reproduces the CARINAS items verbatim and links the scale as a self-assessment tool; the AMA's "знаю, но не иду" 2×2 quadrant is the CARINAS-measured state.
- **Career indecision** — explicitly distinguished (indecision precedes the decision; inaction hinders enactment); indecision is flagged as a possible *antecedent* of career inaction worth testing with CARINAS.
- **Career shocks** — flagged as a promising future antecedent: positive shocks may lower the desire for change; negative shocks (missed promotion) may either trigger action or deepen inaction/paralysis.
- **Career success / satisfaction (subjective & objective)** — career satisfaction is a validated *outcome* of career inaction; connects to the success-outcomes cluster.
- **Adaptability / proactivity / self-directedness** — proactive personality and career self-directedness are validated correlates (both negative); career inaction is the low-agency counterpart of adaptability.
- **Regret** — regret about not changing is a validated outcome, linking to the recall-phase counterfactual machinery in `career-inaction.md`.
- **Being locked-in, helplessness** — convergent-validity constructs; "being locked-in" is the externally-caused cousin of internally-caused career inaction.
- **Impostorism / well-being** — affective well-being and perceived health as outcomes link to the well-being themes across the corpus.
