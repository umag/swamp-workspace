## Overview

This Major Review (The Career Development Quarterly, March 2019, Vol. 67, 2-20) reviews literature published 2000-2017 on the theoretical and empirical progress of career indecision structural models and measurement. It updates Osipow's (1999) seminal summary. The authors searched PsycARTICLES, Google Scholar, and nine professional journals using keywords "career indecision" and "career decision-making difficulty," selecting 36 final articles. They review theory-driven and data-driven approaches separately, then propose an integrative five-factor model. Career indecision is defined as "a state of being undecided about one's educational, occupational, or career-related path." The review uses career decision-making difficulty as the guiding definition.

## Key Constructs & Definitions

- **Career indecision** — defined inclusively as "a state of being undecided about one's educational, occupational, or career-related path." Historically defined as an inability to make an occupational/educational decision when asked, and a delay in bringing closure to the decision-making process (linear/barrier view; Osipow, Slaney). Contemporary views characterize it as a wavering, pause, hesitation (Savickas), openness to alternative pathways (Krumboltz), or a state of adaptive uncertainty (Krieshok et al.) — i.e., normative or even positive.
- **Career decidedness** — an individual's level of career indecision (undecided vs. decided as contrasting anchors).
- **Theory-driven approach** — uses a predetermined factor structure based on decision-making theory; items sample each indecision domain.
- **Data-driven approach** — relies on empirical data as the predominant source for the factor structure.
- **Neuroticism/negative affectivity** — a tendency toward negative affect, including neuroticism and low self-esteem; pervasive pattern of negative emotional experience.
- **Choice/commitment anxiety** — resistance/hesitance to commit to a single choice; anxiety arising in the process of making a final career choice.
- **Need for information** — the difficulty in collecting and processing self- and career-related information (lack of decision-related information).
- **Lack of readiness** — disengagement from the career decision-making process; low career decision-making self-efficacy, lack of planfulness and goal directedness; tendency to disengage / drop out.
- **Interpersonal conflicts** — disagreement with and discouragement from important people; the inhibitive interpersonal dynamic for career decision-making.
- **Developmental indecision vs. chronic indecisiveness** — distinction noted in the EPCD model literature (Germeijs, Verschueren, & Soenens, 2006; Santos, 2001).

## Main Findings & Arguments

- Since Osipow (1999), there has been significant theoretical and empirical progress, but no synthesis until this review.
- **Theory-driven models** are best represented by the CDDQ (rational decision process) and the EPCD (emotional/personality-related factors).
  - CDDQ reliability: the Lack of Readiness subscale consistently shows low internal consistency (α often .50-.70) vs. Lack of Information and Inconsistent Information subscales (α .80-.95). This suggests the three subdomains of Lack of Readiness — lack of motivation, indecisiveness, dysfunctional beliefs — represent distinct aspects; Xu & Tracey (2014) proposed treating them as distinct (a five-factor revision of CDDQ).
  - CDDQ structural validity: the three-factor structure generally holds cross-culturally, though the lack of readiness factor is less stable.
  - CDDQ criterion validity: consistently supported (e.g., r = .82 with CDS total; negative association with career decision-making self-efficacy; differentiates undecided vs. decided high school students). Divergent/convergent validity of subscales is under-researched.
  - EPCD reliability: αs .79-.95 for each of three factors across cultural contexts (Israel, US, China, Korea, Turkey); adequate 2-week test-retest.
  - EPCD structural validity: three-factor structure supported cross-culturally; some self-concept/identity items may not apply in East Asian contexts.
  - EPCD criterion validity: associated with emotional/personality variables, career decision-making self-efficacy, CDS; longitudinally predicts later decidedness and choice confidence.
  - CDDQ and EPCD items show almost complete independence (except one CDDQ Lack of Readiness item relating to EPCD self-concept/identity, "I am usually afraid of failure").
- **Data-driven models** evolved in two stages: Kelly & Lee (2002) factor analysis, and Brown & Rector (2008) meta-analysis.
  - Kelly & Lee (2002): factor-analyzed CDS, CFI, CDDQ in a college sample; found six factors — lack of information, need for information, trait indecision, disagreement with others, identity diffusion, choice anxiety.
  - Nauta (2012): in a more diverse sample only five of the six factors were present.
  - Brown & Rector (2008) meta-analysis: first meta-analytically derived model; four factors — neuroticism/negative affectivity, choice/commitment anxiety, lack of readiness, interpersonal conflicts. Validated in Brown et al. (2012).
- **Cross-cultural variance**: The four-factor CIP model holds in Western cultures (Icelandic, Italian, French, American) but NOT in Eastern cultures (Chinese, Korean) — choice/commitment anxiety and need for information separate into two distinct factors in Eastern contexts. Proposed reasons (Roche et al., 2017): in Chinese/Korean contexts independence in career decision-making is increasingly salient making information needs particularly salient, and high future orientation/performance attainment make information-collecting an integral part of the process rather than only an anxiety symptom.
- The CDDQ, EPCD, and CIP-65 all show sound psychometric performance across Western and Eastern contexts, but the CIP shows notable structural variance between Western and Eastern contexts (CDDQ/EPCD built on universal decision theories; CIP built on context-specific data).
- **Identity diffusion**: not a stand-alone CIP factor; Brown et al. (2012) found identity diffusion loaded on choice/commitment anxiety. Chronic indecisiveness is not unidimensional — it may have two underlying causes: neuroticism/negative affectivity and choice/commitment anxiety.

## Frameworks & Models

**The integrative five-factor model of career indecision** (the central contribution; see Table 1):

1. **Neuroticism/negative affectivity** — general anxiety, low self-esteem, pervasive negativity. Encompasses CIP neuroticism/negative affectivity and EPCD general anxiety + self-esteem. A general (not career-specific) domain.
2. **Choice/commitment anxiety** — difficulty in committing to a single career choice. Encompasses CIP choice/commitment anxiety and EPCD anxiety. Career-specific.
3. **Need for information** — lack of decision-related information. Encompasses CDDQ lack of information.
4. **Lack of readiness** — difficulty in initiating career decision-making or tendency to disengage. Encompasses CIP and CDDQ lack of readiness.
5. **Interpersonal conflicts** — disapproval from important people. Encompasses CIP interpersonal conflicts and CDDQ inconsistent information.

The five factors are named after CIP factors to avoid variable proliferation; the five-factor structure was indicated by convergent findings of Kelly & Lee (2002) and Nauta (2012), and supported in a factor analysis of Xu & Tracey's (2017) published correlation matrix (CDDQ, EPCD, CIP-Short, CDSE). The model differs from the CIP only in that it keeps choice/commitment anxiety and need for information as separate conceptually distinct factors (in the CIP they are empirically unified for Western populations). Rationale: "need for information may be reduced by a broader and deeper career exploration, but choice/commitment anxiety cannot be alleviated simply by collecting more information; rather, ambiguity tolerance may be more important for alleviating choice/commitment anxiety."

The authors recommend establishing a normative model (the integrative five-factor model) plus several context-specific idiothetic models (e.g., the four-factor CIP-Short as a Western cultural variant).

## Measurement

The review catalogs the major indecision measures across three generations and two epistemological approaches:

**Theory-driven measures:**
- **Career Decision Scale (CDS)** — Osipow, Carney, & Barak (1976); Osipow (1987). First-generation; originally a diagnostic tool for differential treatment but often used as a one-dimensional research tool. Generally found to have four underlying factors: diffusion, support, approach-approach conflict, external barriers. Diffusion reflects indecision due to various reasons; support addresses career choice certainty; approach-approach resonates with choice/commitment anxiety; external barriers resonate with interpersonal conflicts.
- **Career Factors Inventory (CFI)** — Chartrand, Robbins, Morrill, & Boggs (1990). Rationally developed on a dichotomous model: need for career information and affective/personality-related impediments. Four underlying factors: need for career information, need for self-knowledge, career choice anxiety, generalized indecisiveness.
- **Career Decision Difficulties Questionnaire (CDDQ)** — Gati, Krausz, & Osipow (1996). Adaptation of decision-making theory; taxonomy with three overarching domains — lack of readiness, lack of information, inconsistent information — further divided into 10 subdomains. Lack of Readiness subscale comprises lack of motivation, indecisiveness, dysfunctional beliefs.
- **Emotional and Personality-related Career Decision-making Difficulties scale (EPCD)** — Saka, Gati, & Kelly (2008). Three overarching factors: pessimistic views, anxiety, self-concept and identity. Focuses on emotional/personality-related factors. Also exists as EPCD-SF (short form).

**Data-driven measures:**
- **Career Decision Profile (CDP)** — Jones (1989). Four underlying factors: self-clarity, knowledge about occupations and training, decisiveness, career choice importance.
- **Career Indecision Profile (CIP)** — Brown et al. (2012). Operationalizes the Brown & Rector (2008) four-factor meta-analytic model (neuroticism/negative affectivity, choice/commitment anxiety, lack of readiness, interpersonal conflicts). Two forms: long 167-item CIP-167, and abbreviated 65-item CIP-65 (Hacker et al., 2013). CIP-65 α = .88-.97 (US), .72-.94 (French), .74-.94 (Italian).
- **CIP-Short** — Xu & Tracey (2017b). Developed with item response theory to increase efficiency; retains items with monotonic level-response relations and minimizes differential item functioning across gender; good internal consistency, structural, convergent/divergent validity. The latest evolution of the data-driven approach.

Other measures referenced: Career Decision-Making Self-Efficacy Scale (CDSE; Betz, Klein, & Taylor, 1996); Strong Interest Inventory (Donnay, 2005); Career Decision Ambiguity Tolerance Scale (Xu & Tracey, 2015b).

Comparison summary: CDDQ and EPCD assess cognitive, emotional, and personality-related indecision in *detail*; the CIP-Short assesses broad domains *comprehensively*. The five-factor model incorporates all factors of the CFI and CDP, and the two CDS factors (approach-approach, external barriers) with direct relevance to antecedents of career decidedness.

## Practical Implications

- **Differential strategies** — different decision-making difficulties require different interventions:
  - High neuroticism/negative affectivity → counselors attend to pervasive emotion/self-concept patterns; interventions to improve self-esteem and anxiety management; this dimension can signal identity foreclosure (clients may have prematurely foreclosed options to avoid deciding).
  - Choice/commitment anxiety → may stem from a maximizing decision strategy; encourage a satisficing strategy and "good-enough" options; may also result from low career decision ambiguity tolerance — help develop career calling or an organizing interest structure.
  - Need for information → information-giving is effective in reducing anxiety; use O*NET OnLine, Strong Interest Inventory; but probe the psychological mechanism, since people may mistake choice/commitment anxiety for need for information and keep collecting information even after they have enough.
  - Lack of readiness → develop self-efficacy beliefs through deliberate learning from positive experience, rationally-focused decision-making skills, goal directedness; address maladaptive perfectionism and avoidance of personal responsibility.
  - Interpersonal conflicts → explore cultural/family/relational background; consider collectivistic vs. individualistic orientations and acculturation; teach communication/negotiation skills with parents; the conflict may reflect a general interpersonal pattern.
- **Systematic assessment** — the CIP-Short can be used both intrapersonally and interpersonally; its standardized distributional information normalizes clients' experience and detects early symptoms; can track intervention progress every session due to its efficiency.
- **Cultural considerations** — Eastern and Western cultures experience indecision differently; for Eastern clients, need for information is a salient concern from the beginning, so provide information-access interventions early; for Eastern clients, collecting information cannot completely alleviate choice/commitment anxiety, so also consider ambiguity tolerance.
- General recommendation: use the CDDQ and EPCD as in-depth measures of cognitive and emotional/personality-related indecision respectively, and the CIP-Short as a comprehensive measure of broad domains (particularly in Western cultures).

## Notable Quotes

- "We define career indecision as a state of being undecided about one's educational, occupational, or career-related path."
- "The three subdomains underlying the Lack of Readiness subscale (i.e., lack of motivation, indecisiveness, and dysfunctional beliefs) might represent distinct aspects of career indecision."
- "Need for information may be reduced by a broader and deeper career exploration, but choice/commitment anxiety cannot be alleviated simply by collecting more information; rather, ambiguity tolerance may be more important for alleviating choice/commitment anxiety."
- "There might be no single indecision model consistently present across populations, whereas an obsessive search for the 'right' model could lead to model proliferation, potentially preventing knowledge accumulation."
- "Contemporary perspectives on indecision have emphasized that obsession with decidedness, rather than indecision per se, could be a problem."
- "Little is known about how to facilitate a positive relationship between decision makers and indecision."
