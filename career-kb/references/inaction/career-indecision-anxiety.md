## Overview

A research article (Journal of Career Assessment, 22(2), 2014, 233–247) by Aaron D. Miller and Patrick J. Rottinghaus (Southern Illinois University at Carbondale). It applies an existential framework — drawn from Yalom (1980), Frankl (1984), and Cohen (2003) — to explain the long-observed but theoretically under-explained positive correlation between career indecision and anxiety. The study introduces meaning in life as the missing theoretical mechanism, testing both a mediation model (presence of meaning mediates the indecision→anxiety link) and a moderation model (search for meaning moderates that link). Sample: 229 students at a large Midwestern university.

## Key Constructs & Definitions

- **Career indecision** — "the difficulty, or inability, to make a career decision" (Hartman, Fuqua, & Blum, 1985); particularly salient for college students in a transitional period when they must crystallize, specify, and implement a career choice. ~50% of college students have difficulty selecting/committing to a career decision (Gianakos, 1999).
- **Anxiety** — "the diffuse apprehension that is notably associated with feelings of uncertainty and helplessness" (May, 1977). Split into **trait anxiety** (general stable susceptibility) and **state anxiety** (manifestation in a given situation; the primary variable of interest here). Consists of affective, cognitive, and somatic symptoms.
- **Zeteophobia** — Krumboltz's (1992) term for the anxiety associated with choosing a career; literally "a fear of searching out," signaling the intimate linkage between career indecision and anxiety.
- **Meaning in life** — "the sense made of, and significance felt regarding, the nature of one's being and existence" (Steger, Frazier, Oishi, & Kaler, 2006). Distinguished into two facets: **presence of meaning** (the extent to which one experiences life as meaningful) and **search for meaning** (the orientation toward, and attempts to find, meaning in one's life).
- **Ultimate concerns** — Yalom's (1980) set of existential givens fundamental to human existence: freedom, death, isolation, and meaninglessness.
- **Meaninglessness** — the ultimate concern that there is no inherent meaning in life and individuals must construct their own; the motivation for human behavior is the search for meaning in an inherently meaningless world (Frankl, 1984; Yalom, 1980).
- **Boundary decision** — "a life-changing event or an urgent experience that brings certain existential concerns into awareness" (Yalom, 1980). Cohen (2003) asserts a career choice is a major life decision and therefore a boundary decision; anxiety invariably co-occurs with boundary decisions.

## Main Findings & Arguments

- **Existential model of career decision making (Cohen, 2003).** Anxiety, meaning in life, and career indecision are "indissoluble components of the process." A career decision, experienced as a boundary decision, confronts individuals with existential givens; anxiety results from this increased awareness of ultimate concerns. People want their careers to provide fulfillment and contribute to meaning in life.
- **Hypothesis 1 supported**: significant positive correlation between career indecision and state anxiety, r(227)=.398, p<.001. College students who experience career indecision are more likely to experience anxiety.
- **Hypothesis 2 supported**: significant negative correlation between career indecision and presence of meaning in life, r(227)=−.337, p<.001. As students experience greater indecision, they perceive less sense of meaning.
- **Hypothesis 3 supported**: significant negative correlation between presence of meaning and state anxiety, r(227)=−.31, p<.001. Students with greater meaning report less anxiety.
- **Hypothesis 4 supported (mediation)**: presence of meaning in life significantly mediates the relationship between career indecision and state anxiety. Bootstrapping (20,000 resamples): indirect effect = .064, 95% CI [.023, .124], SE=.023, p=.007; direct effect after controlling for mediator = .194, p<.001. Standardized path coefficients: indecision→presence of meaning = −.706***; presence of meaning→state anxiety = −.090**; indecision→state anxiety total = .377*** (direct .314*** with mediator). Presence of meaning is "part of the mechanism by which an individual's career indecision affects his or her level of anxiety."
- **Hypothesis 5 NOT supported (moderation)**: the search for meaning did NOT moderate the indecision→anxiety relationship. The interaction term added non-significant variance (ΔR²=.001, F(1,225)=.320, p=.572). The indecision–anxiety relationship does not differ by level of search for meaning.
- **Unexpected finding**: search for meaning was *positively* correlated with anxiety, r(227)=.22, p<.001 — counter to Steger et al. (2006), who found a negative correlation. The authors interpret this as the search for meaning being itself an anxiety-provoking process: searching prompts reassessment of unsatisfactory aspects of one's life, including career goals (also possibly a function of anxiety's curvilinear relationships, Yerkes-Dodson, 1908).
- **No college-year differences**: ANOVAs found no significant differences across freshman/sophomore/junior/senior in state anxiety, presence of meaning, search for meaning, or career indecision.

## Frameworks & Models

**Existential model of career decision making** (applied from Cohen, 2003, building on Yalom, 1980):
- A career choice = a *boundary decision* → brings existential *ultimate concerns* (freedom, death, isolation, meaninglessness) into awareness → produces *anxiety* (existential angst).
- The ultimate concern of *meaninglessness* is central: humans must construct their own meaning; one primary motivation in career decision making is the search for meaningful work (Kierkegaard, 1950; Cohen, 2003).
- Anxiety is present "to the extent that a career decision causes an individual to assess his or her experience of meaning in life."

**Two-part tested model:**
1. *Mediation model*: Career indecision (predictor) → Presence of meaning in life (mediator) → State anxiety (criterion). Three paths (a: indecision→presence; b: presence→anxiety; c: indecision→anxiety). Tested via bootstrapping. — Supported.
2. *Moderation model*: Search for meaning moderates the Career indecision → State anxiety relationship. Tested via hierarchical multiple regression with centered interaction term. — Not supported.

## Measurement

- **Career Decision Scale (CDS; Osipow et al., 1976)**: 19 self-report items, 4-point scale (1 = not at all like me, 4 = exactly like me); 16 items measure indecision, 2 measure certainty, 1 open-ended. Only the 16-item indecision subscale used; higher score = greater indecision. Cronbach's α=.88 in this study.
- **Meaning in Life Questionnaire (MLQ; Steger et al., 2006)**: 10 items, 7-point scale (1 = absolutely untrue, 7 = absolutely true). Two subscales: presence of meaning (MLQ-P, 5 items, α=.89) and search for meaning (MLQ-S, 5 items, α=.86). A strength is its individualized, subjective understanding of meaning.
- **State-Trait Inventory for Cognitive and Somatic Anxiety (STICSA; Ree, MacLeod, French, & Locke, 2000)**: state anxiety scale used — 21 items, 4-point scale (1 = not at all, 4 = very much so); 10 cognitive items (anxious thoughts), 11 somatic items (physiological arousal). State scale α=.91 in this study.
- Descriptives (Table 1): CDS M=1.83, SD=.57; State anxiety M=1.74, SD=1.74; MLQ-P M=5.22, SD=1.20; MLQ-S M=4.94, SD=1.44.
- Data transformations applied to satisfy normality (log base 10 for state anxiety; cubic for both meaning facets); non-transformed variables used in the bootstrap mediation analysis.

## Practical Implications

- Supports an existential model of career counseling and the use of existential/phenomenological perspectives in conceptualization and intervention. The client's existential/phenomenological perspective can provide a focal point for exploration (Maglio et al., 2005).
- Demonstrates the need for interventions and assessment strategies aimed at exploring clients' sense of meaning — supports the push for increased focus on meaning in career counseling.
- Presence of meaning provides "scaffolding for value systems"; counselors can examine work values with clients to understand the meaning they draw from career choices.
- Counselors should attend to meaning even if clients do not raise it spontaneously — counselors may help stimulate client awareness of meaning in life.
- For clients experiencing career-decision difficulty, anxiety likely follows; supports conceptualizing the career decision as a boundary decision in both career and personal-emotional counseling.
- Few formal assessments address meaning in vocational contexts; the field may benefit from measures developed specifically for career counseling.

## Connections

- **Career indecision (core)**: uses the CDS, the classic single-construct operationalization of career decidedness; contrasts with multidimensional models (Brown & Rector, Germeijs & De Boeck, Gordon).
- **Anxiety**: directly establishes and explains the indecision→anxiety link via meaning; complements Bian's (2023) "vicious circle" of anxiety and indecision, and the emotional tensions / "uncertain me" in the Rogiers et al. psychodynamic study.
- **Meaning in life**: introduces meaning as the mediating mechanism — Bian's review cites this paper under "outcomes" (indecisive individuals lack a sense of meaning).
- **Career inaction**: the existential strain of a "boundary decision" and the anxiety of searching parallel the psychological strain and tension-easing strategies of career inaction (Rogiers et al.).
- **Decision theory**: Germeijs & De Boeck's "outcome uncertainty" (future-oriented) resonates with the existential ultimate concerns brought into awareness by a boundary decision.
- **Transitions**: frames the college-to-career transition as an existential boundary event; calls for longitudinal/life-span study (integrating Super's life-span theory with existentialism).
