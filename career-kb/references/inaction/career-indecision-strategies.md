## Overview

This article (Journal of Career Assessment, 2016, Vol. 24[1], 42-66) develops and tests a theoretical model and corresponding measure — the Strategies for Coping with Career Indecision (SCCI). The model identifies 14 categories of coping strategies grouped into three major clusters: Productive coping, Support-seeking, and Nonproductive coping. The model concepts were adopted from general coping theories (Frydenberg & Lewis, 1993; Skinner, Edge, Altman, & Sherwood, 2003) and adapted to the career decision-making context. The authors argue that because effectiveness of coping varies by stressor type, a stress-specific (rather than general) measure of coping is needed for career indecision. The SCCI questionnaire was refined across 10 samples (total N = 3,081). Study 1 develops and tests the questionnaire (N = 460 Israeli young adults); Study 2a runs confirmatory factor analyses on American (N = 386) and Israeli (N = 819) samples; Study 2b tests concurrent validity.

## Key Constructs & Definitions

- **Coping** — "behavioral or cognitive efforts to manage situations that are appraised as stressful" (Lazarus & Folkman, 1984).
- **Strategies for Coping with Career Indecision (SCCI)** — a comprehensive theoretical model and 45-item questionnaire (3 items per category) mapping how young adults cope with the stress of making a career decision.
- **Productive coping** — six categories that facilitate coping with career indecision: (a) *instrumental information-seeking* — active search for information relevant to making the decision; (b) *emotional information-seeking* — active search for information to reduce uncertainty/anxiety or prepare oneself emotionally; (c) *problem-solving* — investment in planning, systematic analysis, comparing outcomes of alternatives; (d) *flexibility* — willingness to be flexible in one's preferences and consider compromises; (e) *accommodation* — finding a positive way of thinking about the challenge of deciding; (f) *self-regulation* — monitoring and controlling feelings/thoughts that impede deciding.
- **Support-seeking** — three categories involving others: (a) *instrumental help-seeking* — seeking others' guidance/advice to obtain instrumental tools for the decision; (b) *emotional help-seeking* — seeking affective support to deal with emotional consequences (anxiety, worry, frustration); (c) *delegation* — asking others to make the decision or shift responsibility to them.
- **Nonproductive coping** — five categories that hinder coping: (a) *escape* — intentional or unconscious avoidance, denial, or wishful thinking; (b) *helplessness* — feelings of being unable to advance the decision (passivity, confusion, pessimism); (c) *isolation* — concealing difficulties from others; (d) *submission* — repetitive focus on adverse features through rumination, rigid perseveration, intrusive thoughts, worry; (e) *opposition* — blaming others for making it harder to decide.
- **Range of Considered Alternatives (RCA)** — self-report measure of how far individuals have narrowed occupational alternatives, reflecting decision status (6-point statement scale from "I do not even have a general direction" to "I am already sure of the occupation I want").

## Main Findings & Arguments

- The 14 coping strategies — 12 adopted from Skinner et al. (2003), with help-seeking and information-seeking each split into instrumental and emotional facets — group into the three hypothesized clusters across both Israeli and American samples.
- Internal consistency: median Cronbach's α of the 14 scales was .83 (range .70-.90); cluster reliabilities were .86 (Productive), .92 (Support-seeking), .88 (Nonproductive) in Study 1.
- Confirmatory factor analysis compared three models: H (42-14-3, the hypothesized model), A1 (42-14-2), and A2 (42-43). The 42-14-3 model fit best in both samples but required modification — adding cross-loadings — for adequate fit. American modified model: χ²(799) = 1736.09, RMSEA = .055, CFI = .91, TLI = .91, SRMR = .10.
- **Cross-loadings discovered in both samples**: *delegation* and *emotional help-seeking* loaded on Nonproductive coping (in addition to Support-seeking); *instrumental help-seeking* loaded on Productive coping. This means Support-seeking was partially productive and partially nonproductive.
- *Flexibility* had a low loading on its second-order factor (.32 Israeli, .25 American), suggesting it may be more tied to career decision-making style than to ways of coping.
- Cluster intercorrelations: Productive and Nonproductive coping were strongly negatively correlated (r = -.54 American, -.13 Israeli at scale level after modification); Support-seeking had low positive correlations with both other clusters.
- Measurement invariance (configural, metric, second-order loadings, covariances) held across American and Israeli samples (ΔCFI ≤ .01 criterion).
- **Concurrent validity (Study 2b)**: Undecided participants used more Nonproductive coping and less Productive coping than partially decided participants. Participants with high perceived decisional difficulty and high decisional distress used more Nonproductive coping and Support-seeking than those with moderate levels.
- **Key asymmetry**: The inhibiting effect of Nonproductive coping (mean d = 0.62 across six comparisons, all significant) was roughly twice as large as the facilitating effect of Productive coping (mean d = 0.25, only three significant).
- **Gender differences**: Women scored higher than men on *emotional help-seeking* (d = .41) and *helplessness* (d = .23).
- **Cross-cultural differences** (7 of 14 categories): Americans scored higher on emotional information-seeking, flexibility, accommodation, isolation; Israelis scored higher on delegation, helplessness, submission. Explained by educational/sociocultural background — Israelis often make first major career decision after mandatory military service; Israeli context is moderately collectivist (interdependence, family assistance) vs. the more individualistic American context.

## Frameworks & Models

**The SCCI model — 3 clusters, 14 categories:**

- **Productive coping**: instrumental information-seeking, emotional information-seeking, problem-solving, flexibility, accommodation, self-regulation.
- **Support-seeking**: instrumental help-seeking, emotional help-seeking, delegation.
- **Nonproductive coping**: escape, helplessness, isolation, submission, opposition.

The three clusters derive from Frydenberg & Lewis (1993), who distinguished functional (direct attempts to deal with the problem) from dysfunctional styles (use of nonproductive strategies). Twelve of the 14 categories derive from Skinner et al. (2003), who reviewed 400+ ways of coping and proposed 12 families of coping; six of these (self-reliance/regulation, support seeking, problem solving, information seeking, accommodation, negotiation) are typically adaptive, the other six (delegation, isolation, helplessness, escape, submission, opposition) typically maladaptive. The authors split information-seeking and help-seeking into instrumental and emotional facets, treating these as functionally distinct rather than equivalent.

## Measurement

- **SCCI questionnaire**: 45 items in the validated 10th version — 3 items per each of the 14 categories (42 substantive items), 1 warm-up item, 2 validity items. Rated on a 9-point Likert scale (1 = does not describe me at all, 9 = describes me very well).
- Construction: started with 142 preliminary items; graduate-student judges classified items into clusters and categories; iteratively refined across 10 samples down to 3 items per category. English version back-translated from Hebrew.
- Example items (from Appendix A): instrumental information-seeking — "I gather relevant and updated information about the occupations or careers I am interested in"; problem-solving — "I weigh the alternatives... in terms of their advantages and disadvantages"; accommodation — "I maintain a positive attitude toward the challenge of making a career decision"; self-regulation — "I am able to manage the concerns that make it harder for me to decide"; delegation — "I ask other people to tell me which major, occupation or career I should choose"; escape — "I find myself trying not to think about the decision I have to make"; helplessness — "I feel helpless whenever I try to deal with the decision-making process"; isolation — "I do not want others to know how hard it is for me to cope with making a decision"; submission — "I think constantly about what will happen if I make a decision that I later regret"; opposition — "I get annoyed by others around me because I feel they are making it harder for me to decide."
- Validity items: "It is important for me to choose an occupation suited to my interests and abilities" / "It would not bother me if I chose an unsuitable occupation."
- Companion measures: Range of Considered Alternatives (RCA, 6-point decision status); Perceived Decisional Difficulty ("How difficult is it for you to make a career decision?", 9-point); Perceived Decisional Distress ("How stressful do you find the need to choose a major or a career?", 9-point).

## Practical Implications

- The SCCI gives career counselors an initial diagnosis of how counselees cope with career decision-making challenges, complementing other measures (CDSE, CDDQ, CDMP).
- Because the inhibiting effect of Nonproductive coping is twice as large as the facilitating effect of Productive coping, counselors should prioritize *discovering and reducing* nonproductive coping (self-blame, helplessness, ruminative thinking) — not only encouraging productive coping.
- Counselors detecting nonproductive coping can initiate discussion about whether to help the counselee regulate emotions or solve specific career problems.
- Support-seeking is neither uniformly adaptive nor maladaptive: dependent help-seeking (delegation) is not adaptive; instrumental help-seeking can support task-focused coping; emotional help-seeking can be adaptive (reassurance returning one to problem-focused coping) or maladaptive (co-rumination).
- The model can help match counseling strategies to clients' specific sources of decision-making problems.

## Connections

- **Indecision**: SCCI presupposes career-decision-making difficulties (CDDQ; Gati et al., 1996) as the stressor; coping is the response layer. Complements taxonomies of indecision sources.
- **Inaction**: Nonproductive coping — escape, helplessness, submission, isolation, opposition — is the behavioral signature of career inaction; these strategies delay or stall the decision process.
- **Adaptability**: Productive coping (flexibility, accommodation, self-regulation) overlaps conceptually with career adaptability resources; flexibility relates to career decision-making style.
- **Shocks/transitions**: The stress-and-coping framing (Lazarus & Folkman) links to how individuals respond to disruptive career events; the Israeli-sample timing (post-military-service decisions) is itself a transition context.
- **Impostorism / decisional distress**: helplessness, isolation, and chronic worry (submission) connect to self-doubt and concealment themes.
- Cross-references the same research group (Gati, Lipshits-Braziler) cited heavily in the Xu (2019) review and shares the CDDQ lineage.
