## Overview

This is the foundational theoretical article (Academy of Management Review, 2020, Vol. 45, No. 2, pp. 376–394; DOI 10.5465/amr.2017.0196) that introduced **career inaction** as a distinct construct in the careers literature. The authors argue that career theory has been one-sided: it focuses almost entirely on how career decisions are *made and enacted/realized*, while empirical evidence shows people frequently *do not* realize their career desires — turnover intentions weakly predict actual turnover (meta-analytic correlations .24–.45, explaining only 10–15% of variance), entrepreneurial intentions weakly predict actually starting a business, and life-regret research shows people most often regret things they did *not* do in their careers.

The article grounds career inaction in the **psychology of doing nothing** (Anderson, 2003) — insights from cognitive, social, and clinical psychology about behaviors involving a lack of sufficient action despite a desire or stimulus to change — and combines this with the career decision/transition literature. It is a purely theoretical/propositional paper (no data); it develops a definition, a three-phase process model, mechanisms, boundary conditions, and nine formal propositions. Epigraph: "The price of inaction is far greater than the cost of making a mistake" (Meister Eckhart).

## Key Constructs & Definitions

- **Career inaction** — "the failure to act sufficiently over some period of time on a desire to make a change in one's career." Has **three key features**: (1) the person desires to make a change in their career; (2) the person recognizes that they can take action to initiate the desired change but does not do so in a sufficient way; (3) this situation persists for some period of time.
- **Career** (adopted from Arthur, Hall & Lawrence, 1989) — "the unfolding sequence of a person's work experiences over time"; captures both the occurrence and the absence of changes and how people experience them.
- **Desire for change** — can vary from vague (wanting a new challenge) to crystallized (wanting to start a specific business); can arise from **push factors** (boredom, abusive supervisor) and **pull factors** (an attractive unsolicited job offer).
- **Awareness phase** — people become aware of their desire to make a change; prefactual thoughts about alternative outcomes are induced.
- **Inaction phase** — the period during which people fail to act, or do not act sufficiently; the "beating heart" of career inaction. People realize they could act more but do not.
- **Recall phase** — people perceive that at least part of the opportunity has passed, look back, and realize the desired change was not achieved due to a lack of sufficient action on their part; counterfactual thoughts arise.
- **Prefactual thoughts** — mental representations, formed in the awareness phase, of what it would be like to realize the desired change ("I would have less stress"; "I could be the artist I always dreamed of being").
- **Process-related counterfactuals** — recall-phase ruminations about how the past career *enactment process* could have been done better ("If only I had talked to my supervisor about my aspirations"); upward in direction; tend to induce **self-blame regret**.
- **Outcome-related counterfactuals** — recall-phase ruminations about *outcomes* one could have had "if only"; emotional response depends on a comparison process with the current situation.
- **Self-blame (process-related) regret** — regret induced because upward process-related counterfactuals complicate justifying one's factual lack of action; a threat to self-esteem and self-image.
- **Inertia-enhancing mechanisms** — implicit, largely unconscious processes that keep people from acting (see Frameworks).
- **Inaction inertia** — having bypassed an initial opportunity to act, people become less likely to act on a subsequent (slightly less attractive) opportunity, because acting on it would be a constant reminder of the missed earlier one.
- **Crystallization** — the degree to which the desired alternative is vague vs. concrete/specified.
- **Perceived magnitude** — how different the current job and the desired alternative are seen to be (skills, habits, location, knowledge). Note: a large change for one person may be small for another.
- **Job embeddedness** — contextual on-the-job and off-the-job forces that keep people from leaving (links with colleagues/community, person-environment fit, perceived sacrifices like a good pension).
- **Wantrepreneurs** — people who talk about starting their own business without ever doing anything to realize it; a vivid everyday example of career inaction.
- **Semi-factual thoughts** — "even if I had…" thoughts that arise when non-realization is attributed to *external* factors outside one's control (a plant closure, never being invited to interview). Unlike the counterfactuals of career inaction, semi-factual thoughts *facilitate* rather than complicate closure — which is why career inaction specifically requires perceived **personal agency** (the sense one could have acted *more*).

Career inaction is distinguished from related concepts: **nonevents** (Schlossberg — an expected event that did not happen, not necessarily career-related or within one's control), **unanswered callings** (Berg et al. — occupations felt drawn to but not pursued; a subtype of inaction, but only occupational), and **alternative selves** (Obodaru — counterfactual self-representations; broader, do not require lack of agency). Career inaction can be seen as a career-related nonevent the person had at least some control over.

## Main Findings & Arguments

**Why career inaction needs study (three reasons):** (1) it is prevalent and significant — evident in weak intention-behavior correlations and in life-regret research (in Terman's gifted-subjects study, not completing college, not attending college, and not pursuing a professional interest ranked 1st, 3rd, and 5th of all regrets); (2) it can explain why traditional stable career paths remain dominant despite boundaryless-career rhetoric — most careers still involve long continuous employment with the same employer; (3) it has significant consequences — burnout, counterproductive work behavior, feeling locked-in.

**Mechanisms that keep people from acting.** Career inaction is stimulated by general human tendencies to **delay decision making** and **avoid taking action**, which are particularly salient under **decision difficulty** and **outcome uncertainty** — both typical of career decisions. Three interdependent **inertia-enhancing mechanisms** operate simultaneously: (1) fear and anxiety elicited by thinking of making the change; (2) the disproportionate influence of short-term efforts and costs over long-term gains (via the "availability heuristic" — near-term costs are more concrete and available); (3) the paralyzing effect of overly high cognitive demands, which triggers avoidance behavior to lower cognitive load. **All three mechanisms are implicit processes people are generally not aware of, so people influenced by them typically have difficulty explaining and justifying their inaction.**

**When career inaction is more or less likely.** Characteristics of the desired change and the social context modulate the mechanisms:
- *Crystallization*: a vague desired alternative → larger option set, more clarifying steps needed, more anxiety, more perceived short-term cost, more cognitively demanding → more inaction (**P1**).
- *Perceived magnitude*: a larger perceived transition feels riskier → more fear/anxiety, more anticipated intermediate steps, higher short-term costs, more cognitively demanding comparison → more inaction (**P2**).
- *Time frame*: a longer or undefined window of opportunity → less time pressure, more "costs with acting now" (forgone future opportunities), more cognitively demanding → more inaction; a short time frame motivates action (**P3**).
- *Job embeddedness*: more embedded → more anxiety/fear about losing what one has, more vivid/concrete short-term losses, more cognitively demanding comparison → more inaction (**P4**).
- *Social norms*: norms favoring staying/stability → deviating feels abnormal, more anticipated resistance, less social support, more cognitively demanding → more inaction (**P5**).

**How career inaction impacts people.** In the recall phase, two types of counterfactuals arise.
- *Process-related counterfactuals* are upward and, combined with felt personal responsibility, induce **self-blame regret** because they make it hard to justify one's factual lack of action (**P6**). However, if people *can* justify their inaction (e.g., a widely accepted reason existed), process-related counterfactuals trigger *less* regret.
- *Outcome-related counterfactuals* trigger emotions via a **comparison process**: when factual outcomes are perceived as comparatively *better* than counterfactual ones, people feel contentment or relief; when *worse*, they feel outcome-related regret (**P7**).
- Over time, positive outcome-related emotions (contentment, relief) can facilitate **closure** for self-blame regret, whereas negative outcome-related regret intensifies and reinforces self-blame regret as time passes (**P8**). Self-blame regret tends to intensify over time because the further removed people are from a missed opportunity, the more convinced they become they "would have done just fine."
- **Inaction inertia** (**P9**): the experience of regret about career inaction hinders people from acting on *new, less attractive* career opportunities but can stimulate them to act on *new, more attractive* ones (which provide a "silver lining" justification for the past inaction).

**Contributions.** The theory (1) sheds light on why people have stable careers — though the authors stress they "do not argue that all stable careers are due to career inaction"; (2) highlights that people's career desires and behaviors are not always consistent, calling for **multifinality** approaches (different paths from one starting point); (3) challenges the uniformly positive view of self-directedness — the societal trend of holding people personally accountable for making "the best possible choice" is more cognitively demanding and may paradoxically *paralyze* people (the "dark side" of self-directedness); (4) gives a prominent role to **time** and calls for longitudinal/temporal research.

## Frameworks & Models

**The three-phase process model (Figure 1: "Career Inaction: A Phenomenon Occurring Through Several Phases").** A timeline:
- **Awareness phase** — *What happens:* people become aware they desire a change. *Why important:* prefactual thoughts about alternative outcomes are induced. Influenced by **decision characteristics** (P1–3: crystallization, perceived magnitude, time frame).
- **Inaction phase** — *What happens:* people realize they can act more on their desire but fail to do so sufficiently. *Why important:* (1) the ease with which alternative actions are imagined is facilitated; (2) a sense of personal responsibility. Influenced by **contextual characteristics** (P4–5: job embeddedness, social norms).
- **Recall phase** — *What happens:* (1) people realize the past career decision process was suboptimal, hindering justification of their lack of sufficient action; (2) prefactual thoughts evolve into counterfactual thoughts compared with factual outcomes. *Why important:* evaluations of the decision process and outcomes induce emotions. Splits into **process-related counterfactuals** and **outcome-related counterfactuals**.
- A **feedback loop (P9)** runs from the recall phase back to the inaction phase — how people feel about not having acted affects the likelihood of subsequent (in)action.

**The two-mechanism roots:** career decisions are characterized by *decision difficulty* (multiple non-comparable options, many intermediate actions) and *outcome uncertainty* (the ideal future is distal, less tangible, hypothetical). These activate the two general human tendencies — to *delay decision making* and to *avoid taking action* — understood through Lewin's (1951) tension-system perspective (psychological/physical forces hold people in a constant state; prefactual thoughts give some energy to pull free, but the three inertia-enhancing mechanisms strengthen the inertial forces).

## Measurement

This is a theoretical paper and develops no scale (the CARINAS scale was built later by D'Huyvetter et al., 2025 — see `career-inaction-scale.md`). It does, however, *operationalize* the key constructs conceptually:
- **Career inaction** is operationalized through its three features (desire for change + recognized opportunity to act + insufficient action over time). The authors note it is "difficult to judge from an outsider's perspective" — what matters is **how people appraise their own situation** (whether they recognize they had personal agency and could have acted more).
- **Methodological recommendations** for future empirical work: a mixed-method approach; multiple-wave survey studies (assessing desires, intentions, actions, situation, and emotions at multiple time points, time spans of one to three years) that categorize individuals by (1) desire to change at one point and (2) degree of acting/realizing at a later point; longitudinal qualitative case studies over the whole career; retrospective surveys with people at retirement age; experimental/vignette studies manipulating decision and contextual conditions (e.g., vignettes with vs. without a widely accepted reason to not act, or with factual outcomes comparing more/less favorably to counterfactual ones).

## Practical Implications

**For career counselors / practitioners:** help people become aware of, and *weaken*, the inertia-enhancing mechanisms keeping them from acting on their desire to change. Specifically:
- Help people deal with the **anxiety and fear** elicited by the thought of changing.
- Help **reevaluate the possible gains versus losses** associated with the desired change.
- **Break down the career choice and enactment process into small steps** to lower the cognitive demands.
- Help people deal with the negative impact of *past* career inaction — e.g., help them understand and justify why they did not act sufficiently, or help them reappraise their current situation by emphasizing other features in the comparison process.

**For organizations / HR managers and leaders:** be aware of the phenomenon. The counterfactual thoughts induced by career inaction can produce regret, affecting attitudes, well-being, and organizational outcomes (e.g., lowered performance). Pay attention to the underlying reasons for stable career paths — examine whether stability is due to career inaction and whether employees should be helped/encouraged to enact their career desires. This highlights the importance of **organizational career management**: practices aimed at discovering career desires (personal development plans, career conversations with supervisors) and practices aimed at helping people act on their goals (introducing them to relevant people in the organization or sector).

## Connections

- **Career inaction scale (CARINAS)** — this paper is the direct theoretical parent of `career-inaction-scale.md`; D'Huyvetter et al. (2025) operationalize the *inaction phase* of this model into an 8-item scale and empirically test several of these nine propositions.
- **Career transitions** — career inaction is positioned as the *failure to enact* a desired transition; complements transition/decision-enactment models (Nicholson, 1984; Schlossberg, 1981).
- **Career indecision** — distinguished: indecision precedes the career decision; inaction follows it (inaction is broader and can include indecision).
- **Career shocks** — push/pull factors that trigger the desire for change; the AMA deck and later scale work flag shocks (layoffs, missed promotions) as antecedents that can either trigger action or deepen inaction.
- **Career success (subjective & objective)** — career inaction is theorized to lower career satisfaction and subjective success; the comparison process in the recall phase mirrors subjective-success appraisal.
- **Adaptability / self-directedness** — the paper explicitly critiques the "dark side" of self-directedness; career inaction is conceptually the low-agency opposite of career adaptability.
- **Regret & counterfactual thinking** — the recall-phase machinery (prefactual → counterfactual thoughts, process- vs. outcome-related regret, inaction inertia) is shared theoretical ground with the regret literature (Connolly & Zeelenberg; Gilovich & Medvec).
- **Impostorism / well-being** — self-blame regret is framed as a threat to self-esteem and self-image, linking to impostorism and well-being themes.
- This is the most-cited source in the AMA deck and the scale paper — the spine of the entire `inaction` cluster.
