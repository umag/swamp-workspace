## Overview

A research article (Journal of Vocational Behavior, 62, 2003, 11–25) that grounds the atheoretical construct of "career indecision" in normative decision theory. The authors argue that prior research on career indecision lacked consistency and theoretical foundation, and propose that three distinct sources of indecision can be derived from the expected utility model and from Pitz & Harren's (1980) decomposition of decision problems. The study had three purposes: (1) establish empirical differentiation between the three theoretical sources via factor analysis; (2) test which sources actually predict career indecision; (3) test whether the three sources mediate the relation between general indecisiveness and career indecision. Participants were 174 Belgian sixth-year (final-year) high school students choosing further studies, surveyed in May.

## Key Constructs & Definitions

- **Career indecision** — problems experienced during the career decision-making process; here specified as the indecision of high school students regarding the choice of further studies. A "developmental" state tied to a particular decision.
- **General indecisiveness** — a broad personality characteristic of being undecided across a wide range of decisions; present before the decision process starts and seen as a trait, contrasted with situation-specific career indecision.
- **Lack of information (Source 1)** — not having a view on possible alternatives, not knowing the attributes/characteristics of alternatives, not having enough information to evaluate attributes against objectives. Related to steps 3–4 of the expected utility model and to elements 1 and 4 of Pitz & Harren's decomposition. Conceptualized as a problem related to the *present*.
- **Valuation problems (Source 2)** — a category comprising three sub-problems: **value unclarity** (goals are unclear; the person doesn't know what they want), **value conflict** (separate goals can be realized in different alternatives with no alternative combining them all), and **evaluative evenness** (the most preferred alternatives are equally attractive). Related to steps 1–2 (and 5–6) of the expected utility model. Conceptualized as a problem related to the *present*.
- **Uncertainty about the outcomes (Source 3)** — uncertainty arising from unpredictable events or limitations on one's capability to bring an alternative to a good end. Related to step 7 of the expected utility model (estimating probability of success/entry). Conceptualized as a problem related to the *future*. Not previously distinguished in the literature as a separate factor.

## Main Findings & Arguments

- **Theoretical derivation.** The normative expected utility model (Gati, 1986) solves a decision problem in seven steps: (1) identify desirable goals, (2) weigh goals by importance, (3) identify which alternatives exist, (4) rate each alternative on satisfaction of each value, (5) multiply rating by value's weight, (6) sum products of importance and opportunity per alternative, (7) modulate by estimated probability of success/entry. The optimal choice maximizes expected utility. Pitz & Harren (1980) decompose a decision problem into four basic elements: *alternatives*, *objectives/goals*, *outcomes*, and *attributes* of outcomes.
- **Three-factor differentiation confirmed.** A 17-item scale on sources of indecision (8 specific sources collapsed into 8 sum scores) was subjected to confirmatory factor analysis (LISREL 8). The three-factor model fit well (χ²=13.36, df=17, p=.711, RMSEA=.000) and was clearly superior to a one-factor model (χ²=48.82, df=20, p=.0003, RMSEA=.095). The eight specific sources loaded cleanly onto the three intended factors.
- **High inter-factor correlations.** Information–valuation r=.72; valuation–outcomes r=.76; information–outcomes r=.71.
- **Only valuation and outcomes predict career indecision.** In a model with career indecision as a latent variable, the information factor had no significant effect (t(159)=.50, ns), whereas the valuation factor (t(159)=2.69, p<.01) and outcomes factor (t(159)=2.28, p<.05) did. This is attributed to the study capturing a *later* stage of the decision process; consistent with De Boeck (1999), information matters in early stages but not late stages.
- **Mediation of general indecisiveness.** General indecisiveness has a significant total effect on career indecision (t(166)=4.91, p<.01). When valuation and outcomes factors are entered as intermediate variables (Model 6), the direct path from general indecisiveness to career indecision becomes non-significant (t(157)=−.22, ns) and is dropped for parsimony. General indecisiveness contributes to all three factors (valuation t=3.35; outcomes t=4.40; information t=2.60), but its effect on career indecision operates *through* valuation and outcomes only.
- **Career indecision scale is unidimensional.** A 22-item career indecision scale showed a dominant first factor (53.99% variance, eigenvalues 11.88/1.66/1.02), Cronbach's α=.96.

## Frameworks & Models

**Three sources of career indecision (derived from decision theory):**
1. Information factor — how well informed students feel about the alternatives. Specific sources: knowing which alternatives exist; knowing characteristics of alternatives; ability to link characteristics to objectives.
2. Valuation factor — valuation problems regarding objectives and the value of outcomes. Specific sources: value unclarity; value conflict; evaluative evenness.
3. Outcomes factor — uncertainty about the outcomes/future. Specific sources: having no idea what the outcome of choosing an alternative will be; not knowing whether one is prepared enough to succeed.

**Final structural model (Fig. 1):** General indecisiveness → (Information, Valuation, Outcomes) → Career indecision, with Information having no path to career indecision. Standardized path coefficients: indecisiveness→valuation .36, indecisiveness→outcomes .49, indecisiveness→information .24; valuation→career indecision .55, outcomes→career indecision .40.

**Relation to Gati et al. (1996) taxonomy:** "Lack of information about occupations" and "lack of information about self" map onto this study's sources; present/future distinction in Gati's taxonomy resembles the information vs. outcomes distinction.

## Measurement

- **General Indecisiveness Scale** (Germeijs & De Boeck, 2002): 22-item Likert scale (0 strongly disagree – 6 strongly agree), based on 11 features of decision difficulties, each with one positive and one negative item. One-factor solution; Cronbach's α=.91. Eleven descriptors of difficulties in decision-making: (1) deciding takes a long time, (2) finding it difficult to make a decision, (3) not knowing how to make a decision, (4) feeling uncertain during the process, (5) delaying a decision, (6) avoiding a decision, (7) leaving a decision to someone else, (8) instability of a decision, (9) worrying about a decision made, (10) regretting a decision made, (11) calling oneself indecisive when confronted with decision situations.
- **Career Indecision Scale**: 22 items parallel to the indecisiveness scale but referring to "this decision" (choice of further studies); α=.96.
- **Sources of career indecision scale**: 17 items across 8 specific sources (7 positive/negative pairs + 1 triad), 7-point agreement scale. Items collapsed into 8 sum scores for CFA. Eight specific sources: knowing which alternatives exist; knowing characteristics of alternatives; ability to link characteristics and objectives; value unclarity; value conflict; evaluative evenness; having no idea what the outcome will be; not knowing whether enough prepared. Sample items: "I don't have an overview of the different alternatives yet"; "I don't know what I want to achieve by making this decision"; "I have the feeling that the goals I want to achieve don't match"; "I have the feeling that one alternative towers above all the others"; "I don't know what the end of choosing an alternative will be"; "I am uncertain about my chance of success next year."
- **Standardized factor loadings** (Table 4): *information factor* — knowing alternatives exist .71, knowing characteristics .88, no link characteristics→objectives .78; *valuation factor* — value unclarity .78, value conflict .65, evaluative evenness .51; *outcomes factor* — not knowing outcome .74, not knowing whether enough prepared .63.

## Practical Implications

- A complex career indecision problem can be decomposed into three components, two of which (valuation problems, uncertainty about outcomes) have important effects in the later stages of the decision process. This decomposition allows more specific diagnostics and targeted career counseling.
- Counseling focus should be specified depending on which source plays a role for a given student; generic indecision counseling is less effective.
- For students late in the decision process, counseling should target valuation problems (clarifying values, resolving value conflict, breaking evaluative ties) and outcome uncertainty (assessing preparedness, probability of success), not information provision.
- Information interventions may matter more for students early in the decision process (hypothesized, not directly tested due to single time point).

- **Anxiety**: outcome uncertainty about probability of success connects to anxiety/zeteophobia in Miller & Rottinghaus.
